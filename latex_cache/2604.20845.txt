Unlike many recommendation scenarios with small or static item sets, POI recommendation typically operates under large candidate spaces and strong spatiotemporal constraints, where fine-grained ranking quality directly impacts downstream decision-making systems such as route planning, urban mobility optimization, and location-based service platforms. In practice, even small ranking errors among geographically or semantically similar POIs can lead to substantially different user experiences and system-level outcomes. A large body of prior work has explored deep learning approaches for POI recommendation. Sequential models based on RNNs and Transformers model temporal dependencies in check-in sequences~, graph-based methods capture spatial relations among POIs~, and contrastive learning techniques are introduced to alleviate data sparsity~. More recently, Large Language Models have been explored for POI prediction due to their semantic reasoning capabilities~. Despite their architectural differences, most existing POI rankers share a common design principle: they encode a user’s historical trajectory once into a single representation and reuse it to score all candidate POIs. This design choice is often adopted implicitly for efficiency and simplicity, and is rarely treated as an explicit modeling assumption. Formally, given a user trajectory $_u$, these candidate-agnostic approaches first compute a fixed representation $_u = $ and then evaluate each candidate $c$ via a scoring function $s =f $. This formulation implicitly assumes that the relevance of each historical visit is independent of which candidate is being evaluated. While this assumption simplifies modeling and computation, it is rarely questioned and has become a default choice in ranking-based POI recommendation, even as models grow increasingly complex in other aspects such as architecture, supervision, or auxiliary signals. We argue that this fundamentally mismatches how human mobility decisions are made. The relevance of past behaviors is inherently candidate-relative and spatiotemporally contextual. For example, a user may visit coffee shops in the morning, restaurants at midday, and gyms in the evening. When evaluating a coffee shop candidate, recent morning visits at nearby locations should dominate the decision, whereas for a gym candidate, evening exercise-related visits become more informative while morning coffee visits are largely irrelevant. Compressing the entire trajectory into a single vector forces the model to conflate these heterogeneous signals, which becomes particularly problematic under large candidate pools or sparse interaction histories. Importantly, this issue is not a corner case but a systematic limitation of candidate-agnostic ranking formulations. This mismatch reflects a broader issue in ranking systems: when multiple intents coexist in historical behavior, candidate-agnostic encoding inevitably entangles heterogeneous signals, making fine-grained discrimination increasingly difficult as the candidate space grows. To address this limitation, we propose . CaST-POI reformulates POI ranking as a problem: instead of producing a single user representation, each candidate POI dynamically queries and reinterprets the same historical trajectory to construct a candidate-specific representation. We instantiate this idea with an efficient cross-attention mechanism that reuses history-derived keys and values across candidates, and further incorporate candidate-relative temporal and spatial biases to capture fine-grained spatiotemporal relevance. This formulation preserves the efficiency of neural ranking pipelines while explicitly aligning the modeling process with the candidate-dependent nature of human mobility decisions. We emphasize that CaST-POI does not introduce attention as a novel operator. Instead, our contribution lies in reformulating the ranking problem itself: historical trajectories should be interpreted conditioned on each candidate, rather than compressed into a single candidate-agnostic representation. Our main are summarized as follows: We identify the candidate-agnostic encoding assumption as a fundamental limitation in ranking-based POI recommendation and advocate a candidate-conditioned perspective in which the relevance of historical behaviors depends on the evaluated candidate. We propose CaST-POI, a candidate-conditioned spatiotemporal ranking framework that enables each candidate POI to dynamically read and reinterpret the same user trajectory while remaining computationally efficient for large candidate sets. We conduct extensive experiments on multiple real-world benchmarks to demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of CaST-POI, with particularly strong improvements under large candidate pools and sparse mobility scenarios. Next POI recommendation has evolved from early collaborative filtering methods to sophisticated deep sequential models. Recurrent approaches such as GRU4Rec~ demonstrated the effectiveness of modeling user behavior as sequences, while STRNN~ and DeepMove~ further incorporated spatial and temporal signals into recurrent architectures. These methods marked early attempts to capture the spatiotemporal nature of human mobility within neural sequence models. The introduction of Transformer-based architectures further advanced sequential recommendation. SASRec~ and BERT4Rec~ leveraged self-attention to model long-range dependencies and complex sequential patterns. Subsequent POI-specific models extended these ideas by integrating spatial structures and mobility dynamics, such as STGN~, STP-UDGAT~, GETNext~, and MTNet~. Despite their architectural diversity, these methods share a common design principle: user trajectories are encoded into a single fixed representation any candidate POI is considered. As a result, the relevance of historical visits is implicitly assumed to be invariant across candidates, which limits the model’s ability to resolve competing spatiotemporal preference signals during ranking. Modeling spatiotemporal context is central to POI recommendation. Existing approaches typically encode temporal information through absolute features or relative features , and encode spatial information through distances, graphs, or learned spatial representations. Representative methods include STAN~, TiCoSeRec~, Graph-Flashback~, POIGDE~, and diffusion-based models such as DiffuRec~. While these approaches significantly enrich spatiotemporal representations, they model spatial and temporal relevance independently of the candidate being evaluated. In other words, spatiotemporal features are encoded at the trajectory level, rather than as candidate-relative quantities. This design implicitly assumes that the same spatial or temporal signal carries equal importance for all candidate POIs, an assumption that conflicts with the inherently candidate-dependent nature of human mobility decisions. CaST-POI departs from this paradigm by explicitly modeling spatiotemporal relevance as a function of both history and candidate. Large-scale industrial recommender systems typically adopt a two-stage architecture, where a retrieval model generates a candidate set and a ranking model performs fine-grained scoring~. This paradigm motivates increasing interest in candidate-aware ranking models, as ranking decisions are inherently conditioned on the candidate pool. Recent studies have explored incorporating candidate information into ranking and pre-ranking stages~. In the POI domain, CoMaPOI~ represents a notable attempt to introduce candidate-awareness by employing LLM-based agents to dynamically reason over and constrain the candidate space. While effective, CoMaPOI achieves candidate-awareness through explicit symbolic reasoning at the pipeline level, leading to substantial inference overhead and without producing a reusable, candidate-conditioned user representation. In contrast, CaST-POI addresses candidate-awareness at the representation learning level, enabling efficient candidate-conditioned reading of user history within standard neural ranking frameworks. Relative position representations have been widely adopted in sequence modeling. Shaw et al.~ demonstrated that encoding relative distances between tokens improves generalization in self-attention models. Subsequent work has extended relative bias mechanisms to various domains, including temporal and structural modeling in Transformers~. CaST-POI extends this line of work to the spatiotemporal recommendation setting with a key distinction: rather than modeling relative relationships solely within the input sequence, we model candidate-relative temporal and spatial biases between historical visits and external candidate POIs. This design allows the same historical visit to receive different attention weights depending on the evaluated candidate, a capability fundamentally absent in candidate-agnostic architectures. The improvements are particularly pronounced on NYC and TKY, where many geographically proximate POIs compete within the candidate set. Sequential models such as SASRec and BERT4Rec exhibit substantially lower performance, as they encode user history into a single candidate-agnostic representation and lack mechanisms to model candidate-relative spatial relevance. Graph-based and spatiotemporal models, including GETNext and MTNet, capture richer global mobility structures, but still rely on candidate-agnostic user representations during ranking, which limits their ability to distinguish among highly similar POIs. We analyze the sensitivity of CaST-POI to key hyperparameters on the NYC dataset. Performance improves as the number of attention heads increases from 2 to 8, indicating that multiple heads help capture diverse candidate--history relationships. Further increasing the number of heads yields diminishing returns, suggesting that excessive partitioning of attention capacity provides limited additional benefit. A similar trade-off is observed for the embedding dimension. An embedding size of 64 provides a favorable balance between expressiveness and efficiency, while smaller dimensions underfit the data and larger dimensions offer only marginal gains at increased computational cost. The granularity of temporal and spatial bias bucketization also affects performance: too few buckets fail to capture important distinctions, whereas overly fine-grained bucketization can lead to sparse observations and reduced generalization. These trends indicate that CaST-POI remains stable across a reasonable range of hyperparameter settings. To qualitatively illustrate how candidate-conditioned attention operates in practice, we analyze a representative user from the NYC dataset. This user exhibits a diverse daily mobility pattern, including morning coffee shops, lunch restaurants, afternoon workplaces, and evening entertainment venues, making the trajectory well suited for examining candidate-dependent preference shifts. When evaluating a Japanese restaurant candidate for dinner recommendation, CaST-POI assigns the highest attention weights to the user’s previous visits to Japanese restaurants, even though these visits occurred several weeks earlier. In contrast, a recent coffee shop visit from the same day receives negligible attention. When the same user history is queried by a coffee shop candidate for a morning recommendation, the attention distribution shifts accordingly: morning coffee visits receive the largest attention weights, while dinner-related restaurant visits are largely ignored. These contrasting attention patterns arise without modifying the user trajectory, indicating that the relevance of historical visits is conditioned on the evaluated candidate rather than temporal proximity alone. This case study demonstrates that CaST-POI does not rely on a static notion of user preference. Instead, it performs a candidate-conditioned reading of user history, where relevance is jointly determined by candidate-specific intent, temporal context, and spatial proximity.
