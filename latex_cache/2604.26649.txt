Models such as DeepSeek-R1~, OpenAI o1~, and QwQ~ generate extended chains of thought spanning 12,000--25,000 tokens, exhibiting emergent behaviors including self-verification, backtracking, and strategy switching. Unlike standard chain-of-thought prompting~, these models are trained via reinforcement learning to develop genuine multi-step reasoning capabilities that cannot be replicated through prompting alone. However, a critical limitation persists: reasoning models excel at but struggle when is required. Recent analyses reveal that reasoning models show factual accuracy compared to their non-reasoning counterparts on fact-seeking tasks~, with increased hallucination rates on benchmarks like SimpleQA. This creates a fundamental opportunity for retrieval augmentation; yet existing RAG systems are fundamentally misaligned with reasoning model architectures. The core mismatch is temporal: current RAG pipelines optimize for providing relevant context before generation begins~. This ``retrieve-then-generate'' paradigm assumes a single retrieval step suffices. For reasoning models generating thousands of tokens across multiple logical steps, this assumption fails catastrophically. A reasoning chain may begin with sufficient context but encounter knowledge gaps mid-inference, gaps that cannot be anticipated at query time. Iterative retrieval methods such as IRCoT~, FLARE~, and Self-RAG~ address this by interleaving retrieval with generation. Yet these methods were designed for standard language models, not reasoning architectures, and exhibit fundamental incompatibilities: : Existing methods trigger retrieval at token level or sentence level , while reasoning models operate at reasoning step granularity . : Methods relying on token probabilities or internal attention states cannot operate with completion-only models like o1. : Fixed-interval retrieval becomes prohibitively expensive for reasoning chains spanning thousands of tokens, adding 2--5 seconds per retrieval call. We propose , a reasoning-aware retrieval framework that addresses these challenges through principled integration of retrieval into reasoning model inference. Our approach makes three key contributions: We propose a novel uncertainty quantification method operating at reasoning-step granularity. Unlike token-level entropy or sentence-level confidence, our Reasoning Step Uncertainty Score identifies when a logical inference requires external knowledge versus when reasoning should continue with existing context. We formalize reasoning-retrieval integration as a sequential decision problem and learn an intervention policy $ $ that decides whether to retrieve, what query to formulate, and how to integrate retrieved content, all conditioned on the current reasoning state. We develop implicit compression and speculative caching mechanisms that reduce per-retrieval overhead by 3.2$ $ compared to naive integration , enabling practical deployment of reasoning-retrieval systems with 1.33$ $ lower end-to-end latency than fixed-interval approaches. We employ a lightweight classifier trained on human-annotated reasoning traces to identify step boundaries. The classifier operates on a sliding window of tokens, predicting boundary probability based on: Discourse markers ; Logical connectives indicating inference completion; Topic shifts detected via embedding similarity; Punctuation and formatting patterns. For open-weight models , we additionally use the model's own reasoning structure markers when available. The step boundary classifier is a 3-layer transformer encoder operating on 128-token sliding windows with 64-token stride. Training data consists of 2,847 human-annotated reasoning traces from DeepSeek-R1 outputs on a held-out subset of NaturalQuestions . The three annotators were given written instructions to mark a boundary wherever the model completes one logical inference and shifts to a new sub-claim, sub-question, or strategy; introduces a discourse marker signalling such a shift; or closes a verification or backtracking episode. They were instructed to mark boundaries at every sentence, and to prefer fewer, semantically coherent segments over finer ones.
