This question is important because retrieval is the first stage in a sponsored search pipeline: it determines which ads a user sees, and everything downstream---ranking, bidding, revenue---depends on its quality~. On platforms like Walmart.com, even small improvements in retrieval relevance translate to substantial business outcomes. Consider a concrete example. Our work sits at the intersection of four research areas: neural retrieval architectures, training data generation, LLM-based annotation, and curriculum learning. The two-tower architecture was introduced by Huang et al.~ as DSSM and later adopted for YouTube recommendations~. It has since become the dominant paradigm for large-scale retrieval~. In e-commerce, Nigam et al.~ built semantic product search at Amazon, Huang et al.~ deployed embedding-based retrieval at Facebook, and Fan et al.~ proposed MOBIUS for Baidu's sponsored search. At Walmart, Magnani et al.~ introduced semantic retrieval for product search, Wang et al.~ proposed progressive fusion training for ads retrieval, and Desai et al.~ created a unified supervision framework using relevance and engagement signals. Our work does not propose a new model architecture. Instead, we address the upstream problem of training data quality, which is orthogonal to these architectural advances. Recent work has produced strong general-purpose embedding models such as E5~, BGE~, and GTE~, which achieve impressive zero-shot performance on the BEIR benchmark~. Learned sparse approaches like SPLADE~ offer a complementary efficiency profile. These models are strong off-the-shelf baselines, but our production setting imposes constraints they do not address out of the box: sponsored-ad catalog matching, strict serving latency requirements with ANN indexing, and weekly refresh on a shifting query distribution. In retrieval, Hofst{\"a}tter et al.~ proposed cross-architecture distillation for efficient ranking, Qu et al.~ introduced RocketQA with cross-encoder supervision, and Reimers and Gurevych~ distilled monolingual embeddings into multilingual models. All of these methods require the teacher during student training. Our approach is different: it is better described as distant supervision~. This is conceptually similar to weakly-supervised pre-training~ and LLM-generated training data~, but differs in its source---we mine real production query-item pairs rather than generating synthetic queries---and in its scale and production integration. These channels exhibit low pairwise overlap---dictionary-ANN overlap is 13.4\ The key idea of our annotation approach is simple: use a cascade of progressively larger fine-tuned classifiers to label query-item pairs with 5-class graded relevance, and calibrate each model so that confident predictions are accepted early while ambiguous pairs are deferred to larger models. The cascade produces hard labels, not soft probabilities. This is distant supervision~, not classical knowledge distillation~: the cascade is used once, offline, and the student is trained with standard supervised objectives. We make this choice deliberately because it decouples the annotator from the student: annotations are computed in batch and cached; the cascade adds no serving latency; and cascade and student can be upgraded independently. The cost is that we discard the cascade's uncertainty on borderline pairs; Section~ discusses this trade-off. The annotation engine is a calibrated model cascade that routes query-item pairs through three progressively larger domain-specific fine-tuned classifiers~: a cross-encoder , a LoRA-adapted 2B-parameter LLM, and a LoRA-adapted 8B-parameter LLM. Each model produces a five-class relevance prediction with a calibrated confidence score. If the confidence exceeds a per-model threshold, the prediction is accepted; otherwise, the pair is deferred to the next, larger model. Pairs that remain unresolved after all three models are decided by majority vote with a max-prediction tiebreaker. A critical design choice is calibrating each predicted class separately via isotonic regression, rather than applying a single global calibration. This captures heterogeneous confidence distributions across classes---models tend to be overconfident on extreme classes and underconfident on middle classes . Per-class calibration achieves the best cascade routing accuracy compared to temperature scaling, Platt scaling, and global isotonic methods. We audit the cascade against trained human annotators on a held-out set of 100K query-item pairs disjoint from all training data. We believe three factors contribute. First, the cascade provides relevance signals that do not depend on click position or query frequency. Unlike click-trained models, which receive no signal for tail queries, the cascade judges every query-item pair on text semantics alone. The cascade has its own biases---inherited from its fine-tuning data---but these biases are systematic and predictable, unlike the heterogeneous noise in click signals. Second, the multi-channel mining pipeline creates training examples at five difficulty levels, so the model sees a diverse range of failure modes: missed relevant items, retrieved irrelevant items, and lexically similar distractors. This decouples the cascade from the student entirely: annotations are computed once, cached, and reused across training runs and model architectures. However, it discards the cascade's uncertainty on borderline pairs , where collapsing the distribution to a single class may propagate systematic biases. Quantifying this gap through a controlled comparison of hard-label training against soft-label distillation from the same cascade is the most informative follow-up to this work. This suggests that the current title-only input could benefit from structured product attributes such as brand, category, and description. The token-similar negative mining may also be less effective for categories where genuinely different products share most of their vocabulary. We note the following limitations of our work. : our training and evaluation data are proprietary to Walmart and cannot be publicly released, though the methodology is described in sufficient detail to be reproduced on other corpora. : the 89.1\ The system runs in production with a weekly retraining cadence. The fully automated pipeline---from multi-channel data extraction through cascade annotation, curriculum training, and ONNX export---eliminates manual annotation and enables continuous model improvement. Serving uses GPU-accelerated inference with approximate nearest neighbor search, meeting production latency requirements. The total retraining cycle completes within 24--48 hours on GPU infrastructure. A subtlety of weekly retraining is the feedback loop: the ANN channel is the current production model, so the hard positives shift each week as the ANN improves. In practice, we observe that this loop is stable---each iteration's hard-positive set shrinks as the ANN closes gaps with the lexical systems. However, we have not formally characterized the convergence properties of this loop, and pathological drift remains a theoretical risk.
