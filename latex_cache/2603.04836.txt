 {acmart} {} {leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt, topsep=2pt} {algorithm2e} {RGB}{0,128,0} } } } Modern e-commerce search is inherently multimodal: customers make purchase decisions by jointly considering product text and visual informations. However, most industrial retrieval and ranking systems primarily rely on textual information, underutilizing the rich visual signals available in product images. In this work, we study unified text-image fusion for two-tower retrieval models in the e-commerce domain. We demonstrate that domain-specific fine-tuning and two stage alignment between query with product text and image modalities are both crucial for effective multimodal retrieval. Building on these insights, we propose a noval modality fusion network to fuse image and text information and capture cross-modal complementary information. Experiments on large-scale e-commerce datasets validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Large-scale e-commerce retrieval systems are typically optimized around textual relevance, encoding user queries and product descriptions into a shared embedding space to enable efficient nearest-neighbor search at scale. While this design is computationally attractive and widely adopted in industry~, it fails to reflect the inherently multimodal nature of user decision-making in online shopping. In practice, as illustrated in Figure~, users often rely on visual information—such as appearance, style, color, and fine-grained details conveyed by product images—to assess relevance and make interaction decisions, especially when textual descriptions are sparse, ambiguous, or insufficient. This mismatch between the multimodal evidence users consider and the unimodal signals retrieval models are trained on leads to suboptimal relevance modeling, causing text-only retrieval methods to struggle in visually driven categories where subtle visual distinctions dominate relevance judgments. These limitations inspire the development of effective multimodal retrieval approaches that incorporate image information into representation learning, which has been shown to be beneficial for product retrieval and ranking~. {figs/search_example.png} Despite substantial progress in multimodal representation learning, applying multimodal techniques to e-commerce retrieval poses domain-specific challenges. VL-CLIP ~ points out weak object-level alignment and ambiguous textual representations in product data; EI-CLIP ~ identifies domain-specific entities as confounders and introduces causal interventions; and FashionKLIP ~ leverages external knowledge graphs to address the lack of fine-grained cross-modal conceptual knowledge. While these approaches improve multimodal understanding, many introduce additional architectural complexity or computational overhead that makes large-scale nearest-neighbor retrieval challenging ~. This motivates us to build an efficient multimodal retriever that maintains scalable two-tower search, supports large-scale indexing, and can be deployed even in CPU-based production infrastructure. In this work, we directly address the mismatch between user decision signals and model learning signals in e-commerce retrieval by studying unified text–image fusion for two-tower retrieval models. To effectively transfer general multi-modal models to the e-commerce domain,, we first show that domain-specific fine-tuning is essential for aligning multi-modal representations with commercial relevance. Building on this foundation, we explicitly align user queries with both product text and product images, enabling the model to learn from the same visual cues that users rely on during relevance judgment. Under this aligned setting, we conduct systematic ablation studies on fusion strategies and propose a light-weight yet effective mixture-of-modality-experts fusion architecture, where each text and image encoder act as an expert, combined with bilinear interaction to model fine-grained cross-modal relationships. Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in retrieval effectiveness and downstream ranking performance. This work makes the following contributions to large-scale e-commerce retrieval: We systematically analyze the role of product image information in retrieval, showing that visual signals play an important role in improving retrieval effectiveness, especially for visually driven product categories. We propose a mixture-of-modality-experts fusion architecture with an interaction network that enables effective integration of textual and visual representations for multimodal retrieval. Given a batch of $N$ query--product pairs, we compute the cosine similarity matrix between query and product embeddings, where diagonal entries correspond to positives and off-diagonal entries provide candidate negatives. Instead of sampling negatives uniformly at random, we adopt similarity-weighted negative sampling. For each query, we use the cosine similarity scores as sampling weights and select the top-$K$ most similar products from the off-diagonal entries, with $K = 3$ in our experiments. This self-adversarial strategy exposes the model to highly confusable yet irrelevant items, improving discriminative representation learning and accelerating convergence compared to random sampling~. We adopt a curriculum-style multi-stage training strategy to progressively align model representations with user decision signals in e-commerce search~. Instead of directly optimizing query--item relevance in a fully multimodal space, we decompose the learning process into three stages with increasing supervision complexity, allowing the model to gradually acquire modality-aware and cross-modal relevance patterns. In the first stage, we perform domain-specific fine-tuning of the CLIP encoders on a large-scale title--product dataset constructed from Target product information. Contrastive learning is applied between product titles and product images to adapt pretrained visual-text representations to e-commerce semantics. In the second stage, we explicitly align user queries with individual item modalities. Queries are separately aligned with product titles and product images using the proposed three-part hinge loss. This curriculum step encourages the model to learn modality-specific relevance signals while incorporating graded ranking supervision. In the final stage, we align queries with the fused product representations produced by the proposed mixture-of-modality-experts architecture. This stage enables the model to learn unified multimodal embeddings that capture both unimodal evidence and cross-modal interactions, resulting in more effective retrieval and improved downstream ranking performance. We construct our training data from three months of e-commerce search logs, yielding the top 20M aggregated query--item pairs. Each item is represented by its product title and image. To capture user preference, we derive a desirability label from aggregated interaction signals such as clicks, add-to-cart, and purchases. In addition, we incorporate an auxiliary semantic relevance signal by applying a cross-encoder trained on human-annotated relevance data to estimate query--item matching scores over the corpus. These complementary supervision signals jointly guide model training. We evaluate our approach on two held-out benchmarks capturing complementary aspects of retrieval quality: user preference alignment and semantic relevance .
