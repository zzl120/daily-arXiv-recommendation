Across four diverse corpora , SCAR achieves 92.8\ Retrieval-Augmented Generation has emerged as the standard for grounding Large Language Models in external knowledge. However, document chunking remains a fundamental bottleneck in the retrieval pipeline. Standard strategies, such as recursive character splitting, prioritize local structural integrity but create a rigid, static index. This leads to the boundary fragmentation problem, where critical evidence required for a specific query is split across adjacent segments. Because optimal chunking is query-dependent and constrained by the inherent structure of the source documents, even advanced paragraph-aware splitters cannot prevent logical context breaks at runtime. In technical, legal, financial, or other complex structured documents, critical evidence often spans across standard indexing boundaries. When a retrieval system returns only one fragment of a bifurcated logical unit , the downstream LLM suffers from incomplete context. occurs when the evidence $E$ required to answer query $Q$ is split such that $E = \{c_i, c_{i+1}\}$, but the retriever only identifies $c_i$ as relevant. While a dense retriever may occasionally capture both chunks in a Top-$k$ result, it offers no guarantee of logical continuity. Standard solutions like or are token-inefficient. These methods often inflate the context window with irrelevant "noise," which can degrade LLM performance and increase inference costs. Existing approaches to boundary fragmentation cluster into two camps. methods build semantically continuous units before retrieval , but fix boundaries at indexing time and cannot adapt to query-specific informational density. methods complement this. Static heuristics deterministically include neighbors and are token-inefficient. Iterative methods interleave retrieval with generation or reranking through repeated LLM calls. Utility-trained retrievers such as improve quality but require specialized data synthesis and training. SCAR fills the gap as a single-shot, training-free retrieval-time policy that adaptively expands only when query relevance and structural continuity jointly justify it, and it composes orthogonally with any indexing-time chunking strategy.
