Retrieval-Augmented Generation has emerged as a dominant paradigm for grounding large language model outputs in verifiable external knowledge . A RAG pipeline consists of two stages: a that selects relevant documents from a corpus, and a that produces an answer conditioned on those documents. While significant effort has been devoted to evaluating generation quality, retrieval quality under realistic, noisy conditions remains comparatively underexplored. Existing multi-hop QA benchmarks such as HotpotQA , MuSiQue , and 2WikiMultiHopQA provide rich question sets but are not structured for direct retrieval evaluation: they lack fixed-size document pools, do not guarantee gold-document positions, and do not provide controlled distractor sets for benchmarking. I address this gap with , a dataset that restructures HotpotQA into a retrieval-first evaluation format. Each example in StratRAG presents a retriever with a pool of 15 documents --- 2 gold and 13 distractors --- and asks it to identify the gold documents given only the question. This design enables direct computation of retrieval metrics independently of any downstream generator, making it suitable for ablation studies, retriever development, and RAG system evaluation. My contributions are as follows: I release StratRAG, a clean, structured retrieval benchmark derived from HotpotQA with verified gold-document indices across all 2{,}200 examples. I provide baseline results for three retrieval strategies across five standard metrics. I identify bridge questions as the primary open challenge, motivating research into RL-based retrieval policies for multi-hop reasoning. HotpotQA introduced the distractor setting in which 8 distractor paragraphs accompany 2 gold paragraphs, requiring models to reason across documents. MuSiQue introduced more compositional multi-hop chains, and 2WikiMultiHopQA leveraged structured Wikipedia relationships. StratRAG differs from these in its explicit focus on retrieval evaluation: gold documents are always at known positions, pool size is fixed, and all empty-text paragraphs are filtered out. RAGAS proposes end-to-end RAG evaluation metrics including faithfulness and answer relevance, but does not isolate the retrieval step. BEIR is a widely used retrieval benchmark covering diverse domains, but does not target multi-hop reasoning specifically. StratRAG complements these by providing a focused, multi-hop retrieval benchmark with controlled document pools. BM25 remains a strong sparse retrieval baseline. Dense retrieval using bi-encoders has shown strong performance on single-hop QA. Hybrid methods combining sparse and dense scores consistently outperform either alone on heterogeneous benchmarks. I evaluate all three approaches on StratRAG. StratRAG is derived from HotpotQA in its distractor configuration, which provides 10 paragraphs per question from Wikipedia.
