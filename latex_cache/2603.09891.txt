Building on the foundation of the inaugural 2024 track, this year’s challenge introduces long, multi-sentence narrative queries to better reflect the deep search task with the growing demand for reasoning-driven responses. Participants are tasked with designing pipelines that combine retrieval and generation while ensuring transparency and factual grounding. The track leverages the MS MARCO V2.1 corpus and employs a multi-layered evaluation framework encompassing relevance assessment, response completeness, attribution verification, and agreement analysis. By emphasizing multi-faceted narratives and attribution-rich answers from over 150 submissions this year, the TREC 2025 RAG Track aims to foster innovation in creating trustworthy, context-aware systems for retrieval augmented generation. This paper provides an overview of the TREC 2025 Retrieval Augment Generation Track. The of the Retrieval Augmented Generation Track builds on the foundation laid by the inaugural TREC 2024 RAG Track~, pushing research further into systems that integrate retrieval and generation for complex, real-world information needs. We harness the institutional knowledge and resources provided by the National Institute of Standards and Technology via the Text Retrieval Conference to tackle these challenges. Now in its 34 year, TREC has led the way in many aspects of evaluation in information retrieval , natural language processing , and beyond, producing many innovations that the community take for granted today. Building upon last year's evaluation strategy at the TREC 2024 RAG Track, a key change this year is moving from short, keyword-style queries to multi-sentence, long and complex narratives that mimic scenarios. This shift reflects the growing demand for RAG and agentic systems capable of nuanced interpretation, broader evidence coverage, and reasoning-driven responses. Participants are challenged to design pipelines that not only combine retrieval and generation but also maintain transparency and factual grounding in their outputs. These changes aim to foster innovation in creating systems that can handle intricate queries and deliver well-supported, context-rich answers. To support these advancements, the track continues to use the MS MARCO V2.1 document corpus constructed last year at the TREC 2024 RAG Track, which contains segmentation and document deduplication, useful for assessing retrieval and generation tasks in our track. The corpus ensures a diverse document representation, enabling participants to build systems that can retrieve relevant evidence and generate responses grounded in verifiable and reliable sources. By introducing narratives and emphasizing attribution, the Track encourages approaches that go beyond surface-level matching, requiring deeper reasoning and synthesis of information across multiple documents. This year, the track employs a multi-layered evaluation workflow to ensure reliability and depth: Human assessors judge document relevance using sub-narratives, assigning relevance scores between 0 and 4 based on the number of sub-narratives being answered in the given document. For relevant documents, key information units are extracted and mapped to sub-narratives to measure content coverage and answer completeness. Each sentence in a generated answer is checked against its cited document to verify factual grounding. System labels are compared with human judgments using metrics such as Cohen’s $ $ to assess alignment beyond chance. This layered approach combines manual and automated methods for assessment, enabling a fine-grained evaluation of retrieval quality, attribution accuracy, and response completeness. By emphasizing the narrative-driven queries and attribution-rich answers, the Track aims to push the boundaries of retrieval-augmented generation research, fostering innovations that improve reasoning, coverage, and trustworthiness in AI-driven systems. The track comprised four interconnected tasks: , , , and ; all built on the MS MARCO v2.1 segmented document collection~, which addressed deduplication and provided fine-grained segmentation to support robust retrieval and grounded generation. Participants were given 105 queries ; their ultimate task was to return, for the AG and RAG tasks, well-formed answers for each narrative . The Retrieval task could be viewed as an intermediate product in a full RAG pipeline. Furthermore, the Relevance Judgment task, required participants to provide a set of relevance judgments. Actual submissions to the TREC 2025 RAG Track were in the form of structured JSON, wherein each answer sentence is explicitly linked to citations of documents within the corpus that supported the assertions made in the sentence~. Here is an overview of the four tasks conducted at the TREC 2025 RAG Track: In the Retrieval task, participants are given a set of narratives and must return a TREC-style ranked list of the top-100 segment IDs from the MS MARCO V2.1 deduped segment collection~. Unlike previous MS MARCO data, where passages were selected to be short human-readable answers, the V2.1 passages were constructed using a sliding window approach over full documents to generate more ``natural'' content. These passages form segments suitable for RAG. The AG task isolates the generation component by fixing retrieval: participants receive a top‑k ranked list of segments and must generate an answer of $ $ 400 words, broken into sentences, with sentence-level citations to supporting segment IDs from the provided top-k ranked list. The RAG task evaluates end-to-end systems; participants bring their own retrievers, rerankers, generators, and pipeline, but must map all evidence to MS MARCO V2.1 segment IDs for evaluation. The Relevance Judgment task determines how well individual documents address the information need expressed in a narrative. Each document is scored on a 0--4 scale, where 0 indicates the document is not relevant and 4 indicates comprehensive coverage of the narrative. This task is designed to capture varying degrees of relevance for complex, multi-sentence queries, ensuring that retrieval systems are evaluated on their ability to return documents that meaningfully contribute to answering the full narrative. Reference ranked lists of top-k segments were provided for those who did not wish to perform retrieval on the document collection themselves. This represented the input to the AG task, where participants could focus on the ``augmented generation'' part of RAG. The top documents for each narrative were provided based on an effective multi-stage ranking pipeline, described below: The provided ranked lists included a combination of first-stage retrieval and advanced reranking strategies. For first-stage retrieval, reciprocal rank fusion across two high-performing models: SPLADE-v3~ and Snowflake's Arctic-Embed-L~ was employed. This hybrid approach leveraged the complementary strengths of sparse and dense retrieval, as RRF has consistently demonstrated improved retrieval effectiveness across multiple evaluation benchmarks in the RAG 2024 track~. Following retrieval, a reranking pipeline was deployed to refine the candidate lists. RankLLM~, a neural reranker was applied to process the fused retrieval results and improve narrative-document relevance. Following this, RankQwen3-32B, a reranker operating on the top 1,000 candidates from the previous stage, produced a reordered list, from which the top 100 documents were selected. This cascading reranking strategy ensured that the final ranked lists reflected both lexical and semantic relevance, providing participants with highly optimized results. For each narrative, a decomposition was undertaken to identify and construct a comprehensive set of sub-narratives that collectively capture all dimensions of the original narrative. These sub-narratives were derived from both the initial query cluster to generate the primary narrative and the narrative text itself. The overall process for sub-narrative generation was semi-automated. The first phase of this process utilized GPT‑4.1 nano, which was provided with the narrative and its corresponding query list and was instructed to generate an exhaustive collection of candidate sub-narratives . Each prompt produced a JSON-formatted list of dictionary objects, where each object included a text field describing the sub-narrative and an importance field denoting its relevance to the narrative . In the second phase, both the original narrative and the preliminary sub-narrative list obtained from the preceding step are supplied to GPT‑4.1, which generated a refined and more coherent set of sub-narratives while adhering to the same JSON output structure . Relevance assessment at the Track focuses on determining how well retrieved documents satisfy complex, multi-faceted information needs expressed through narratives. These narratives often encompass diverse aspects, making direct judgment on the entire narrative challenging. To address this complexity, the process begins by decomposing each narrative into a set of sub-narratives, which represent simpler and more focused components of the original information need. This decomposition enables assessors to evaluate relevance in a structured and consistent manner, reducing ambiguity and improving reliability across judgments. The original narrative is broken down into distinct sub-narratives that capture specific thematic dimensions. For example, the broad narrative about sports and its societal impact is decomposed into focused areas such as , , , , and . These sub-narratives make it easier to analyze and address different aspects of the overarching narrative. This structured approach ensures that complex narratives are organized into manageable, well-defined components for deeper exploration. Once sub-narratives are established, assessors assign relevance labels to segments using a five-point scale based on coverage: 0 for segments unrelated to the narrative, 1 for segments offering general but non-specific information, 2 for content addressing one sub-narrative, 3 for segments covering two or three sub-narratives, and 4 for those addressing four or more sub-narratives. This approach simplifies the evaluation of complex narratives, ensures alignment between assessors, and provides a robust foundation for subsequent stages such as nugget extraction and attribution. By focusing on sub-narrative coverage rather than the entire narrative, the relevance assessment process enhances interpretability and fairness in evaluating retrieval and generation systems~. Support evaluation represents an attempt to determine whether information in a RAG answer is factually supported by its cited document~. Evaluation in the is conducted by breaking down the answer, with the unit of evaluation defined at a . An answer $r$ is assumed to be segmented into $n$ sentences, $r = \{a_1, , a_n\}$, where each sentence $a_i$ can contain a maximum of $k$ document citations $ $, with each document drawn from a corpus. Support is calculated as the function $f $ where $f$ can be a human or LLM assessor that generates a scalar value $s_{i,j}$, indicating the extent to which the cited document $d_j$ provides support for sentence $a_i$. As with all TREC evaluations, NIST provides the resources to perform human evaluations based on the guidance of the track organizers. NIST first trains every human assessor to understand the task, and then each human assessor evaluates each narrative sequentially. This year, the support evaluation focuses on sparse annotations by restricting to evaluate only the first cited document, similar to last year~. For evaluation, NIST assessors use a three-level grade, with the following descriptions: : All of the information in the answer sentence is factually consistent with and supported by the cited segment. : Some of the information in the answer sentence is factually consistent with and supported by the cited segment, but other parts of the sentence are not supported. : The cited segment is completely irrelevant and does not support any part of the answer sentence. An edge case arises when a sentence contains zero citations: the support level is automatically considered ``No support'', as the sentence does not cite any retrieved segment. This year, two cases are considered for support evaluation: , in which GPT-OSS 120B performs the automatic assessment, and , in which NIST assessors perform the evaluation and assign a support level to each answer--document pair based on their judgment.
