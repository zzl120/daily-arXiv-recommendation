Using a permutation-based evaluation framework, they show that multilingual encoder-based embedding models exhibit systematic positional imbalance: early segments are over-represented in the document embedding, while later segments are progressively less reflected. They attribute this effect to front-loaded attention distributions of the pooling token, whose query assigns disproportionate attention mass to early key tokens across transformer layers. However, their analysis focuses on representation profiles rather than downstream retrieval, and it remains open whether the same attention-based mechanism applies across pooling architectures. To mitigate this imbalance, they propose an inference-time attention calibration that redistributes the pooling token's attention more evenly across document positions. The method partitions the key sequence into contiguous baskets of size $$, isolating the document representation token in its own basket to avoid redistributing special-token attention across content tokens, and enforces equal total attention mass per basket while preserving relative attention weights within each basket. For a token at position $k$ assigned to basket $b $, the calibrated attention weight is: \[ a^{}_k = { _{j b } a_j} {n_{}}, \] where $a_k$ is the original post-softmax attention weight and $n_{}$ is the number of baskets. Calibration is applied only to the pooling token's query row in selected transformer layers and requires no retraining. While this method was shown to flatten representation profiles in a controlled permutation setting, its effect on downstream retrieval has not been examined. We study attention calibration as an inference-time mitigation for position bias in dense retrieval. The coefficient enables controlled exploration of the trade-off between positional redistribution and retrieval effectiveness, rather than committing to a single fixed level of redistribution. We report retrieval performance using nDCG@10 with binary relevance labels, following . To summarize performance across positional groups, we report the harmonic mean : \[ = { _{i=1}^{n} {_i}}, \] where $n$ is the number of positional groups and $_i$ is the score for group $i$. The harmonic mean penalizes low-performing groups more strongly than the arithmetic mean and therefore reflects both overall retrieval effectiveness and sensitivity to positional disparities. We therefore use as the primary summary metric for the PosQ experiments. We additionally report the Position Sensitivity Index introduced by , which captures the relative gap between the best- and worst-performing positional groups. PSI is useful for diagnosing positional spread, but reductions in PSI are only desirable when retrieval effectiveness is preserved. We therefore interpret PSI together with or aggregate nDCG@10 rather than as a standalone success criterion. We studied inference-time attention calibration as a mitigation for positional bias in dense retrieval. We extended the method of with a strength coefficient $ $ that interpolates between the original and fully calibrated attention distribution. This extension enables a systematic study of how basket size $$, calibrated layer set $^C$, and strength $ $ trade off positional fairness against retrieval effectiveness. We find that partial calibration provides a better retrieval trade-off than full calibration: across three embedding models, the harmonic mean of nDCG@10 on FineWeb-PosQ peaks at moderate strength , while stronger settings improve late-position performance at the cost of begin-position performance. That this outcome is not a given is shown by the position-agnostic SEA baseline, which reaches comparable PSI reductions only at a substantial effectiveness cost. The configuration therefore offers a simple inference-time intervention for dense retrieval systems, and its transfer from a small English-only parameter exploration to PosIR’s multilingual, cross-lingual, and broad-domain coverage suggests that the calibration-strength trade-off characterized here is not specific to the datasets used for parameter analysis. *{Limitations} This work focuses exclusively on dense embedding-based retrieval. Lexical retrieval methods, cross-encoder rerankers, and hybrid approaches are out of scope. The parameter grid covers three basket sizes, two layer-set choices, and two strength values, refined for $ $ through a fine-grained sweep on FineWeb-PosQ. The identified default is the strongest-performing configuration in this grid and an intuitive choice given the ablation, but it is not the result of a global joint optimization over $$, $^C$, and $ $, which would be prohibitively expensive. We show how calibration affects retrieval performance but do not provide a mechanistic explanation for why partial calibration gives a better retrieval trade-off than full calibration. In particular, we do not analyze how basket-level redistribution interacts with learned attention patterns, pooling strategy, or positional encoding. This remains an open question. The attention calibration method studied here redistributes the pooling token's attention through basket-level equalization.
