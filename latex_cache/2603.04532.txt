In our work, we investigate how temporal corpus drift affects FreshStack, a retrieval benchmark focused on technical domains. We examine two independent corpus snapshots of FreshStack from October 2024 and October 2025 to answer questions about LangChain. Our analysis shows that all but one query posed in 2024 remain fully supported by the 2025 corpus, as relevant documents ``migrate'' from LangChain to competitor repositories, such as LlamaIndex. Next, we compare the accuracy of retrieval models on both snapshots and observe only minor shifts in model rankings, with overall strong correlation of up to 0.978 Kendall $ $ at Recall@50. Under the Cranfield paradigm, test collections are constructed once with a fixed document corpus, a set of queries, and relevance judgments. Popular IR benchmarks such as MS MARCO~, TREC Deep Learning ~, and BEIR~ all follow this approach, with fixed corpus snapshots and relevance judgments created during benchmark construction. Test collections have helped establish reusable baselines~, but information changes and corpora continue to evolve and incur temporal changes~. Real-world document collections, such as technical documentation, are highly dynamic, as code is frequently created, deleted, and reorganized. Over time, the document collection snapshot captured in a benchmark diverges from the current state of the domain. In our work, we evaluate temporal drift in , introduced by Thakur et al.~, using temporal snapshots of technical documentation in LangChain. We construct test collections using two independent corpus snapshots of ten GitHub repositories providing documentation for retrieval-augmented generation frameworks and developer tools, by reproducing at October 2024 and extending it to October 2025. During this period, LangChain's documentation decreased by 67\ We reproduce the corpus construction and generate relevance judgments at both snapshots. We retrieve potentially relevant documents using a hybrid fusion by incorporating BM25~, BGE ~, E5 Mistral ~, and Qwen3 Embedding~. Next, we use Cohere's Command A as the judge~ for nugget-level support assessment~ to automatically construct test collections at both time snapshots for 203 queries in LangChain~. We explore the following three research questions in our work: Can existing queries be grounded in a corpus dynamically changing with time? How does the distribution of relevant documents across repositories change over time? Do model rankings remain consistent under temporal drift in technical documentation? The contribution of this work is, to our knowledge, the first to evaluate temporal changes in a highly dynamic corpus, namely technical documentation in a niche domain. Prior work has evaluated temporal changes in more generic corpora, such as news~, federal documents~, or scientific collections in LongEval~. Our main contribution stems from our evaluation of various retrieval systems on a dynamically changing corpus, which enables us to assess performance changes over time. Our experimental results demonstrate that surprisingly query remains fully grounded and answerable in both corpus snapshots, despite extensive restructuring in repositories such as LangChain. A major reason for this observation lies in the fact that relevant documents present in LangChain in the 2024 snapshot, are distributed across related GitHub repositories in the 2025 snapshot. Finally, model rankings on the document retrieval task remain strongly correlated , demonstrating that benchmarks can robustly evaluate retrieval systems, despite temporal changes affecting the corpus. Temporal drift refers to the systematic change in a corpus, queries and its associated relevance landscape over time, caused by additions, deletions, updates, or reorganization of documents. Recent work on evaluating temporal drift in test collections, suggests that benchmarks may lose validity over time~, those studies primarily addresses judgment variation or query drift rather than corpus drift. What remains unclear is how benchmarks behave when the corpus undergoes substantial reorganization. Other works share conceptual similarity with our experiments~. However, these explorations focus on web search data, where queries are compact and short. In contrast, our analysis focuses on more complex question settings, posing a strong challenge for the retrieval systems being tested. Futhermore, Cancellieri et al.~ explore similar topics by varying both the corpus and queries across time. However, in this work we limit ourselves to a dynamic corpus such as technical documentation. In the context of coding, queries tend to be related to the same task, but due to documentation changes, implementation can vary from version to version. Therefore, in our context a dynamic corpus provides sufficient material for examination. While observing multiple domains may allow for a comprehensive analysis on the effects of temporal changes on retrieval performance, focusing on a single domain allows us to study temporal changes more effectively. LangChain is a representative domain for temporal exploration, as its GitHub repository is one of the most active and rapidly changing repositories in NLP~. Our pipeline follows FreshStack~ and automatically creates a retrieval benchmark at any time step based on the steps listed below. Each step is described in the following subsections. : We collect and chunk two versions of LangChain--related repositories for corpus preparation. : We generate nuggets, or key facts using question and answers from Stack Overflow. : We retrieve a subset of document chunks from the corpus, potentially relevant in answering the query. : Using the subset of document chunks, for every query, we determine whether each chunk supports a nugget . The goal here is to collect and construct a corpus using two distinct versions of LangChain related repositories, at different time snapshots. Moreover, we combine multiple repositories to form our corpus because a query tagged as LangChain on Stack Overflow might require context from other repositories. For example, a LangChain question could concern the ability to properly load a HuggingFace transformer model, or use ChromaDB. We provide the ten relevant repositories used to form our corpus in . To automate the procedure, we use the main branch snapshot for all GitHub repositories as of October 2024 and October 2025. After providing the repository name, we search for GitHub commits prior to those dates and use the latest commit hash. Then, we all documentation, code files, Jupyter notebooks, etc., with a maximum of 2048 tokens. To create a unique document identifier, we use the repository name, the relative file path within the local repository and the chunk start and end byte information, for disambiguation. Nuggets are atomic key facts that are essential for constructing an answer~. We automatically generate nuggets from Stack Overflow queries and answers to break down long-form answers and facilitate document-level assessment. The nuggets also crucially serve as queries in the oracle retrieval phase~. The use of nuggets as a tool for evaluation has grown in the field of retrieval system performance~. Similarly, LLMs demonstrate a strong ability to extract relevant and concise information from documents, rivaling the human annotation process, which is cumbersome and time-consuming~. Therefore, for each query in LangChain, we reutilize the nuggets generated using GPT-4o in FreshStack~. To create a test collection we require judgment pools. In other words, we must retrieve and annotate documents that are relevant for each query. Previous studies show the importance of using diverse retrieval models when constructing judgment pools. First, we utilize BM25~, which retrieves lexically similar documents. Our next model is BGE ~, a dense retriever model} with an embedding size of 3584 and an 8K context length. Next, E5 Mistral ~ is another dense retriever model} with an embedding size of 4096. Furthermore, we employ Qwen3 } which has an embedding size of 4096 and a 32K context length. Qwen3 embeddings achieve state-of-the-art accuracy in retrieval on various domains~. Finally, we normalize the retrieval scores for each model between $ $ and sum them up in hybrid fusion. To further increase diversity in our judgment pools, we use different approaches to construct queries in the oracle setting. We begin by using the original answers provided on Stack Overflow. Next we use the generated nuggets by GPT-4o, which contains concise and structed information. We then generate sub questions using the Qwen3-4B-Instruct model.} Instead of using the original verbose user query, we use Qwen3 to decompose it into sub-questions, enabling effective retrieval through chain of thought prompting~. Furthermore, we use Qwen3 to generate the closed-book answer to the Stack Overflow query. In this work, we investigate how temporal corpus drift affects FreshStack on the LangChain subset. We first observe that ``all except one'' queries continue to have supported documents for each nugget in the October 2025 corpus snapshot. Next, we investigate the reason for the high utility of such queries. We found that functions and classes often migrate to other competitor frameworks, e.g., LangChain $ $ LlamaIndex. Finally, we observe that model rankings across both temporal snapshots of the corpus are strongly correlated, indicating that retrieval systems are consistent in retrieving relevant documents despite changes in the corpus over time. These findings suggest that FreshStack is robust to dynamic changes in technical code documentation. A pivotal aspect of our work is the focus on a test collection built from multiple GitHub repositories as the corpus. Due to the way LangChain is structured, much of the code functionality, even when deprecated within one repository, is preserved or migrated to related or competitor codebases. However, it is pertinent to consider to what extent other domains will behave similarly. For example, in Wikipedia, information within the corpus may change over time, and even the answer to a question may evolve. In such contexts, it may be necessary to generate nuggets at each temporal snapshot before constructing the test collection.
