In such environments, traditional ID-based collaborative filtering fails due to the lack of co-occurrence signals. Conversely, fine-tuning standard Large Language Models presents significant practical barriers, specifically regarding high operational costs and the challenge of managing frequent data drift. To address these challenges, we propose LLMAR , a tuning-free recommendation framework. Our approach is novel in that it goes beyond simple embedding utilization; by systematically integrating the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, it captures user "latent motives" without the need for a training process. Specifically, we introduce three engineering contributions: Inference-Driven Annotation, which employs LLMs to transform user behavioral history from simple time-series data into structured semantic motives, enabling reasoning-based matching unattainable by ID-based methods; a Reflection Loop, a self-correction mechanism where the LLM critically verifies and refines its own generated search queries to mitigate hallucinations and resolve "context competition" between past history and current instructions; and a Cost-Effective Architecture, composed entirely of tuning-free components designed for asynchronous batch processing to minimize maintenance costs. We evaluated our method using public benchmarks and a sparse, text-rich industrial dataset . In these domains, "data sparsity" is pronounced due to the overwhelmingly low total volume of interactions compared to general e-commerce platforms. Consequently, traditional ID-based Collaborative Filtering methods often perform poorly due to the lack of co-occurrence information . On the other hand, a distinct characteristic of these domains is that individual items and interactions are associated with rich, specialized textual information. Therefore, the success of industrial recommender systems hinges on how effectively the semantic interpretation of text information can be integrated into the system to complement sparse ID information. Recently, the emergence of Large Language Models , which possess extensive world knowledge and advanced reasoning capabilities, has opened new avenues for addressing this challenge . LLMs demonstrate superior task performance even in zero-shot settings , and their integration into recommender systems is rapidly advancing. Currently, this integration is generally categorized into three paradigms . The first is the Generative Recommender . These models integrate collaborative filtering information with world knowledge in the parameter space by leveraging the features and generative capabilities of pre-trained LLMs. The second is LLM as Ranker . In this approach, candidates are first narrowed down by traditional, robust, and lightweight recommendation models , followed by reranking using an LLM. This paradigm offers ease of implementation and allows for flexible control via prompting. The third, which is the focus of this study, is LLM-enhanced Retrieval . Applying frameworks such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation and Agent systems, this approach performs item retrieval using queries or embeddings generated by an LLM. Particular attention is being paid to approaches that attempt to extract semantic preferences by moving away from ID-based representations and employing natural language profile generation or query-based retrieval . However, from the perspective of practical industrial application, the first two paradigms face structural barriers. For Generative Recommenders, fine-tuning for domain specialization not only consumes vast computational resources but also creates an operational bottleneck due to the retraining costs required to keep up with daily changing on-site conditions . Furthermore, the tight coupling between the foundation model and the recommendation model hinders the ability to benefit from the rapidly evolving capabilities of LLMs. Meanwhile, LLM as Ranker is difficult to apply to the entire item pool due to computational constraints, and since it relies on the performance of the upstream retrieval model , it struggles to contribute to fundamental improvements in retrieval accuracy. In contrast, the third paradigm, LLM-enhanced Retrieval, allows for a tuning-free configuration, offering a superior balance between cost and flexibility. Nevertheless, in complex decision-support scenarios such as B2B, simply vectorizing items is insufficient. Two essential challenges remain to be solved. The first is the Semantic Gap between "past behavioral history" and "recommended items." In specialized domains, user actions are based on deep "Latent Motives" that cannot be read from superficial logs. Although existing research attempts to bridge this gap by inferring preferences from history, there is still room for improvement. A process is needed that goes beyond mere summary of history to interpret the context of how user preferences have changed over time and convert this into a clear "Search Intent" for exploring future items. The second is Context Competition in recommendation constraints. In advanced recommendation scenarios, "implicit constraints" based on past behavioral history may conflict with "explicit constraints" such as current user instructions or diversity requirements. In tasks requiring high reliability, such as on-site risk management, inappropriate recommendations driven by past trends or LLM-specific hallucinations are unacceptable. Meeting complex requirements necessitates a process that does not end with a single generation but critically verifies and corrects its own output . {fig/fig1.pdf} Our specific approach is as follows. First, we build a robust retrieval infrastructure capable of semantic search by generating and embedding text information for items using an LLM. Next, to bridge the semantic gap, we treat user behavioral history as time-series "bundles" and use an LLM to generate and accumulate "Annotations" that extract preference evolution and universal user tastes. During recommendation, these Annotations are used to generate search queries, thereby projecting latent preferences into the search space. Furthermore, we introduce a Reflection Loop to resolve context competition. This mechanism allows the LLM to verify and correct whether the generated queries and search results contradict the user's current intent or constraints, achieving recommendations that balance diversity and accuracy. Since all components are composed of tuning-free LLMs, the framework is extremely advantageous in terms of model updates and scalability, and can flexibly handle arbitrary natural language inputs at runtime. The integration of LLMs into recommender systems has primarily advanced along three trajectories to bridge the semantic gap inherent in conventional ID-based methods: Generative Recommenders, LLM as Ranker, and LLM-enhanced Retrieval. Generative Recommenders treat item IDs as language tokens and formulate the recommendation task as a sequence generation problem. Represented by models such as P5 , these approaches demonstrate high performance through multi-task learning. However, large-scale fine-tuning is indispensable to align the ID space with the linguistic space. This entails high operational costs, particularly in addressing the cold-start problem and in real-world environments requiring frequent item updates. On the other hand, the LLM as Ranker paradigm leverages the reasoning capabilities of LLMs specifically for re-ranking candidate items. Nevertheless, due to inference costs and context window constraints, scoring the entire item pool is infeasible; thus, performance remains heavily dependent on the precision of the preceding lightweight retriever. In contrast, this study focuses on LLM-enhanced Retrieval , which utilizes LLMs as encoders or query generators to enable full-item retrieval while curbing computational costs. Notably, attempts to overcome the limitations of ID-based methods by describing user behavioral history in natural language and performing retrieval using its embedding representation. However, existing RAG-based methods are limited to either concatenating behavioral history into a single long sequence or summarizing it into a static profile . These approaches leave room for research regarding "Temporal Granularity"—specifically, how user preferences evolve over time—and a deeper investigation into the transition and invariance of preferences is desired. This study addresses this information loss by structuring history into chronological "Bundles" and assigning LLM-generated annotations. Approaches to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs have been established through Chain-of-Thought and Reflection mechanisms, where the model critically verifies and corrects its own output . These techniques have been proven to mitigate hallucinations and improve logical consistency in tasks requiring complex reasoning by interposing intermediate thought processes. In the domain of recommender systems, attempts have been made to introduce reasoning steps within agent-based models . However, most prior works have focused on response generation in conversational interfaces or ensuring consistency in the justification of final rankings. To the best of our knowledge, no studies have incorporated an explicit self-correction loop in the pre-retrieval stage—specifically, the search query generation process—to resolve the "Context Competition" that arises between a user's latent intent and their past history. The Reflection Loop proposed in this study possesses novelty in that it autonomously verifies whether the search query aligns with the user's current intent and optimizes the projection into the search space. However, identifying technical challenges for real-world deployment has also highlighted several promising directions for future research. The performance of our method relies on the quality of textual information; consequently, it may underperform compared to ID-based methods when item descriptions are poor. Expanding the framework to Multimodal LLMs that integrate non-textual modalities—such as image data and interaction logs—is a promising approach to compensate for incomplete metadata and broaden the applicable domains. While the multi-step reasoning of LLMs enhances interpretability, achieving the millisecond-level latency required for B2C applications remains a challenge. Future work involves distinguishing between domain-knowledge-specific and reasoning-specific tasks when calling LLMs via API. By utilizing specialized models to limit the number of parameters, we expect to compress latency to a level suitable for real-time operations while maintaining inference quality. Our current implementation uses fixed time windows for segmenting history, yet user interests do not necessarily shift at uniform intervals. The next step is to introduce dynamic clustering based on the semantic similarity of behaviors. This will allow for a more precise separation of contextually distinct interactions , thereby improving the resolution of the generated Annotations.
