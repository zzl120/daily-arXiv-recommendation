 Generative Recommendation has recently transitioned from atomic item-indexing to Semantic ID -based frameworks to capture intrinsic item relationships and enhance generalization. However, the adoption of high-granularity SIDs leads to two critical challenges: prohibitive training overhead due to sequence expansion and unstable performance reliability characterized by non-monotonic accuracy fluctuations. We identify that these disparate issues are fundamentally rooted in the Semantic Dilution Effect, where redundant tokens waste massive computation and dilute the already sparse learning signals in recommendation. To counteract this, we propose STAMP , a framework utilizing a dual-end optimization strategy. We argue that effective SID learning requires simultaneously addressing low input information density and sparse output supervision. On the input side, Semantic Adaptive Pruning dynamically filters redundancy during the forward pass, converting noise-laden sequences into compact, information-rich representations. On the output side, Multi-step Auxiliary Prediction employs a multi-token objective to densify feedback, strengthening long-range dependency capture and ensuring robust learning signals despite compressed inputs. Unifying input purification and signal amplification, STAMP enhances both training efficiency and representation capability. Generative Recommendation has emerged as a paradigm shift in the field of recommender systems, reformulating the task as an end-to-end sequence generation problem~. However, early frameworks relying on atomic item identifiers were limited by the independence of unique IDs, which hinders information sharing. This limitation often leads to vocabulary explosion and cold-start problem for new items. To mitigate these issues, Semantic Identifier -based Generative Recommendation ~ decomposes each item ID into a sequence of SIDs. The shared SID vocabulary across different items enhances the generalization ability of such methods , which is why it has gained attention recently. {figures/figure_1.png} Although SID-GR demonstrates a higher potential compared to item ID-based GR, it also exposes two critical limitations that hinder its practical deployment: High resource consumption and slow training speed~. Expanding an item ID into multiple SIDs significantly increases the sequence length, leading to quadratic growth in memory consumption and training time. Large performance fluctuations and low stability~. The relationship between the granularity of SIDs and recommendation accuracy is non-monotonic and exhibits inconsistent patterns across different models and datasets. Currently, research on SID-GR remains in its early stages. Efforts have prioritized improving SID informativeness~ and generation speed~, while optimizations of the "GR" backbone aimed at mitigating these specific inefficiencies remain relatively under-explored. Intriguingly, our empirical analysis reveals that these two seemingly disparate issues—training inefficiency and performance instability—are actually two symptoms of the same underlying pathology: the Semantic Dilution Effect. We find that user intent is typically driven by a sparse subset of critical tokens within SIDs, meaning much of the sequence length consists of harmful redundancy~. This redundancy not only wastes massive computation but also dilutes the already sparse learning signal in recommendation, making it difficult for the model to distinguish pivotal features from noise ~. Unlike general language modeling, the sparse feedback loop in recommendation is insufficient to discipline such high-granularity, high-redundancy sequences. Viewed through the lens of Semantic Dilution, existing optimization strategies from adjacent domains reveal inherent limitations. and ~ mitigate the computational burden of long-sequence decoding; however, these are primarily optimized for the long-horizon inference of complex reasoning tasks, whereas SID-GR is severely constrained by the massive retraining overhead required to capture volatile user interests. ~ aims to skip redundant inputs, but unlike NLP where non-informative particles are distinct, every token in a high-granularity SID is a semantically dense identifier with complex inter-dependencies, making it difficult to distinguish "salient" tokens from noise without compromising representation quality~. Generative recommendation has recently emerged as a transformative paradigm~. Prior to this paradigm shift, researchers primarily utilized LLMs as auxiliary components to enhance conventional discriminative models~. In contrast, the generative retrieval framework serializes item interaction sequences into discrete tokens. This approach allows the model to directly predict target items in an autoregressive manner~. Building on this foundation, subsequent studies have focused on refining the learning process by developing optimized training frameworks~ to handle large-scale data. Furthermore, advanced strategies such as reinforcement learning~ have been integrated to better align generated outputs with complex user preferences. The efficacy of generative recommendation relies heavily on the tokenization strategy. The Semantic ID paradigm, pioneered by TIGER~, represents items as sequences of hierarchical codewords. This structure enables knowledge transfer among semantically similar entities. Early SID methods primarily derived codes from content modalities. However, subsequent research argued that content alone is insufficient for capturing user preferences. Consequently, a growing body of work~ has shifted towards incorporating collaborative signals. Regarding encoding efficiency~, approaches like OneRec~ and RPG~ have streamlined the quantization pipeline using Residual Quantized K-means and Optimized Product Quantization ~ to effectively reduce the codebook size. More recently, GRID~ introduced a unified and open-source framework specifically designed for SID-GR. FORGE~ addressed the challenges of industrial deployment by introducing a large-scale benchmark and proposing advanced optimization strategies to ensure SID robustness in real-world scenarios. Extensive efforts have been made to address the efficiency challenges of generative recommendation from different perspectives. Regarding model complexity, techniques like quantization~ and knowledge distillation~ effectively reduce the memory usage. However, these approaches usually impose additional post-training phases and often incur a loss in model performance. To target latency reduction, researchers have explored speculative sampling~ and efficient attention mechanisms~. More recently, token pruning has been investigated as a promising direction to skip redundant computation in NLP and CV~.
