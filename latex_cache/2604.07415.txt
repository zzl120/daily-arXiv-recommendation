As complex queries often require multi-step reasoning over the retrieved information, with no clear or predetermined reasoning path, they remain challenging. Recent approaches train models using reinforcement learning on the model's outcome, showing promise in improving how models handle complex information. We introduce SubSearch, a specialized framework that shifts from outcome-only supervision to intermediate reward signals that incentivize planning high-quality reasoning. Unlike previous work on process reward modeling, which focuses on training a separate reward model with annotated trajectories by either human annotators or large LLM judges, SubSearch directly optimizes the generator using process rewards, which we define as internally-derived rewards, eliminating the need for external supervision, and moving towards autonomous information-intensive reasoning. Experiments on seven benchmarks show that rewarding intermediate reasoning steps with intrinsic rewards leads to more robust reasoning traces in both QA and multi-hop QA datasets over using only outcome rewards. SubSearch can help in building reasoning traces that allow agents to better integrate search engines for complex query answering, while offering a data-efficient alternative to supervised process modeling.} Focus on model reasoning has shifted from simple question-answering to information-intensive complex tasks, for which current large language models still face challenges, such as the need for external information . Search agents have emerged as specialized retrieval-augmented generation systems that, compared to traditional RAG systems that rely on static retrieval from a fixed database, treat search as a dynamic tool . Dynamic search provides access to vast, up to date information, however a challenge still exists in how to reason over and aggregate retrieved information into useful knowledge . Previous work has looked at how to guide the reasoning process through prompt-based approaches , or supervised fine-tuning . As the possible reasoning trajectories of the LLM effectively cover an infinite search space, supervised methods cannot scale, blocking them from generalizing to multi-step reasoning for unpredictable, real-world information retrieval tasks. A shift towards optimization with reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards has addressed the generalizability concerns of SFT . train an LLM to interleave reasoning and search by reasoning over the task, generating a search query, and calling a dynamic search tool to retrieve relevant documents. Their model then aggregates the retrieved information to evaluate its knowledge and, if insufficient, reformulates the query to apply subsequent steps until the knowledge gap dissapears. Once the aggregated information is sufficient to answer the complex query, the model generates a response that conditions a sparse, outcome-based reward used for model training. Following this paradigm shift, multiple aspects of RLVF-based agents have been explored, e.g., tool calling , formatting , evidence generation instead of retrieval , or parallel query decomposition . Most prior work keeps the outcome-based rewards fixed. However, reliance on outcome-only rewards enables reward hacking , where a model can reach a correct conclusion through flawed intermediate reasoning. We propose SubSearch, a framework for training deep search agents using intermediate reasoning rewards that incentivize the generation and decomposition of complex reasoning traces. Unlike process reward models that rely on external supervision, we introduce process rewards as internally-derived signals conditioned on the generator alone. We propose: a template for decomposing a complex task into subqueries used for dynamic search, a policy that assigns intermediate rewards at the subquery level , and a comparison of aggregation methods for constructing a stable and informative signal. In reinforcement learning an agent adapts while learning from an environment by taking actions and receiving feedback, reinforcing the agent's beliefs of the environment. RL has been incorporated into LLMs through human feedback , and through RLVF. The model update based on feedback is often made through algorithms such as proximal policy optimization , direct preference optimization , or group relative policy optimization . In contrast, supervised fine-tuning trains the model using annotations, requiring extensive resources. While often reaching better effectiveness, it can cause models to memorize solution paths, bringing generalizability limitations and evidence that reasoning is not achieved . LLMs are powerful reasoners~, however, their performance is conditioned on domain-specific knowledge , which is often insufficient. RAG addresses the knowledge gap by incorporating external information as context to the model. The main challenge is knowing how to reason over these external sources and aggregate them to form an answer . Recent frameworks such as Search-R1 employ reinforcement learning to develop specialized search policies, where the model iteratively reasons about its knowledge and refines its search trajectory. This paradigm offers an effective solution to reasoning over relevant documents. Several frameworks cater for the interaction between agents and dynamic search systems. DeepResearcher treats search as an interactive engine using web search interactions instead of retrieving from a fixed corpus. OTC and MaskSearch optimize for calling the search engine. ZeroSearch eliminates the search engine entirely, and trains the model to generate documents instead of retrieving. Another challenge addressed by previous work is deciding on when to search. IKEA optimizes the model to search only when crucial information does not already exist in its parametric knowledge. R-Search does not control when the search engine is called. It can be called at any generated token. InForage and O\ -Searcher apply specialized SFT using human-guided search reasoning datasets with annotated reasoning trajectories. TreeSearch has a different schema for GRPO, modeling it as a tree search structure, where each tree node represents a complete agent interaction step, effectively designing an orchestration system. ParallelSearch trains a model to decompose the query and apply search for each. Similarly, GlobalRAG decomposes questions into subgoals. An important aspect of training deep search agents is credit assignment, i.e., assigning rewards to reasoning steps that contribute to the final answer generation. To mitigate reward hacking from outcome-only supervision, process reward models shifted to intermediate signals to guide the model through the reasoning trace. Frameworks such as RAG-Gym and ReasonRAG explicitly train a reward model with human feedback or LLM judges to become better selectors of generated reasoning traces. Unlike previous work on PRMs, SubSearch directly optimizes the generator using intrinsic process rewards, where a process reward is intrinsic if it is derived only from the model's state, such as semantic coverage, rather than from an external annotator, thus eliminating the need for additional resources and moving towards autonomous information-intensive reasoning. We have proposed SubSearch, a deep search agent that incentivizes robust step-by-step reasoning through intermediate reward signals at the subquery and decomposition level. Through query decomposition and by rewarding each query split, alongside how answerable each subquery is, SubSearch effectively learns to aggregate over a well-curated query reasoning tree. Experimental results show that SubSearch outperforms other SFT-Free methods on benchmarks such as NQ, TriviaQA, HotpotQA and 2wiki. While SubSearch integrates intrinsic process signals in the form of subquery-dependent rewards, it also adds computational complexity. Future work should explore how to make intrinsic intermediate reward calculation more efficient. Moreover, the added answerability reward is conditioned on the quality of the retriever, while only optimizing the reasoning agent and not the search engine. We aim to study the possibility of optimizing both the generator and retriever, with specialized signals for each module of the pipeline.
