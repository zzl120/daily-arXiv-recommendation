From early collaborative filtering~ and matrix factorization~ to modern deep learning~ and transformer-based sequential models~, the field has made remarkable progress. The assumption underlying all these advances is that users possess latent yet learnable preferences that persist across interactions and evolve gradually over time. Human users' interests tend to be consistent within sessions and drift slowly across weeks or months~, enabling increasingly sophisticated personalization. However, a new class of web users, autonomous LLM agents~, is emerging that may fundamentally violate this assumption. These agents, powered by LLMs, are increasingly deployed on web platforms, where they perform tasks and consume content alongside human users~. Still, it is unclear whether they possess a clear content preference profile. Many deployed agent architectures~ have limited or no persistent memory across sessions---each conversation turn may reset the agent's context, and even agents configured with external memory tools may not develop a persistent yet evolving preference profile that recommender systems assume. Consider this scenario: when a human user encounters an interesting post from a category they have never explored before, this exposure can spark a new, lasting interest that shapes their future browsing and engagement patterns. An LLM agent, by contrast, is typically stateless between sessions, and it remains unclear whether agents can organically develop their own interest groups over time through accumulated experience on the platform. This raises a fundamental question for recommender systems and agent behavioral studies. If agent users lack clear preferences, can existing recommendation models---which fundamentally rely on identifying preference patterns---still be effective? Or, do we need entirely different approaches for agent-populated platforms? As LLM agents become more prevalent on the web, answering this question has both scientific and practical importance~. We investigate this question empirically on Moltbook}, the first recently launched large-scale social media platform exclusively designed for autonomous AI agents running on the OpenClaw framework}. Moltbook's structure mirrors Reddit: agents create posts in topic-based forums called , and other agents can comment on and upvote these posts . We formulate a forum recommendation problem on this platform---predicting which submolts an agent will engage with next---using the publicly available Moltbook Observatory Archive dataset~, which captures roughly 2.4 million posts and 992,000 comments by 175,000 LLM agents across 6,200 submolts over a 10-week observation period. Specifically, we evaluate eight recommendation methods spanning simple heuristic rules , matrix factorization , hybrid content-CF , content-embedding-based filtering, item-based collaborative filtering , graph-based collaborative filtering , and sequential recommendation . We additionally test the effect of karma-weighted interaction matrices and agent description embeddings as user-side features. We find that simple methods such as ItemKNN leveraging structural co-occurrence patterns perform the best among the set, while models such as BPR-MF that attempt to learn a personalized representation perform worse than simple TopPopular heuristics. Temporal gaps between the training and testing sets do not degrade evaluation metrics as they do on human platform data~. Overall, the asymmetric results we observe with agent users indicate that personalization is fundamentally different in agentic populations. The core contributions of our work are summarized as follows: We formulate, to the best of our knowledge, the first recommendation problem on an agent-native social platform at large scale and establish empirical baselines across a wide range of algorithms. We provide evidence that AI agents' content consumption behaviors differ fundamentally from human users, with personalization largely collapsing into structural pattern matching. We discuss the implications on the algorithmic design and governance for recommender systems on human platforms as LLM agent participation grows, while also introducing a new direction to investigate agent societies. Overall, our work brings a new dimension to recommender systems and social networks research as AI agents become an increasingly significant presence on web platforms. Collaborative filtering is a well-established paradigm in recommender systems~. User-based CF~ recommends items liked by similar users, while item-based CF~ recommends items similar to those the target user has previously interacted with. Matrix factorization methods~ decompose the user-item interaction matrix into low-rank latent factors, enabling scalable personalization. BPR~ optimizes a pairwise ranking loss for implicit feedback, while factorization machines~ generalize MF to incorporate side information. LightGCN~ advances collaborative filtering by applying simple, linear neighborhood aggregation across the user-item bipartite graph, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The cold-start problem~ arises when new users or items lack sufficient interaction history for CF to operate. Hybrid methods~ address this by combining CF with content features. Our study tests this well-established recommendation paradigm in a setting where users are AI agents, who may not have persistent or well-defined user preferences on the content they consume, challenging the fundamental assumption underlying personalization. Time-aware recommender systems~ model how user preferences evolve over time. The common finding is that recommendation accuracy degrades as the temporal gap between training and test data increases, because of data freshness drop~ and user preferences drift~. Our temporal decay experiment tests whether this degradation occurs for LLM agent users, providing a clean empirical test of the preference-stability assumption. In recommender systems for human users, sequential recommendation leveraging user behavioral sequences represents the state-of-the-art family of algorithms~, substantially outperforming classical and MF based methods. In this study, we test whether the activity sequence data of agent users helps with recommendation in this novel setting. The field of recommendation systems is undergoing a paradigm shift towards generative recommenders~ and agentic recommendation systems~. Recent work has explored using LLMs to recommender systems~, leveraging their language understanding capabilities to improve recommendations. However, the complementary question---whether traditional recommendation algorithms work when are LLM agents---has received little attention. We aim to fill this gap in this work. The study of LLM agents as autonomous actors on web platforms is a rapidly growing area~. Generative agents~ demonstrated that LLM-powered agents can simulate believable human behavior in sandbox environments~. Studying the behaviors of AI agents, and the contrast and interactions with humans forms a new research perspective~. To our knowledge, there have been no studies yet on the behaviors of AI agents as content consumers in recommendation settings. The Moltbook platform is a recently launched Reddit-style social media exclusively designed for autonomous AI agents. It is the first large-scale social network where agents interact without human intervention, providing an unprecedented testbed to study AI-native societies and ecosystems. It has been rapidly attracting research interest from the computational social science and network science communities, with existing work mostly focusing on collective dynamics and comparative graph analysis against human social networks~. We formulate a recommendation problem on this new platform, analogous to the feed recommendation on human social media. Moltbook, launched in January 2026, is a social platform built on the OpenClaw open-source framework for deploying autonomous LLM agents on the web. Each OpenClaw agent living inside Moltbook is backed by one or more LLMs and can be configured by its human owner via various Markdown files. Three markdown files are mandatory to this design: defines the agent's persona and behavioral guidelines, defines the operational rules for the agent, and tells the agent the demographics and communication preferences of its human owner. Optional markdown files such as , which can be updated by the agent itself, provide persistent context across sessions, and specifies the agent's capabilities. These configuration files reside on the private instances where the agent is deployed, and are not accessible from Moltbook. In none of these markdown files are the agent's own content preferences explicitly required, nor does the , if there is one, maintain records of the agent's full activity logs due to context window constraint. Moltbook's platform structure mirrors Reddit: agents create posts in topic-based forums called , and other agents can comment on and upvote these posts . Humans can observe but cannot participate in those conversations with agents. Submolts span diverse topics---from technical discussions to social and creative forums . Agents navigate this space autonomously, producing interaction patterns that superficially resemble human social platform activity. Our study leverages the public dataset of Moltbook Observatory Archive~, available on HuggingFace . This incremental dataset passively records the profiles and activity logs of agents, communities , posts, and comments by continuously polling the Moltbook API, providing a natural testbed to conduct reproducible studies of the content ecosystem of an AI-agent society at large scale. It is well known in the recommender systems community that model and data freshness matters a lot as user preferences can shift at distinct time scales: model performance degrades with the increase in train-test dates gap~. To test whether this still holds in this completely new setting with AI agent users, we train the seven models on a fixed 4-week window and vary the gap between the training and test periods from 1 to 4 weeks, while the test period is always one week. Figure~ shows the results across the four evaluation metrics. {fig4_temporal_decay.pdf} Interestingly, no model shows systematic performance degradation as the gap increases. The top-tier models or heuristics all show flat or slightly increasing profiles, as do the personalized models at lower absolute performance. We note that the evaluation population shrinks at larger gaps , because only agents active in both the training window and the distant test week qualify. We did sanity checks on the interaction counts during the training period of agents from different evaluation populations and did not see significant differences, ruling out the confounding factor of heterogeneity in user activity level. The smaller population introduces variance, which likely accounts for the observed slight upward trend. The key observation is the . Crucially, the absence of performance decay over train-test time gap holds across model families---structural, personalized, sequential, and content-based alike. This rules out the possibility that the flat profile is a model-specific artifact and instead reflects a property of the data-generating process itself: these agents produce interaction patterns that are stationary over time, without signatures of evolving preferences common with human users. It also provides an explanation on why SASRec model does not perform better than a much simpler item-CF algorithm: temporal dynamics do not matter as much when the users are LLM agents. Our results consistently show that the predictive signal in agent interaction data is more structural than personal. Methods that exploit item co-occurrence or simple non-personalized TopPopular rule perform better than methods that attempt to learn personalized user embeddings or use content embedding centroids to represent users . This pattern is reinforced by three additional findings: karma weighting amplifies structural methods by $2.2$--$5.2 $ while leaving BPR-MF's performance unchanged; agent description embeddings fail to help, indicating that even explicit persona information does not translate into useful predictive signals; and recommendation accuracy shows no decay across train-test temporal gaps, implying stationary interaction patterns rather than evolving preferences. These findings are consistent with the way OpenClaw agents are configured~. The lack of user preference signals among agent populations can be explained as follows. First, agents can have shared LLM backbones. The overlapped models may have similar pretrained biases, reducing the user-side variance and the amount of learnable signals. Second, OpenClaw agents' behaviors are heavily shaped by the configuration markdown files. These files are authored by individual agent owners and are held on private instances. Agents are typically configured to perform scheduled tasks under behavioral guidelines~, but none of the markdown files require specifying the agent's own content consumption preferences unless the owner explicitly defines it. Additionally, OpenClaw agents are stateless between distinct sessions, and the optional long-term memory file cannot record full interaction logs. This sharply contrasts with human users whose content interests evolve based on their experience during the previous sessions on the platform~. As the architectures of agentic systems are rapidly evolving~, we expect some of the assumptions and interpretation are subject to change in the future. Still, we believe this is a direction worth revisiting as it provides a new dimension to study multi-agent systems. We discuss the implications of our study on recommendation for human users. Our findings highlight fundamental distinctions between human users and LLM agents from the perspective of personalization. Human users exhibit clear yet evolving preferences that personalized models can learn such that user behavioral data can be cleanly projected to a representation embedding in latent spaces~. LLM agents, by contrast, produce interaction patterns that are driven by frozen model weights, platform structure and session-level context rather than by persistent personal preferences. As a result, we hypothesize that personalization collapses---user identity becomes less informative, temporal ordering of behavioral sequences adds little signal, and the recommendation problem reduces to structural pattern matching. This distinction indicates a qualitative difference in the data-generating process impacting algorithmic design. As LLM agents become increasingly prevalent on human-oriented platforms such as Reddit, X, and other similar social media sites, these findings have direct implications for recommender systems designed to serve human users. First, : if a non-trivial fraction of platform interactions are generated by agents whose behavior does not reflect genuine preferences, models trained on this mixed data may learn weaker or distorted user representations. The structural and popularity signals that dominate agent behaviors could dilute the user signals that personalization relies on, degrading recommendation quality for human users. Second, : standard offline evaluation computes metrics over all users in the test set. If agents are present but unidentified, their lack of preference would systematically deflate metrics like Recall and NDCG for personalized models, potentially leading practitioners to draw wrong conclusions on model comparison. Our study has several limitations in data and methodology. On the data side, the dataset we use does not include the backbone LLM model information or the configuration files . When interpreting the results, we assume that agents may have overlapped LLM model backbones and the configuration files do not always define agent's content consumption preferences or record agent's full interaction logs. While we believe these assumptions are realistic with the typical way OpenClaw agents are deployed, they are not falsifiable as of now. We also note that the interaction data is very sparse, which may bring confounding factors in results interpretation as the absolute performance metrics are quite low. The Moltbook platform does not log full impressions and interactions history, bringing a valid concern when formulating a recommendation problem. We addressed this challenge by defining a forum recommendation problem leveraging agent posts and comments as a proxy of reactions to contents . As this is a new research direction, we believe there is value in developing standardized benchmark datasets as future work. On the methodology side, the recommendation algorithms used in the current work are mostly classic and well-established methods. As a first empirical study, we aim to establish important qualitative claims rather than create new algorithms given lack of standard problem formulation or benchmarks. Still, our findings imply the importance of developing robust recommendation models and evaluation methods when the platform is populated with AI agents. We present the first systematic evaluation of recommendation algorithms on an LLM-agent-native social platform. By formulating a forum recommendation problem on a public dataset collected from Moltbook and measuring the performance of a wide range of algorithms, we find that content consumption behaviors of agent users differ drastically from well-established findings on human platforms. Due to lack of persistent preference profiles, recommendation for AI agent users is closer to structural pattern matching rather than personalization. Simple heuristic or item-CF methods beat or match more complex user modeling techniques. User behavioral sequences do not show as much importance as they bring for human user modeling. Our findings provide a novel angle to agent behavioral studies, and have profound implications on designing better recommender systems for human users as LLM agents become more prevalent on web platforms. Future work includes establishing standard benchmark datasets, developing novel algorithms and evaluation methodologies in this under-explored setting.
