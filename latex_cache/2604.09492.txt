 Large Language Models have been widely used in reranking. Computational overhead and large context lengths remain a challenging issue for LLM rerankers. Efficient reranking usually involves selecting a subset of the ranked list from the first stage, known as ranked list truncation . The truncated list is processed further by a reranker. For LLM rerankers, the ranked list is often partitioned and processed sequentially in batches to reduce the context length. Both these steps involve hyperparameters and topic-agnostic heuristics. Recently, LLMs have been shown to be effective for relevance judgment. Equivalently, we propose that LLMs can be used to generate reference documents that can act as a pivot between relevant and non-relevant documents in a ranked list. We propose methods to use these generated reference documents for RLT as well as for efficient listwise reranking. While reranking, we process the ranked list in either parallel batches of non-overlapping windows or overlapping windows with adaptive strides, improving the existing fixed stride setup. The generated reference documents are also shown to improve existing efficient listwise reranking frameworks. Experiments on TREC Deep Learning benchmarks show that our approach outperforms existing RLT-based approaches. These methods determine the optimal cut-off depth for a ranked list before reranking to balance effectiveness against efficiency. Lien et al. explored efficient top-$k$ processing by dynamically pruning candidates unlikely to affect the final evaluation metric. For neural retrieval, RLT has evolved from heuristic score-based thresholding to learned models. Bahri et al. introduced , a transformer-based model that predicts query-specific cut-offs by analyzing the score distribution of the top retrieved documents. Similarly, Wu et al. proposed , which utilizes an attention mechanism to model the dependencies between document scores and the optimal truncation point. More recently, Meng et al. investigated RLT specifically for reranking pipelines, demonstrating that fixed-depth reranking is often suboptimal and that dynamic truncation can significantly improve the efficiency of LLM-based rerankers. The recent developments in LLM capabilities and scale, especially large context windows, have led to the emergence of zero-shot listwise reranking with generative ranking models~. Listwise objectives better capture inter-document dependencies than pointwise or pairwise methods or traditional cross-encoders~. Distilled LLMs, trained via permutation distillation on teacher rankings from zero-shot prompts, outperform much larger supervised cross-encoders like monoT5~ Despite these gains, inference efficiency remains the main challenge while deploying listwise LLM rankers at scale. Retrieval and reranking pipelines are increasingly being adopted in search systems. Managing the efficiency-effectiveness trade-off for multi-stage retrieval pipelines poses a significant challenge due to the use of effective but computationally expensive reranking models~. Reranking strategies vary significantly across models and can be broadly categorized into pointwise, pairwise, and listwise setups~. Typically, in a pointwise or pairwise setup, the first-stage retrieve candidate list is pruned before reranking~. This method of pruning before reranking in a typical ``'' setup is known as ranked list truncation and has been widely adopted in reranking pipelines~. However, recent literature shows that a fixed cut-off or score-based heuristic method is often suboptimal because they ignore query-specific relevance distributions and reranking gains~. Most existing approaches view truncation as a selection over the retrieved list, without exploiting richer semantic structure~. Additionally, there are instances where reranking a larger ranked list without truncation is beneficial to facilitate performance gains, particularly for listwise LLM rerankers~. However, the improvement in retrieval performance comes at the expense of added reranking time, which harms the overall responsiveness of retrieval pipelines, particularly in real-time applications~. Recent studies indicate that LLMs demonstrate strong capabilities in understanding semantics and query-document relationships~. In particular, LLMs have been successfully utilised for zero-shot, few-shot, and instruction-tuned relevance judgment, surpassing even human annotators in terms of consistency and completeness~. However, while research has extensively explored the capability of LLMs for relevance judgment, their potential to generate semantically controlled reference-documents within retrieval pipelines remains largely unexplored~. Our work investigates the complementary role of LLMs in multi-stage retrieval, utilizing them to generate a moderately relevant reference-document for a given query and then leveraging this document as a semantic pivot to distinguish between highly relevant and non-relevant documents in a ranked list. The idea of leveraging a semantic pivot enables designing a new RLT approach for efficient reranking in pointwise and pairwise setups that is both adaptive and model-agnostic, thereby improving efficiency without requiring explicit model training or parameter tuning. In listwise reranking, for which LLMs are now commonly used, the ranked list is often partitioned into overlapping windows and processed sequentially in batches to reduce the context length, known as the sliding window~. Using the generated pivot, we propose methods that can process the ranked list in either parallel batches of non-overlapping windows or overlapping windows with adaptive strides, improving the existing fixed stride setup. We further investigate the application of this semantic pivot in existing methods that require partitioning to facilitate efficient listwise reranking. The primary contributions of this work are summarized as follows: We leverage the generation capability of LLMs to generate a semantically controlled pivot-document that acts as a distinguishing relevant from non-relevant documents within a ranked list. We propose PSI-Rank to utilize the generated pivot for RLT in pointwise and pairwise setups, wherein the retriever itself determines the pivot's position in the ranked list. This approach requires only a single model inference call for scoring the pivot, thereby improving reranking efficiency without exhaustive scoring. In listwise reranking, we incorporate the semantic pivot to enable document propagation across non-overlapping windows , facilitating parallel batch processing and substantially reducing inference latency. We extend the conventional sliding window strategy by introducing an mechanism . The pivot dynamically estimates relevance density across reranking windows to balance contextual completeness and computational efficiency. We adapt the TD-Part algorithm for efficient listwise reranking and demonstrate that substituting the conventional top-document anchor with our LLM-generated pivot yields improved relevance consistency and ranking stability . Our experiments across in-domain and out-of-domain benchmarks demonstrate that our truncation mechanism outperforms existing RLT-based approaches, while requiring no task-specific training or sensitive hyperparameter tuning.
