Sequential recommendation presents non-trivial challenges due to the diversity of user behaviors, the complexity of item spaces, and the multi-faceted nature of user–item interactions . Furthermore, a single user sequence can reflect various patterns—such as short-term interests, long-term preferences, or shifts in user tastes—making it difficult for a single model to capture all relevant signals . These challenges highlight the need for models that can interpret sequences from multiple perspectives. Instead of relying on a single model to capture the full range of behavioral patterns, a promising direction is to combine multiple models, each focusing on complementary aspects of the data. This is the core idea behind ensemble methods , which have shown strong performance in various domains by capturing diverse and complementary patterns. In this light, ensemble learning is particularly well-suited for sequential recommendation, where user intent and item semantics vary widely. Recently, several ensemble-based methods have been proposed to capture the diverse dynamics embedded within user sequences . These approaches commonly train multiple networks from scratch in parallel, with each network encouraged to specialize in complementary behavioral patterns. Inspired by recent advancements in computer vision field , many of these methods further incorporate mutual knowledge transfer among networks to enhance collective learning. For example, EMKD performs both representation-level contrastive learning and logit-level knowledge distillation, allowing individual networks to align their outputs while maintaining diversity in internal representations. Such strategies aim to promote inter-network agreement and help each network effectively navigate the extremely large parameter search space inherent to deep neural networks, enabling more efficient optimization than training networks independently. Despite their effectiveness, recent ensemble-based methods suffer from two major limitations: { computational inefficiency} and { training instability}. First, employing multiple networks significantly increases computational costs, including parameters, memory footprint, and training time. In particular, typical ensemble prediction requires forwarding each network independently and aggregating their output, resulting in training and inference costs that are several times higher than those of a single network. This makes such methods impractical for real-time services. Second, training all networks from scratch with mutual knowledge transfer often leads to unstable optimization, especially in the early stages when none of the networks has reliable prior knowledge , ultimately limiting performance. This problem can be substantially aggravated in sequential recommendation, where data tends to be sparse and noisy . In our experiments, existing ensemble methods show large training fluctuations and require 2.06 to 2.94 times longer to converge than standard model . To address the inefficiency and instability of ensemble-based sequential recommendation methods, we focus on two key insights informed by previous literature and our empirical study. First, { different sub-modules within a single network can capture distinct patterns}. Prior works commonly observe that as layers deepen, neural representations become increasingly abstract and discriminative. A similar phenomenon can be expected in sequential recommendation, where, for instance, the embedding module captures static item semantics, while early layers of the sequence encoder reflect localized and item-specific signals, and deeper layers encode temporally smoothed representations of user preferences. This structural separation suggests that even a single network can internally generate diverse representations by interpreting sequences from multiple perspectives. Building on this, our key idea is to simulate ensemble-like behavior by leveraging an exponential number of sub-module combinations constructed from just two networks--without explicitly constructing multiple models. Second, { pretraining and freezing just one network of an ensemble can substantially improve training stability}. We empirically observe that even a single pretrained network—trained on the same data and in the same manner, except without mutual knowledge transfer—can provide effective prior knowledge that guides the training of the remaining learnable networks, resulting in more stable and effective optimization. In , we conduct experiments with SASRec under three settings: Single , Ensemble-Scratch , and Ensemble-Guide . Both networks in Ensemble-Scratch exhibit higher fluctuations and slower convergence than Single, whereas the learnable network in Ensemble-Guide converges more quickly and stably. Notably, while each network in Ensemble-Scratch underperforms compared to Single—indicating that noisy supervision between untrained models can hinder ensemble learning—the learnable network in Ensemble-Guide outperforms Single by leveraging guidance from the frozen network. This finding aligns with observations in self-distillation, where a pretrained teacher improves both training stability and final performance of an identical student . Building on these insights, we propose { F}rozen and { L}earnable networks with { A}ligned { M}odular { E}nsemble , a novel framework that condenses ensemble-level diversity into a single network for efficient sequential recommendation. FLAME consists of two networks with identical architecture and capacity: one is pretrained and frozen, and the other is learned from scratch. Each network comprises $M$ sub-modules, and each input is forwarded through a combination of frozen and learnable sub-modules--yielding $2^M$ unique representations. These representations are aligned into a unified semantic space via contrastive learning, utilizing the frozen network as a stable semantic anchor. Remarkably, this design allows FLAME to simulate the diversity of ensembling an exponentially large number of networks using just two, enabling the learnable network to capture complementary patterns without instability. Consequently, inference relies solely on the learnable network alone, achieving ensemble-level performance with the efficiency of a single network. Thus, FLAME presents a practical and robust alternative to conventional ensemble methods. Before detailing the proposed FLAME framework, we first elaborate on its two foundational designs-- and --which are designed to mitigate the computational inefficiency and training instability inherent in conventional ensemble approaches. Modular ensemble, depicted in b, addresses the computational inefficiency of conventional ensemble methods while preserving, or even enhancing, representational diversity. Unlike conventional ensemble, which generates representations by forwarding the input through an entire network as a whole, modular ensemble flexibly chooses the sub-module to use at each stage from any of the networks. This design allows for a combinatorial expansion of representation paths without increasing the number of complete networks. For example, with three networks each split into three sub-modules, the modular ensemble produces $3^3 = 27$ unique representations, compared to only 3 in a conventional setup. Conversely, even a small number of networks can simulate the effect of an 8-network conventional ensemble through $2^3 = 8$ modular combinations, significantly improving efficiency. Another critical challenge of conventional ensemble training, as observed in , is optimization instability, presumably stemming from noisy supervision exchanged among randomly initialized networks. This issue can be alleviated by pretraining and freezing some of the networks in ensemble. The frozen networks, imbued with prior knowledge, serves as a stable reference during training, guiding the rest learnable networks toward faster and more reliable convergence. In addition to stabilizing, this setup also improves the standalone quality of the learnable networks through mutual enhancement between networks . This strategy integrates particularly well with modular ensemble, where fine-grained knowledge exchange occurs structurally at the sub-module level.
