Conversational recommender systems aim to support recommendation through interactive natural language exchanges, where the system gradually elicits user preferences and provides personalized suggestions~. Existing CRS methods are commonly grouped into attribute-based and generation-based paradigms~. Attribute-based systems usually guide conversations through predefined slots or templates, which makes their behavior easier to control but limits conversational flexibility. Generation-based systems relax these constraints and produce more natural responses~, yet traditional generation models often struggle with long-context understanding, preference tracking, and robust dialogue planning. Recent work has explored LLMs as a new foundation for CRSs. ZSCRS~ studies the zero-shot recommendation ability of LLMs in conversational settings, while ChatRec~ and InteRecAgent~ combine LLMs with external recommendation models or tools to improve interactive recommendation. To further strengthen dialogue management, MACRS~ introduces a multi-agent framework for strategic planning in multi-turn recommendation. These studies show the potential of LLM-based CRSs, but they also make evaluation more challenging: system responses become more open-ended, dialogue trajectories diverge quickly, and performance depends on both recommendation quality and conversational behavior. Therefore, reliable user simulation and fine-grained evaluation protocols are increasingly important for assessing CRS capabilities under realistic interaction conditions. In the study of user simulation in CRS leveraging Large Language Models, existing methods can be broadly categorized into two main paradigms: single-prompt frameworks and agent-based frameworks. Early works, such as iEvaLM , MACRS , and SimpleUserSim , typically adopt the single-prompt paradigm. These approaches rely on manually designed static templates to consolidate user history, candidate items, and task instructions into a single prompt, thereby generating a simulated user response in a single pass. However, the inherent rigidity of this approach leads to several limitations. First, because the generation process is heavily constrained by a fixed template structure, the resulting dialogue flows exhibit limited diversity and often fall into predictable and repetitive patterns. Second, this one-shot generation mechanism makes fine-grained control over the response content challenging, hindering the ability to dynamically adjust the simulated user's intent or emotional state. Finally, these methods typically rely on ground-truth labels to evaluate the dialogue system, which places emphasis solely on the system’s recommendation accuracy while overlooking its conversational competence. Moreover, ground-truth data are scarce in most real-world scenarios, further constraining the applicability and scalability of such approaches. To overcome the aforementioned limitations, researchers have shifted towards more dynamic, agent-based frameworks. The LLM-driven user agent, CSHI , represents a notable attempt in this direction. It guides the agent's behavior by explicitly integrating user profiles and a fixed action space. This design aims to enhance the reliability and personalization of simulated behaviors, making the agent's decisions more grounded. Despite this advancement, CSHI still faces challenges in consistency, behavioral diversity, and persona stability in practice. Building upon CSHI, RecUserSim further expands CSHI's capabilities by augmenting its action space and modeling linguistic styles to improve the diversity of simulated user behavior. However, its core design does not escape the constraints of a predefined framework. Although its action space is more extensive, it remains fundamentally fixed and fails to capture the open-ended and unanticipated behaviors humans exhibit in real interactions. Furthermore, the post-refinement strategy employed by RecUserSim, while capable of locally optimizing response quality, sometimes compromises contextual coherence. This can disrupt the natural flow of the dialogue, thereby limiting its overall performance. In summary, both the single-prompt and the existing agent-based frameworks suffer from a common, fundamental bottleneck: a heavy reliance on manually crafted prompts and predefined action spaces. Its core objective is to replace the tedious and often intuitive process of manual prompt engineering. Existing approaches have explored various strategies in this domain. For instance, methods like APE iteratively search for better instructions in a propose-and-score paradigm, combining manually designed seed prompts with feedback from the model itself. Other approaches, such as GPS and SPRIG , integrate human feedback into the optimization loop, leveraging human preferences to guide the evolution of prompts to enhance their quality and alignment. Meanwhile, to reduce the reliance on human intervention, methods like DAPO attempt to leverage the language model's own capabilities for instruction induction, automatically inferring the optimal task instruction from a few examples. Although these methods have achieved promising results in static tasks , they face significant challenges when applied to dynamic, multi-turn user simulation scenarios. Static tasks typically have clear, fixed evaluation criteria, such as accuracy or overlap with a reference answer, whereas the quality assessment of multi-turn dialogues is considerably more complex. The core difficulty lies in the lack of an objective, quantifiable comparative standard across turns. In a simulated dialogue, a ``good'' response must not only be locally optimal for the current turn but also maintain consistency and coherence with the historical context, while steering the conversation in a meaningful direction. Existing APO methods, typically designed to optimize for single-turn interactions, struggle to capture this long-term dependency on global conversational quality. To address these issues, we draw inspiration from the work of ProTeGi , which demonstrates the effectiveness of optimizing generation strategies via pairwise preference learning in an unsupervised setting. We apply this concept to prompt optimization for dynamic multi-turn user simulation by proposing a novel approach. This approach introduces a multi-round interaction mechanism, where the model generates entire conversational snippets rather than single responses, combined with pairwise comparisons of historical outputs for evaluation. Specifically, the system generates two conversational trajectories driven by different candidate prompts, and then a model or a human annotator determines which trajectory is globally superior . This design allows the prompt refinement process to move beyond a reliance on hard-to-obtain ground-truth labels, instead leveraging relative preferences for iterative optimization. Consequently, our method not only effectively improves prompt quality for dynamic interaction tasks but also significantly reduces supervision costs, offering a viable path for optimizing user simulators in complex scenarios. We then shift our focus to robustness testing—a comparatively less explored but critical area—to define its scope and outline corresponding evaluation approaches. CRS robustness refers to the system's ability to maintain performance and accurately infer user intent when faced with diverse and imperfect user inputs. In practice, users often generate non-standard inputs due to spelling errors, ambiguous language, colloquial expressions, or incomplete information. A truly robust CRS must be able to effectively recognize and process such deviations to fulfill the user's request, rather than misunderstanding or failing completely. To evaluate the basic capabilities and robustness of conversational recommender systems , we introduce a BFS-based turn-level pairwise evaluation protocol. The goal is to compare systems under matched dialogue contexts rather than independently evolving conversations. By expanding dialogue states with Breadth-First Search , the protocol preserves context consistency at each comparison point, while turn-level pairwise judgments provide fine-grained signals and reduce the instability of absolute scoring. As outlined in Algorithm~, we use a tree-structured simulation process to evaluate the basic capabilities of CRSs. Unlike linear simulation, where two systems quickly enter different dialogue contexts after producing different responses, our BFS-based protocol maintains local for each pairwise comparison. The process starts from an initial user query $a_0$ and initializes a queue $$ with the corresponding dialogue history. At each iteration, the algorithm dequeues a history state $h$ and asks both target systems, $_1$ and $_2$, to generate responses $r_1$ and $r_2$ from the same history. This synchronous generation step ensures that $r_1$ and $r_2$ are evaluated under an identical context, avoiding bias caused by context divergence. The user simulator $$ then interacts with the two responses independently, producing two branches: $h r_1 a_1$ and $h r_2 a_2$. These new states are added back to the queue, allowing the evaluation to expand breadth-wise over multiple possible dialogue trajectories. A branch terminates when the simulator outputs ``END'' or when the maximum depth $$ is reached. As shown in Algorithm~, we adapt the same tree-structured protocol to evaluate CRS robustness under user-side noise. Instead of comparing two systems, this setting performs a within a CRS. We use two user simulators: a careless user $_c$, which produces noisy inputs such as typos or incomplete expressions, and a normal user $_n$, which produces clean inputs. For each dequeued history state $h$, the two simulators generate a noisy query $a_c$ and a clean query $a_n$ in parallel. The target CRS responds to them separately, producing $r_c$ and $r_n$. Since both inputs are generated from the same dialogue history, the resulting pairwise comparison isolates the effect of input noise and measures how much the CRS performance changes under perturbation. To avoid cumulative error propagation, we use a strategy during tree expansion. The noisy branch $h a_c r_c$ is used only for evaluation and is not enqueued. Instead, only the clean trajectory $h a_n r_n$ is retained for subsequent turns. This design keeps later comparisons grounded in coherent dialogue histories while still measuring the immediate effect of noisy user input at each turn. Although AdaptSim demonstrates strong performance across domains and backbones, several limitations tied to its specific design choices warrant further investigation. First, the automatic prompt optimization process relies on an LLM both to detect behavioral issues in generated dialogues and to refine the prompt accordingly. This creates a circular dependency: the quality of the optimized prompt is bounded by the meta-evaluation LLM's ability to identify subtle simulation failures, such as underspecified intent or inconsistent guidance. When the backbone LLM is weak, both the error detection and the refinement steps may degrade simultaneously, making it difficult to diagnose whether poor simulation quality stems from the prompt or the model itself. Second, the open-ended action generator selects the most probable candidate action, which favors high-frequency, prototypical user behaviors. This greedy selection may suppress rare but realistic actions---such as abrupt topic shifts or contradictory preference updates---that are important for stress-testing CRS robustness. Incorporating diversity-aware sampling or explicit coverage objectives could address this. Third, the thinking-enhanced style controller enforces stylistic consistency through a prompt-driven thinking step, without modifying the model's internal representations. In very long dialogues, the style signal may still drift as the thinking step competes with an increasingly long context. A more robust solution might involve fine-tuning or retrieval-augmented style anchoring. Finally, the BFS-based evaluation expands dialogue states breadth-first, which provides context-consistent pairwise comparisons but grows exponentially with branching factor and depth. In practice, we cap the depth at $R$ and limit branching, which may miss important late-turn failure modes. Future work could explore beam-search-style pruning or learned branch selection to improve coverage without proportional cost. However, the construction and evaluation of a CRS still face numerous challenges due to its dynamic and interactive nature~. Specifically, AdaptSim addresses the aforementioned three challenges through the following four key mechanisms: By leveraging domain-specific knowledge and historical interaction data, it automatically refines prompts to enable the simulator to generate high-quality dialogues across all domains. AdaptSim moves away from relying on a set of predefined actions and instead adopts an open-ended action generation mechanism, dynamically constructing user actions based on context, which significantly boosts the simulator's flexibility across new domains. To enhance fine-grained control over linguistic behavior of users, we adopt a ``think-then-respond'' structure in response generation. This method guides the model to think on its language style before generating a response, significantly improving stylistic consistency while maintaining contextual coherence. To achieve an unbiased and fine-grained turn-level assessment of a CRS's basic capabilities and robustness, this method combines three key elements. It leverages a Breadth-First Search to ensure contextual consistency, while the turn-level analysis provides detailed feedback, and the pairwise comparison framework guarantees objectivity. Conversational Recommender Systems enhance user experience through multi-turn interactions, yet accurately evaluating their performance remains challenging. Recently, employing Large Language Model based user simulators for evaluation becomes an effective approach. However, existing LLM-based user simulators suffer from three key limitations: Lack of Domain Adaptability: The simulators' heavy reliance on fixed prompts and predefined action spaces hinders their ability to adapt and transfer to novel application domains; Limited User Modeling Capability: The simulators lack the capacity for fine-grained user modeling, particularly in accurately replicating subtle linguistic styles and dynamic user preferences; Insufficient Evaluation Validity for Downstream CRSs: When evaluating CRSs, the existing simulators provide not only an unreasonable assessment of fundamental capabilities but also an insufficient examination of system robustness. AdaptSim offers an efficient and comprehensive framework for evaluating the fundamental capabilities and robustness of CRSs by enabling realistic behavior modeling and diverse style generation. AdaptSim leverages automatic prompt generation and optimization to reduce manual effort in domain adaptation, and introduces an open action generation mechanism to improve flexibility across domains. For response generation, we employ controlled text generation combined with a ``think-then-respond'' strategy, enabling fine-grained control over user behavior and language style. For evaluation of CRSs, AdaptSim incorporates a novel framework centered around a Breadth-First Search -based, turn-level pairwise comparison to facilitate a comprehensive and rigorous assessment of the basic capabilities and robustness of CRSs.
