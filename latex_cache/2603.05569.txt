While a promising approach is to use Large Language Models to translate natural language questions to SQL via Retrieval-Augmented Generation , adapting this approach to the medical domain is non-trivial. Standard RAG relies on single-step retrieval from a static pool of examples, which struggles with the variability and noise of medical terminology and jargon. This often leads to anti-patterns such as expanding the task demonstration pool to improve coverage, which in turn introduces noise and scalability problems. To address this, we introduce , a framework inspired by Case-Based Reasoning . It represents question-SQL pairs as reusable, abstract case templates and utilizes a two-stage retrieval process that first captures logical structure and then resolves relevant entities. Evaluated on MIMICSQL, CBR-to-SQL achieves state-of-the-art logical form accuracy and competitive execution accuracy. More importantly, it demonstrates higher sample efficiency and robustness than standard RAG approaches, particularly under data scarcity and retrieval perturbations. Electronic Health Record databases collect large volumes of patient data daily, including demographics, medical conditions, and diagnoses, which are critical for clinical decision-making and research. However, extracting insights from these databases is challenging for non-technical users, as it requires expertise in SQL and knowledge of database schemas . Traditional interfaces rely on rigid, rule-based mappings that fall short in addressing the variability of real-world queries. A promising solution is to use Large Language Models for text-to-SQL translation, converting natural language questions into executable SQL, allowing users to focus on the information they are seeking rather than the technical details. While generalist LLMs have already demonstrated strong performance on open-domain text-to-SQL benchmarks, they struggle in specialized settings due to a lack of contextual grounding . Fine-tuning can improve domain adaptation but is costly, complex, and unsuitable for dynamic environments . Retrieval-Augmented Generation has emerged as a promising alternative, using an external knowledge base of question-SQL pairs to provide the LLM with contextual demonstrations at inference time, allowing effective generalization from few-shot learning without parameter updates. Although RAG has been extensively studied in open-domain text-to-SQL , its adaptation to specialized domains like healthcare remains limited. This is largely due to the challenge of aligning noisy, variable medical jargon and even typos in human questions . For standard RAG strategies to be effective, they must retrieve examples that align with not only the logical structure of the query but also the entities referenced in the NL question. However, most RAG-based designs rely on static, narrowly scoped task demonstrations that are noisy , and their single-step retrieval mechanism requires near-exact matches to generalize effectively. One common workaround is to expand the demonstration pool to improve example diversity , but this introduces noise, redundancy, and computational overhead, ultimately limiting generalization and scalability. To address these limitations, we formulate text-to-SQL as a Case-based Reasoning problem . Building on this formulation, we propose , a retrieval-based framework inspired by the classical CBR cycle that operates in two phases. In the offline phase, we construct reusable case templates from question-SQL pairs by masking schema-specific details to expose the underlying problem pattern. During online inference, a two-step retrieval process leverages these cases: the step retrieves relevant templates to reuse their logical structures and propose a draft SQL query, which the following step revises to adapt to entities mentioned in the NL question. Even though CBR-to-SQL shares certain technical features with , namely in using a similar two-step retrieval process, our key novelty lies in the problem conceptualization. While the prior approach focuses on the specific problem of mapping medical terminology within a standard RAG framework, we argue that this issue reflects a deeper architectural limitation: single-step retrieval cannot simultaneously optimize logical structure alignment and entity mapping. By using a CBR framework that explicitly separates these aspects into clear, principled stages, we aim to address the broader problems of scalability and generalization that standard RAG faces in the medical domain. Overall, our main contributions are threefold. We propose a novel CBR formulation for medical text-to-SQL that uses masked case templates instead of static question-SQL examples to improve generalization and scalability. We present , a framework that decomposes retrieval into individual stages that optimize a focused subproblem, logical structure retrieval and entity retrieval. We empirically demonstrate that this separation enhances sample efficiency, interpretability, and robustness using the MIMICSQL benchmark. We propose a more challenging evaluation setup for generalization robustness of retrieval-based text-to-SQL models, including an setting to evaluate performance under limited knowledge availability, and a metric to quantify sensitivity to retrieved examples. Traditional text-to-SQL methods primarily rely on training specialized neural network architectures from scratch. They focus on addressing primitive challenges such as ensuring basic SQL syntax using reinforcement learning or syntax-aware decoding . In the medical domain, research focuses on introducing mechanisms to handle domain-specific and ambiguous medical terminology, such as attentive copying to align entity references in the output SQL with the schema , or encoder-as-decoder architectures to attend to entities from input questions during decoding . Recent research has increasingly favored utilizing the pre-trained capabilities of LLMs for text-to-SQL tasks via transfer learning methods. With their strong natural language understanding, LLMs can be adapted to complex domains such as healthcare with simple fine-tuning . Since many LLMs in this area were at an early stage and often lack pre-training on coding tasks, many studies also focus on developing specialized decoding modules for LLMs during fine-tuning to enhance SQL syntactic correctness . As modern LLMs become more capable from pretraining on coding tasks, they can be adapted to text-to-SQL using prompt-based approaches such as RAG. Research in this field follows two parallel tracks: one is , focusing on developing more advanced context retrieval strategies , the other is , aiming to enhance context representation through techniques like knowledge graphs . In the medical domain, however, retrieval-based models remain relatively unexplored. The first module, , transforms static question-SQL pairs into abstract case templates by masking away schema-specific entities. This passive stage functions as an offline data ingestion module, building a knowledge base of reusable cases for future questions. During inference, then retrieves similar cases from the constructed knowledge base. It first applies the same masking procedure to the input question to focus on retrieving structural patterns. Based on the retrieved cases, it then generates a tentative SQL template that captures the logical structure and semantic intent, while allowing a margin of error for schema-specific details. Finally, grounds the SQL template in the target schema by fixing these entity details. It utilizes a lookup table to find the correct schema-specific entities, which an LLM agent then applies to the SQL template to produce the final, executable SQL query. We utilize a masking procedure to transform question-SQL pairs into abstract case templates. This technique was established in previous literature as a query abstraction strategy to remove noisy information pre-retrieval . The process begins by using an LLM with function calling to perform entity tagging on NL questions, extracting text spans and assigning them to a set of general categories , avoiding the need for a specialized tagging model. These entities are then replaced with their respective category tags in both the NL question and SQL query, creating respective masked templates. This abstraction strips away noisy details, supporting better matching for NL questions based on structural similarity and allowing the reuse of SQL templates across different problems. Although LLM-based entity tagging may introduce hallucinations due to limited domain knowledge, the goal of this stage is not perfect labeling, but rather replacing noisy details with general semantic categories to highlight the underlying problem structure. Therefore, even approximate masking can improve retrieval. For example, as illustrated in , a query mentioning a procedure can still be matched with a case labeled with a diagnosis-type entity because their underlying problem patterns are aligned. We then ingest the masked question–SQL templates into a vector database, with dense embeddings generated from the masked NL questions as their index. A generalist embedding model is utilized for this task, since masking already removes specialized medical keywords, leaving only general language in the NL queries. Case retrieval is formulated as a Dense Passage Retrieval problem , where the goal is to find semantically similar passages by comparing their learned vector representations. For a new input question, we first apply the same masking procedure described in . We then retrieve the top-$k$ most relevant cases from the knowledge base via nearest neighbor search, using cosine similarity between the masked query and indexed NL templates. Once relevant cases are obtained, the linked masked SQL queries are reused to construct a draft SQL template for the input question. This step is performed by an LLM, which leverages the retrieved examples to generalize via few-shot learning. Because the reused solutions are masked, the LLM only receives contextual signals about the query's logical structure. This allows the model to prioritize resolving SQL structure but requires it to predict specific entity values and their associated elements directly from the question, which may be incorrect. To flag these predictions, the LLM inserts them as placeholder tokens into the SQL template in a special format , where is the predicted components and is the entity's semantic category . An example of the resulting template is given in . This creates a parameterized template that highlights potentially incorrect references that require disambiguation, allowing the next stage, Source Discovery, to revise accordingly. We construct an entity lookup table to translate free-form natural language entity references into potential schema-specific values, functioning as a dictionary of ``synonyms'' to support disambiguation. It is built by extracting unique values and their schema locations from the EHR database. These values are then ingested into a vector store where each entity is indexed by a dense vector representation from a specialized medical encoding model, with its location linked as metadata. Including every unique value in the lookup table would be impractical, so a targeted subset of entities is curated. We first identify general concepts that frequently appear as main subjects in NL questions by inspecting entity categories from offline entity tagging . Then, we select the schema columns corresponding to these concepts, targeting semantically rich, name-driven entity types that are often vaguely mentioned in questions and require disambiguation. The detailed process is described in . While this process is semi-manual, it remains simple and reproducible: the LLM tagging process already systematically identifies frequently queried concepts, allowing developers to simply select the relevant columns and extract their values. For each entity prediction flagged in , we identify its schema-specific matches in the lookup table using a two-step process. To narrow the candidate space to conceptually relevant entities, we first perform a semantic search over the lookup table using a medical embedding model and cosine similarity. This captures synonyms and paraphrases while prioritizing recall by retrieving the top 100 candidates per entity. Next, a re-ranking step is applied to improve precision by scoring candidates based on their Levenshtein distance to the masked entity, prioritizing close syntactic matches. To maintain contextual clarity and minimize noise in the later stage, the final candidate list is restricted to the top 5 matches per entity. The use of the re-ranking step is a task-specific design choice. In the dataset considered for this study, user queries often contain minor spelling errors or small deviations from actual entity names, making exact syntactical matches more valuable. In other scenarios, such as when users provide imprecise descriptions of symptoms without schema knowledge, a purely semantic retrieval approach may be more appropriate. For example, a query mentioning “stomachache” should be matched with “abdominal pain” rather than “headache,” even though the latter may have a closer Levenshtein distance. Once potential entity matches are located, an LLM selects the correct candidate and fills them into the parameterized SQL template. The model is provided with contexts from the input question, database schema, and candidate entities to allow context-aware disambiguation, unlike naive strategies that insert the top-ranked candidate into slots by default. For example, if an entity has similar matches in both the PROCEDURE and DIAGNOSIS tables, the LLM can use contextual signals from the question to determine the correct entity type and table placement. The final output is a fully executable SQL query whose logical intent aligns with the question and whose entity values are grounded in the target schema. We conduct experiments using the clinical text-to-SQL dataset MIMICSQL and the underlying EHR database MIMIC-III . MIMICSQL contains 10,000 machine-generated question-SQL pairs that were modified by human annotators to introduce real-world clinical challenges like ambiguous terminology references, abbreviations, and typos. Further statistics of this dataset are provided in . The dataset is partitioned into training, validation, and test sets in an 8:1:1 ratio. We use the training split to build our knowledge base and the test split for evaluation. We construct a environment that includes the original training set of 8,000 question–SQL pairs. This environment provides high coverage for retrieval models but also introduces noise from overlapping examples. This creates a testbed to benchmark our CBR framework against standard RAG in a dense, high-resource setting, evaluating their ability to exploit relevant patterns among noisy distractors. Furthermore, this environment facilitates direct comparison with prior work that used complete MIMICSQL data for training and evaluation. To stress-test model generalization robustness and sample efficiency, we introduce a more challenging environment called . This setting creates data scarcity conditions, where each remaining case is nearly structurally unique, preventing models from relying on diverse task demonstrations as in CDB. While prior approaches often simulate incomplete data using global random dropping , which introduces training pattern inconsistencies and requires multiple runs to stabilize, our method constructs a minimal yet representative dataset. The IDB environment is created from the MIMICSQL training set using a two-step process: SQL masking to reveal the underlying problem pattern, and clustering to aggressively reduce these structures to a nearly unique set. This approach allows retrieval-based systems to operate in a limited data setting while preserving the core problem patterns necessary for generalization. First, we apply the masking procedure described in to remove entity values from SQL queries, resulting in abstract skeletons that retain column names, operators, and clause structures. These masked SQL queries are then encoded using a generalist embedding model and clustered into groups, with each group representing a distinct problem pattern. From each cluster, we retain a single random sample, while all outliers are preserved. Overall, this reduces the original 8,000 training examples to a set of 774 data points, capturing the most representative cases. Using the same underlying LLM, our experiments demonstrate that CBR-to-SQL's performance advantage over RAG-to-SQL stems from its architectural design. While RAG utilizes single-step retrieval, CBR-to-SQL decomposes the problems of retrieving logical structures and relevant entities into two explicit stages. Each stage can therefore focus on optimizing a specific reasoning sub-problem. Moreover, these two components draw from independent data sources: logical reasoning learns from the knowledge base, while entity grounding is linked to the EHR database. This modularity makes the system more adaptable, as the logical templates can be curated and improved independently of the evolving database schema, leading to more efficient long-term maintenance. Further ablation studies in confirm the important role of each component. When Template Construction is replaced with standard RAG retrieval, semantic alignment with NL questions decreases. Similarly, removing Source Discovery leads to substantial drops in entity grounding accuracy. These results validate that each component fulfills its intended role within the CBR-to-SQL framework. Our experiments show that CBR-to-SQL achieves higher sample efficiency than standard RAG. It not only generalizes more effectively from limited example diversity in sparse data environments but also exploits the abundance of data more efficiently in dense settings . Furthermore, in top-$k$ ablation tests , CBR-to-SQL consistently outperforms RAG-to-SQL across all values of $k$ and exhibits a more graceful degradation as $k$ decreases. These results suggest that CBR formulations lead to more effective knowledge extraction from task demonstrations, providing a practical advantage for real-world EHR deployments where annotated SQL queries can be scarce. CBR-to-SQL's multi-stage architecture provides interpretability into the reasoning process. With RAG's single-step nature, it can be unclear to diagnose how the retrieval process fails to identify correct candidates. In contrast, by separating the tasks of structure retrieval and entity resolution, the stages are more transparent and their failures can be easily traceable, as evidenced in the error analysis in . This transparency allows explainable reasoning, precise debugging, and targeted improvements, which are important features in high-stakes domains like healthcare. From the efficiency study in , the trade-off of higher performance is higher computational cost, as using CBR-to-SQL yields more time and token usage compared to RAG-to-SQL. However, the marginal cost analysis in shows that these increases are modest compared to the gains in $Acc_{LF}$ and especially $Acc_{EX}$. More importantly, this calculation omits the hidden long-term costs of using RAG to match the performance of CBR-to-SQL, such as maintaining a larger knowledge base and recovering from more errors. Furthermore, in practical healthcare settings, the advantages of accuracy, robustness, and scalability of CBR-to-SQL can outweigh its additional cost. Future work should extend CBR-to-SQL to other domains, with open-domain benchmarks like Spider or WikiSQL , to evaluate its ability to handle more challenging SQL problems and a diverse range of entities. In addition, the continual learning aspect of CBR is also an interesting research direction, particularly in incorporating both successful and failed cases into the knowledge base. Finally, we encourage future work to adopt CBR principles where applicable, moving from generic RAG to modular, transparent, and data-centric architectures. This study has several limitations. First, many system components are not yet fully optimized. For instance, qualitative error analysis in shows that entity tagging errors frequently propagate to downstream modules. Furthermore, the simplistic lookup table design in Source Discovery could be replaced with a more advanced medical terminology search system. Second, computational constraints limited our experiments to a proof-of-concept setup, preventing extensive exploration of alternative LLMs, hyperparameters, and advanced techniques like query decomposition and self-consistency mechanisms. Finally, evaluation was confined to a single dataset. While the two experimental setups provide meaningful insights, they cannot validate cross-dataset generalization, which introduces the risk of overfitting. As illustrated in , CBR is conceptually described in a 4-stage cycle : REtrieve, REuse, REvise, and REtain. The process is guided by a similarity metric to assess how closely a past case aligns with the new problem, allowing the retrieval of relevant problem-solving patterns. Once relevant cases are obtained, their solutions are , often requiring further to adapt to the existing problem. Finally, the knowledge base is enriched by the solved cases for future reference.
