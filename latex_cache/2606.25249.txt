While effective, this approach often applies heavy re-ranking models regardless of query complexity, resulting in high latency and wasted computational resources on simple queries. We propose Adaptive Re-Ranking, an utility-based labeling framework for cost-aware routing and present empirical evidence that per-query routing offers large potential gains but is non-trivial to learn from limited supervision. We train a routing classifier with 3 strategies: sparse retrieval , dense re-ranking , and heavy neural re-ranking . While traditional lexical models like BM25 served as the cornerstone of retrieval systems for decades due to their unparalleled efficiency, lexical models often fail to capture the semantic meanings of complex queries. The introduction of the Transformer architecture has also made it possible to use Transformers for ranking, using BERT to re-rank passages~, using sentence-embeddings for similarity checks~, and so on. More recently, powerful cross-encoder rerankers, such as BGE-reranker-v2-m3 , have significantly improved retrieval accuracy. However, these models also have some drawbacks, such as an increase in latency up to $15x$ . Modern ad-hoc passage retrieval pipelines deployed at scale in web search, enterprise search, and question-answering systems are typically structured as a two-stage process : a fast, lexical retriever followed by a computationally expensive cross-encoder. We argue that using the most effective ``heavy'' cross-encoder for every single query might result in drastically high average latency, as well as enormous computational cost, since this pipeline treats every query equally. Our analysis of training data reveals that this uniform approach is wasteful: approximately 40\ and only 11\ To address this efficiency bottleneck, we propose ---a cost-aware framework built on a simple yet effective principle: if the expected effectiveness gain from the expensive ranker is not justified for the given query, a cheaper alternative should be used instead. This principle can be used with any model combination; we use BM25, MiniLM-L6 , and BGE in our case study to observe the difference between models that vary significantly in size. Contributions of our paper include: that defines optimal strategy selection through a trade-off between retrieval effectiveness and a latency penalty, enabling dataset creation according to the desired latency penalty. that shows adaptive routing can significantly reduce latency while maintaining competitive retrieval quality, validated across diverse datasets. that routes each query to one of three efficiency-aware pipelines: Fastest execution with minimal computation using a lexical model. Balanced effectiveness and latency. Highest effectiveness at increased computation and latency cost. While we experiment with 3 classes, our framework can be adjusted to any number of classes. Our adaptive re-ranking framework offers significant practical value for resource-constrained development environments such as mobile edge computing . Recent work has ~ demonstrated that mobile devices face severe constraints in CPU, GPU, memory, and battery life. In such limited-resource environment, using heavy neural re-ranking models for all queries becomes impractical and wasteful. By dynamically routing queries, our classifier enables IR systems to achieve competitive retrieval quality while respecting computational and energy limitations. By adjusting $ $ in our utility function, speed and efficiency can be prioritized for such environments. Traditional QPP methods rely on clarity-based , robustness-based , and score-based approaches that uses corpus statistics and query term frequencies as signals. Recent advances have introduced neural approaches to QPP such as an end-to-end neural framework . The introduction of pre-trained language models further advanced the developments by fine-tuning BERT for QPP, achieving state-of-the-art performance with significantly lower latency than previous neural predictors . Recent work has also explored using LLMs for QPP by, for example, fine-tuning open-source LLMs to generate relevance judgments to estimate effectiveness~. Despite these advances, prior work found that LLMs are not reliable enough to create relevance judgments . In section , we investigate whether post-retrieval signals from BM25 that is used in QPP can predict the re-ranking process for all datasets; however, we find that they are insufficient and not generalizable for per-query routing for all datasets. Our work differs from traditional QPP: rather than predicting a performance score, we are routing a classification problem over re-ranking strategies, and we include into our novel utility-based labeling function. The retrieve-then-rerank architecture is an example of cascade ranking, where re-rankers of increasing cost and accuracy are chained so that expensive models only score a small candidate set produced by cheaper stages. Foundational work framed this as a way to manage the efficiency$-$effectiveness tradeoff . Subsequent work made the cascade explicitly cost-aware , predicted efficiency$-$effectiveness tradeoffs across stages without requiring relevance judgments . These approaches have the cascade structure fixed, therefore going through the same sequence of stages, and optimization in this comes from pruning the candidate set, not changing the path the query takes every time. Our work differs by making the re-ranking sequence a per-query decision. Our router decision is based on the given query and the expected latency cost of using a least efficient approach. A complementary line of work reduces cost at the first-stage retrieval layer rather than at re-ranking. Dynamic pruning algorithms such as WAND and Block-Max WAND skip documents that provably cannot reach the current top-$k$ threshold, substantially reducing the number of postings scored while returning the same results. Selective pruning extends this idea by varying pruning aggressiveness on a per-query basis , which is conceptually close to our own per-query adaptivity but applied to candidate generation rather than re-ranking. These methods are complementary to our router as they speed up the BM25 stage, which all paths use, therefore complementing overall latencies across all paths. Adaptive retrieval methods use learned signals to choose between sparse, dense, and hybrid pipelines . Other work claims that the first-stage ranker is the bottleneck in latency and proposes a unified prediction framework that chooses hyperparameters based on the given query . More recently, a new paradigm has been explored that is similar to mixture of experts in structure and aims to leverage a zero-shot mixture of heterogeneous retrievers—including human-like sources—that dynamically weights and fuses them per query, yielding substantially better retrieval performance across diverse information needs . Our work is similar in the way of offering a selection of retrievers, however we also consider the efficiency of the ideal reranker on a per-query basis. With the recent advances in Retrieval-Augmented Generation , new adaptive pipelines were introduced. Recent work employs a classifier to determine if RAG is necessary for a given prompt . Similarly, MBA-RAG uses reinforcement learning to optimize retrieval strategies. Other work proposes an adaptive router for different RAG paths . Our work differs by focusing specifically on the re-ranking strategies in standard IR pipelines, introducing a supervised learning approach grounded in a cost-benefit function rather than relying on LLM-generated complexity signals. Recent work also explored reducing the cross-encoder cost by terminating the computation early for queries that can be confidently scored with fewer layers . While these methods reduce cost within a single re-ranker, they still use a cross-encoder for every query, and they are not supported by every model. Our approach is complementary to this method; instead of deciding how many layers to go inside a fixed re-ranker, we decide if re-ranking is needed at all, and if so whether a light or heavy re-ranker should be used. Because the two work on different levels in the pipeline, they can complement each other, where a query routed to a heavy re-ranker could exit early within it as well, which we leave to future work. With more capable, bigger LLM's and smaller, cheaper, less capable LLMs' emerging, methods like RouteLLM propose efficient router models that select between stronger and weaker LLMs during inference. Related commercial systems apply similar routing principles in the LLM inference domain, though they focus on LLM generation. Our framework consists of a utility-based labeling that assigns each query to its optimal retrieval strategy, dataset curation, and router training, a routing pipeline that directs queries at inference time. All the data used for labeling is computed on the official BEIR training splits~ of each dataset. Test queries are held out entirely and used for only final evaluation, and we also reserve several domain-specific datasets for only final evaluation to see how the model generalizes with zero-shot evaluation. To create our classifier, we label queries based on what retrieval method provides the best performance with a reasonable latency. For this, we define the effectiveness score as the average of nDCG@10 and MRR@10: _m = {2}\,_m + {2}\,_m . This gives a value between $ $ that captures how good the ranking is for that query, ignoring latency. The nDCG provides a graded, position‑sensitive measure of overall ranking quality for each query. However, users in modern information retrieval systems often care most about how quickly they see the first relevant result, as emphasized by prior work . Therefore, we additionally use MRR, which focuses on the rank of the first relevant item. Having these two metrics helps the pipeline to be able to fit into most setups since we are unaware of the query intent at labeling time; we weight both metrics equally since they represent different intents, while nDCG reflects a recall-oriented user profile who inspects the full result, and MRR reflects a precision-oriented user profile that wants a single relevant result as quickly as possible . Thus, using both nDCG and MRR lets us capture both holistic list quality and the user’s experience of finding the first useful result. However, we do not want to choose models only by effectiveness; slower models should be penalized. While recent works explore the trade-off between retrieval accuracy and computational cost, they do not include measured true latency in decision making. To be able to train our classifier with latency included we introduce a formula that penalizes the latency instead of taking the average of all queries. We are aiming to reward queries that only use more computational resources when doing so will actually improve that query itself. By formalizing the trade-off between retrieval effectiveness and system latency into a quantitative function, we developed a pipeline that dynamically routes queries to the most cost-effective reranking strategy, ranging from skipping re-ranking entirely to a heavy cross-encoder model . Our experiments demonstrated that the ``one-size-fits-all'' paradigm, where one reranker is applied always, is computationally inefficient. We showed that a significant portion of queries benefit from lightweight models without compromising retrieval quality. Due to computational and time constraints, our training dataset might not capture a sufficiently representative variety of queries and domains, therefore our learned router may not be fully general. Future work should curate a diverse dataset that focuses on clean, high-quality multidomain datasets that enable models to learn multiple domain-specific signals, if there are any. Our model selection was affected by the time and computational constraints. We also experimented with DistilBERT as a lighter router but observed a significant drop in accuracy, suggesting that our framework benefits from the full BERT capacity. Future work should explore other architectures and models. We believe our utility function captures a good blend of effectiveness and latency of a selected model. Future work could refine the utility function, introduce new variables and more hyperparameters to adjust the weights according to personal needs.
