This has motivated a broad family of multimodal recommenders that combine user-item interactions with visual and textual item features . Recent surveys synthesize this literature from representation, modeling, and optimization perspectives . Recent work has improved the representation layer by building item-item graphs from content, denoising multimodal features, or enforcing stronger consistency across views . The common premise is simple: a better multimodal representation should yield a better ranker. Our diagnostics support a more selective view. Content evidence helps, but only within an effective range. Moderate cross-view consistency improves ranking, whereas stronger consistency suppresses recommendation-specific variation. Frequency analysis further shows a shared-vs-private split: low-frequency bands are aligned across views, while higher-frequency bands retain more ranking signal. That asymmetry matters because ranking is decided in a narrow candidate set. Once alignment becomes too strong, it blurs the local distinctions that separate plausible items from near neighbors. BRIDGE turns these observations into a behavior-guided design. The multimodal encoder defines the base candidate geometry, and co-user behavior authorizes only local residual corrections inside that candidate set. DFGE preserves frequency-aware multimodal evidence, BEN converts training-only co-user overlap into signed behavior support, and CRI applies that support only within the candidate scope. On Amazon Baby, Sports, and Electronics, BRIDGE reaches 0.1128, 0.1262, and 0.0778 Recall@20, respectively, with corresponding NDCG@20 values of 0.0525, 0.0594, and 0.0385. A second gap is operational. In retrieval-and-rank pipelines, the shortlist is already the decision bottleneck. A content signal that helps representation learning can still hurt ranking when it is allowed to move every score in the catalog. BRIDGE isolates that failure mode: the multimodal backbone defines the shortlist, and behavior evidence is allowed to revise only the candidates that the backbone has already made plausible. This localizes correction to the region where ranking is actually decided. The contributions of this work are: We identify two diagnostics in multimodal recommendation: direct cross-view consistency has an effective range, and the representation spectrum separates shared low-frequency evidence from more private higher-frequency variation. We propose DFGE, a dual-frequency graph evidence encoder that preserves that spectrum with band routing and structural regularization. We propose BEN and CRI, which turn training-only co-user overlap into signed behavior evidence and use it only to calibrate the candidate scope used at training and inference. {figures/Framework.pdf} Multimodal recommendation extends collaborative filtering with visual and textual signals. VBPR shows that content can alleviate sparsity, while graph-based methods such as MMGCN , GRCN , DualGNN , and LATTICE organize these signals with user-item and item-item structure. Recent variants add denoising, diffusion, robustness, dual-path routing, retrieval completion, or stronger cross-view consistency . Related alignment and fusion work also explores dual-graph attention, hierarchical multi-step fusion, and multimodal large language model coupling . The common theme is to improve representation quality before ranking. BRIDGE differs by asking when content evidence should be trusted by the ranker rather than how aggressively it should be fused. The key distinction is that most alignment methods treat cross-view agreement as a proxy for better ranking. In practice, that assumption is too strong. Once content matching becomes dominant, it can erase the differences that separate a plausible item from a near duplicate or a popular lookalike. BRIDGE keeps the same multimodal evidence, but uses it to define candidate geometry rather than to rewrite every score in the catalog. The question is not whether two views can be aligned. The question is whether that alignment should change the rank of a specific candidate. This is why the strongest recent alignment systems still leave a gap. They can improve the encoder, the fusion block, or the item graph, but they do not decide when the resulting evidence should be allowed to alter the shortlist. BRIDGE treats that decision as part of the model, not a post-hoc choice. Frequency-aware recommenders separate stable structure from fine-grained variation. SMORE , FITMM , SSR , MSCF-Net , DuGRec , D-DPDG , and Sequences-as-Nodes show that spectral views can organize multimodal or collaborative evidence. These works differ in whether the spectrum is used for denoising, co-frequency enhancement, or explicit band routing. BRIDGE uses the same general intuition but keeps the band decomposition as a representation core rather than a standalone predictor. That makes BRIDGE different from frequency-aware denoising pipelines. Those models usually use the spectrum to suppress noise or separate stable from unstable components, while BRIDGE uses the decomposition to decide which bands remain shared evidence and which bands are left as residual variation for candidate calibration. The band split is therefore an authorization step, not only a regularizer. The same point applies to band routing. A decomposition can denoise a representation, but denoising alone does not explain how the output should affect ranking. BRIDGE keeps the spectrum inside the backbone and lets candidate calibration consume only the bands that remain useful after decomposition. This keeps the representation and the calibration rule aligned with one another. In parallel, behavior-guided methods such as BPR , LightGCN , DRAGON , FGCM , JBM-Diff , and causal-inspired multimodal recommendation use interaction structure to validate or filter content-derived relations. More recent work extends this idea to dual-graph diffusion, modality coherence, and multi-intention or sequential settings . BRIDGE combines these lines, but uses behavior only as a local authorization signal for candidate- level residual calibration rather than as a global fusion or denoising target. That distinction matters because many behavior-guided methods still operate globally. They improve the score function, the graph structure, or the fusion rule, but the behavior signal is applied across the item space. BRIDGE keeps the same evidence and narrows its effect to a top-$K$ candidate set, which makes the behavior branch easier to interpret and less likely to distort the catalog-wide ranking order. In our diagnostics, global score correction often hurts, while top-$K$ candidate correction can improve ranking. Co-user behavior evidence is also consistently strong, especially on sparse Amazon domains. We therefore interpret behavior evidence as a local validity test for candidate calibration. Table~ shows that BRIDGE improves every metric on every dataset. The Recall gains are consistent, but the NDCG gains are larger, which means the method mainly sharpens the order near the top of the list rather than only recovering more positives at larger cutoffs. The margin is most pronounced on Electronics, where sparsity and popularity skew make a global residual less reliable. Broken down by dataset, Baby shows the smallest but still stable gain, Sports shows the largest balanced improvement across Recall and NDCG, and Electronics shows the clearest benefit from candidate-level calibration. The low-frequency band carries stronger cross-view agreement and larger energy, the high-frequency band is less aligned but still informative, and the low-high cosine is near zero. BRIDGE therefore uses the low bands as shared structure and the high bands as local variation, rather than treating the full representation as a single fused signal. The results support a frequency-first design. DFGE is not a cosmetic front end: it defines the candidate geometry on which ranking acts, and the frequency objectives keep low-frequency shared evidence and higher-frequency private variation from collapsing into one signal. BEN turns co-user overlap into signed behavior evidence, and CRI decides where that evidence may alter the score. The behavior-only, raw co-occurrence, and EASE controls show that neighborhood evidence does not replace representation learning or reduce to a generic ItemKNN signal. The learned fusion variants stay close to the fixed residual but do not exceed it, so additional calibration capacity is not the main driver. Together, the controls indicate that candidate restriction is the main safeguard against global score distortion. Recent dual-graph, retrieval-enhanced, and dual-path alignment models point in a nearby direction: they strengthen modality interaction with extra routing, completion, or alignment modules . BRIDGE keeps the backbone interpretable and pushes complexity into the evidence-selection rule: the base score carries the multimodal representation, and the behavior residual only decides where that score may be locally corrected. A useful reading of the control suite is to separate backbone quality from residual authority. DFGE builds the candidate manifold. BEN converts co-user overlap into signed evidence. CRI decides where that evidence may alter the score. The gain from BRIDGE comes from keeping those roles separate. When the same evidence is allowed to act globally, it starts competing with the multimodal backbone and the improvement shrinks. Candidate restriction is therefore not an implementation detail; it is the mechanism that keeps the residual aligned with ranking. This also explains the popularity diagnostics. Head items dominate the base ranking because they are easier to reconstruct from sparse co-occurrence, while mid, tail, and cold items depend more on cross-modal structure. BRIDGE does not impose an explicit anti-popularity penalty. It preserves head accuracy and reallocates useful mass to less exposed items that survive the candidate gate. The exposure shift in Table~ is a by-product of better localization, not a separate regularizer. At inference, BRIDGE adds a small residual pass over a cached candidate list. The item graph is precomputed, the behavior graph is derived from training interactions only, and the residual is never asked to rewrite the full catalog. That keeps the method compatible with standard retrieval-and-rank pipelines. The main failure mode is equally clear: if the backbone fails to place a relevant item in the candidate set, the residual cannot recover it. No calibration rule removes that ceiling. Noisy co-user neighborhoods can also blur BEN, especially when user overlap is sparse or interaction bursts are highly local. In those settings the conservative coefficient variant is a safer operating point because it limits the magnitude of correction. The fixed candidate-weighted residual is stronger when the backbone already produces a reasonable shortlist, which is the regime targeted by the benchmark. The validation sweep shows that BRIDGE is not sensitive to a single hand-picked coefficient. The fixed-$ _b$ setting wins on all three datasets, but the conservative coefficient control remains close enough to preserve the same qualitative behavior. That matters because the residual is not trying to learn a second ranking model; it only has to decide how much local correction is safe. When the coefficient is too small, the behavior branch becomes decorative. When it is too large, it starts to fight the backbone. The five-point grid identifies the middle ground without introducing a heavy search cost. The same pattern appears across the frequency design. The low-frequency component carries the stable cross-view structure, while the higher-frequency bands capture sparse and item-specific variation. The IB surrogate and the frequency regularizer are both needed because they serve different roles: one controls how much each node can route into the bands, the other preserves a useful band geometry once the routing has happened. The ablations show that dropping either term weakens the ranking signal, while changing the basis has only a small effect. The decomposition is therefore structural, not basis-specific. The resulting scope is narrow on purpose. BRIDGE assumes that the base backbone already places a relevant item in the candidate set. It does not attempt to rescue items that never surface in retrieval, and it does not attempt to override a weak multimodal representation. Those limits are acceptable in the benchmark setting, where the objective is to sharpen the top of the list rather than to replace retrieval. The design therefore fits the standard two-stage pipeline used in practical recommenders. Because the same setup is used across all methods, the residual effect can be interpreted directly. If a baseline is strong in Recall but weak in NDCG, the calibration branch is improving ordering rather than just retrieving more items. If head exposure drops while mid and tail recall rise, the residual is redistributing ranking mass without collapsing popular items. Those are the behaviors reported by the tables and controls, so the narrative stays tied to measured outputs. BRIDGE makes one explicit tradeoff: it keeps the base encoder as the ranking backbone and uses behavior evidence only as a local calibration signal. That choice preserves interpretability and blocks global score rewrites, but it also makes candidate quality the main ceiling on performance. If the base top-$K$ list is weak, the residual branch cannot recover items that never enter the candidate scope. The SVD band routing adds one decomposition over each 64-dimensional channel block, yet the cost is bounded and the multimodal item graph is cached, so the additional overhead remains modest. The implementation is reproducible from the information in this paper and the accompanying anonymous code package. The behavior graph is built only from the training split, the same BEIT3 feature files are used across all methods, and validation selects the residual weight before test evaluation. The released code stores the complete configuration, including candidate size, behavior top-$K$, band count, and random seed, so the reported numbers can be regenerated without any author-identifying information. The package also records the split manifest, feature paths, preprocessing scripts, and full evaluation commands, so the same results can be reproduced from the raw benchmark files without manual intervention. Because the same feature cache is used for every method, the comparison isolates ranking behavior rather than feature engineering. The reported tables use full-sort evaluation with fixed seeds and a validation-only sweep over the residual coefficient. We presented BRIDGE, a behavior-guided residual integration framework for multimodal recommendation. BRIDGE uses DFGE to organize multimodal evidence into frequency bands, BEN to convert training-only co-user overlap into signed behavior evidence, and CRI to apply that evidence only inside the candidate set produced by the backbone. Across Baby, Sports, and Electronics, this candidate-level design improves both Recall and NDCG, with the strongest gains in NDCG, which means the method sharpens the ordering near the top of the list rather than only recovering more items at larger cutoffs. The main lesson is that multimodal recommenders do not need a global residual rewrite to benefit from behavior evidence. They need a bounded calibration rule that acts only where the backbone has already produced plausible candidates. That separation keeps the method interpretable, reproducible, and compatible with standard two-stage pipelines. The remaining limit is explicit: if a relevant item never enters the candidate set, no residual can recover it. Within that scope, BRIDGE provides a compact and effective operating point for top-$K$ multimodal ranking.
