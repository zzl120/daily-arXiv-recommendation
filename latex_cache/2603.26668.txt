Extensive experiments show that Bridge-RAG achieves around 15.65\ Retrieval-Augmented Generation has become an important paradigm for enhancing the generation quality of Large Language Models by integrating external knowledge~. Existing RAG frameworks are primarily of three types: text-based, graph-based, and tree-based. Text-based ones store information as raw text. In contrast, graph-based and tree-based RAG represent knowledge through structured schemas to provide richer context and capture complex relationships among entities. RAG systems face two critical challenges regarding retrieval accuracy and computational efficiency. First, in terms of retrieval accuracy, standard RAG frameworks usually rely on vector similarity search, which tends to retrieve passages that are semantically related but contextually fragmented or noisy. This misalignment often results in the generator receiving insufficient or irrelevant evidence, inevitably propagating errors to the final response. Second, a severe efficiency bottleneck persists in structured RAG frameworks, especially for Tree-RAG. As the dataset scale and tree depth grow, the time required to traverse the hierarchical structure and locate relevant entities significantly increases. Our empirical results in Table~ indicate that the retrieval time of standard RAG framework like Tree-RAG and Graph-RAG are still quite long. These two challenges hinder the deployment of RAG systems in real-world scenarios that demand high precision and low-latency responses. {RagOverviewnew.pdf} This paper proposes Bridge-RAG to effectively address the two challenges above. Bridge-RAG incorporates two key designs. To overcome the accuracy challenge, Bridge-RAG introduces the concept of and proposes the structure to systematically organize knowledge, enabling the retrieval of coherent multi-level context from entities to document chunks. To overcome the efficiency challenge, Bridge-RAG designs an data structure that provides $O $ time complexity for entity lookups, which is significantly lower than naive Tree-RAG and Graph-RAG. The workflow of Bridge-RAG is shown in Figure~. Our first key design focuses on enhancing retrieval accuracy from the aspects of semantic depth and contextual breadth. First, we introduce the concept of to facilitate robust semantic understanding. Specifically, an groups several consecutive document chunks into a high-level knowledge unit. We organize these abstracts into tree structures where upper-level abstracts represent more general concepts and lower-level ones capture more detailed information. This hierarchical organization facilitates a three-tier retrieval path where serves as the bridge between and . In this way, Bridge-RAG ensures the captured content is semantically relevant. Second, we design a multi-level retrieval strategy to ensure the inclusion of sufficient contextual information. Specifically, Bridge-RAG systematically traces the hierarchical lineage of the abstract tree, retrieving document chunks associated with both parent and child abstracts alongside the target node. This strategy ensures that the retrieved context spans multiple abstraction levels, enabling Bridge-RAG to to maintain a comprehensive contextual scope and further improve the answer accuracy. Our second key design introduces an to accelerate the retrieval process by facilitating $O $ lookups from query entities to their corresponding abstracts. This design incorporates two primary optimizations aimed at enhancing efficiency from the perspectives of spatial and temporal locality. First, to optimize spatial locality, we introduce a block linked list structure for entity-abstract mappings. Unlike traditional linked lists, each node aggregates multiple addresses, which significantly reduces memory fragmentation and optimizes cache performance for large-scale hierarchies. Second, to leverage temporal locality, we propose an entity temperature-based sorting mechanism. By maintaining a temperature variable that records access frequency, the Cuckoo Filter dynamically reorders entities within each bucket. By placing high-temperature entities at the front, we effectively optimize the efficiency of linear search within buckets. To maximize storage efficiency, entities are represented within the Cuckoo Filter as 12-bit fingerprints, significantly reducing the memory footprint compared to storing raw strings. To ensure scalability, we also design a dynamic resizing mechanism that maintains the load factor within an optimal range. This strategy ensures high memory utilization while effectively mitigating hash collisions as the dataset expands. Experimental results demonstrate that Bridge-RAG significantly outperforms the baseline RAG, achieving around 15.65\ Cuckoo Filter is an efficient data structure for supporting fast element lookup and deletion operations, which is mainly used for collection operations and data stream processing~. It is developed based on the idea of Cuckoo Hashing~. Cuckoo Filter outperforms traditional Bloom filters in terms of storage efficiency and query performance, especially in scenarios where frequent insertion and deletion of elements are required. The main advantage of Cuckoo Filter is its support for dynamic updates, which enables it to efficiently handle element changes in a collection. Unlike Bloom Filter, Cuckoo Filter can not only query whether an element exists or not, but also support the deletion operation of an element, a feature that is important in many practical applications~. The working principle of Cuckoo Filter is based on multiple hash functions and a bucket structure, which is utilized by storing the elements in fixed-size buckets and using the hash conflicts to achieve fast lookup of elements. Recent research has further explored the integration of such probabilistic data structures into high-throughput retrieval systems to minimize index memory footprints while maintaining low latency~. In 2025, researchers have taken this further by proposing Hybrid-Index RAG~, which combines learned indexes with probabilistic filters like Cuckoo Filters to handle billion-scale vector datasets. Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation is an extension of RAG, where the information retrieval process is augmented by leveraging graph-based structures to organize and retrieve information~. The key difference between traditional RAG and Graph-RAG is the use of a graph, which can improve the relevance and contextuality of the retrieved information~. The algorithmic process of Graph-RAG enhances the understanding of relationships and provides more precise information retrieval than traditional RAG~. Notably, recent approaches like Microsoft's GraphRAG have introduced community detection mechanisms to generate community summaries, enabling "global" answers~. Furthermore, EMG-RAG~ integrates an Editable Memory Graph to manage partitions and capture dynamic relations. To address the limitations of simple binary relations in complex reasoning, Hyper-RAG~ utilizes hypergraphs to model multi-entity relationships, showing superior performance in multi-hop reasoning tasks compared to standard graph methods. However, the complexity in graph construction and maintenance will be a trouble, and the quality of the graph can also affect the accuracy of responses. Therefore, in comparison to Tree-RAG, Graph-RAG still has certain disadvantages. Tree-RAG is an emerging method that combines tree structure and large language models to improve the effectiveness of knowledge retrieval and generation tasks. This hierarchical approach is increasingly vital as indicated by recent benchmarks like AA-LCR ~. The necessity of structural retrieval is further supported by the RAG-Bench-2025~, which reveals that flat retrieval systems fail significantly on questions requiring synthesis of information across more than five document clusters. A prominent recent advancement is RAPTOR~, which employs recursive clustering. Building on this, Dynamic-Tree RAG~ introduces an adaptive pruning mechanism that dynamically adjusts the tree depth based on query complexity, optimizing the trade-off between accuracy and speed. Similarly, Reasoning-RAG~ integrates Chain-of-Thought processes directly into the tree traversal, allowing the model to refine its retrieval path iteratively. In our proposed framework, we extend these concepts by utilizing entities as explicit bridges to access these hierarchical abstracts. The system traverses through the hierarchical structure of the tree to obtain the abstract nodes related to the query. This process effectively combines knowledge retrieval and generation and improves the accuracy and contextual relevance. The foundation of our approach lies in the hierarchical abstract tree structure, which addresses the accuracy limitations of traditional RAG systems. Abstracts are built by semantically grouping five consecutive document chunks into higher-level knowledge units. Each abstract receives a pair identifier $i$ and subsumes the chunk IDs $5i … 5i+4$. The five constituent chunks are fused with embedding aggregation or summarization to produce a concise semantic summary, enabling efficient abstraction-layer retrieval. Abstracts are organized hierarchically in tree structures, where upper-level abstracts represent more general and abstract concepts, while lower-level abstracts capture more specific and detailed information. This hierarchical organization enables multi-level semantic retrieval: when a query entity is identified, we not only retrieve the corresponding abstract and its associated document chunks, but also trace the abstract's parent-child relationships in the tree. Formally, given an initial abstract set $_0 = \{a_i\}$ retrieved from the Cuckoo Filter, we expand it through hierarchical traversal: _{} = _0 _{a _0} _d ) where $_d $ denotes the set of parent abstracts up to $d$ levels upward, and $_d $ denotes the set of child abstracts up to $d$ levels downward. By retrieving these related abstracts along with their corresponding document chunks, we construct a comprehensive context spanning multiple abstraction levels, significantly improving answer accuracy by providing both high-level semantic understanding and granular textual details. While the hierarchical abstract tree structure substantially improves accuracy, it introduces computational overhead. To address this efficiency challenge, we integrate an improved Cuckoo Filter that stores entity-abstract mappings, enabling fast lookup to locate relevant abstracts containing query entities.
