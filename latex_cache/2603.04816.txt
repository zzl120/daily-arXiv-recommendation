 {acmart} }} {Scaling Laws for Reranking in Information Retrieval}{Mar, 2026}{Scaling Laws for Reranking in Information Retrieval} } } } } {Seetharaman, Bansal, Zamani, Dhole} Scaling laws have been observed across a wide range of tasks, such as natural language generation and dense retrieval, where performance follows predictable patterns as model size, data, and compute grow. However, these scaling laws are insufficient for understanding the scaling behavior of multi-stage retrieval systems, which typically include a reranking stage. In large-scale multi-stage retrieval systems, reranking is the final and most influential step before presenting a ranked list of items to the end user. In this work, we present the first systematic study of scaling laws for rerankers by analyzing performance across model sizes and data budgets for three popular paradigms: pointwise, pairwise, and listwise reranking. Using a detailed case study with cross-encoder rerankers, we demonstrate that performance follows a predictable power law. This regularity allows us to accurately forecast the performance of larger models for some metrics more than others using smaller-scale experiments, offering a robust methodology for saving significant computational resources. For example, we accurately estimate the NDCG of a 1B-parameter model by training and evaluating only smaller models , in both in-domain as well as out-of-domain settings. Our results establish scaling principles for reranking and provide actionable insights for building industrial-grade retrieval systems. We will release the code and evaluation protocols used for training, evaluation, and large-scale performance forecasting here{https://github.com/rahulseetharaman/cross-encoder-scaling}}. {{ }} {{ }} Modern search engines often follow multi-stage retrieval pipelines~. Such pipelines typically employ an efficient retrieval model, such as BM25~, to retrieve a relatively small number of candidates from a very large corpus, followed by one or more reranking stages in which the candidates from the last stage are reranked~. While the objective in the early stage retrievers is to maximize recall, the objectives in later stages of the funnel is to maximize , which is often measured by metrics like NDCG . While the principles of scaling are well-established for natural language generation and, more recently, for dense retrieval and embedding scaling , a similar characterization for is underexplored. Reranking differs from first-stage retrieval in ways that make scaling behavior non-obvious: rerankers operate on a candidate set induced by a retriever , they are trained with heterogeneous learning-to-rank objectives , and they are evaluated using discontinuous top-k metrics such as NDCG. As a result, scaling laws established for language modeling or dense retrieval do not directly imply predictable scaling for rerankers. Besides, reranking is the final, high-precision stage of a retrieval pipeline, where a computationally intensive model refines a small set of candidates. The high cost of training large-scale reranking models makes them a prime candidate for scaling analysis. Our goal is to forecast the downstream performance of large rerankers using only smaller-scale training runs, so that we can plan expensive training at 1B+ scale with fewer full-size experiments. We frame this through four research questions to be addressed independently for each paradigm . In that regard, we present the first systematic study of scaling laws for rerankers. To reliably predict the performance of a large reranker by observing the behavior of smaller rerankers, we examine how reranking performance scales across three primary learning-to-rank paradigms as model size and labeled data grow. Through comprehensive experimentation on a set of cross encoder models of varying sizes, finetuned on 100K MSMARCO queries, we demonstrate ranking metrics like follow smooth, predictable power laws and scale with model and dataset size. Crucially, these laws exhibit strong predictive power, enabling accurate forecasts of large-model performance from small-scale experiments. To our knowledge, this is the first study to systematically characterize scaling laws for neural rerankers across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise objectives. From our extensive experiments, we have the following key observations -- 1) We observe predictable power law scaling across model, data and joint scaling axes for MSMARCO-dev and the TREC DL evaluation sets~ 2) It is possible to accurately forecast a downstream metric for the 1B model by training on model sizes up to 400M. Modern search systems are commonly organized as multi-stage pipelines, where an efficient first-stage retriever prioritizes recall by selecting a small candidate set, and a later-stage reranker prioritizes precision by reordering those candidates with a more expressive model. Reranking operates on a decision set induced by the upstream retriever, and is evaluated with top-$k$ ranking metrics that are discontinuous and sensitive to local ordering. These properties make it unclear whether scaling trends established for pretraining or first-stage retrieval transfer to rerankers in a predictable way. Scaling laws characterize how performance changes as we increase model capacity, data, and compute. Early work on learning curves in neural networks identified regularities in generalization with scale~, and subsequent studies showed that language model loss often follows smooth power-law trends with respect to parameters, data, and compute~, enabling performance forecasting and motivating compute-optimal training strategies such as Chinchilla~. Scaling analyses have since been extended across modalities, including vision~, multimodal learning~, and regimes that examine effectively unbounded compute~. However, forecasting task metrics can be substantially less reliable than forecasting training loss, prompting recent work on when scaling-based prediction succeeds and how to improve its robustness~. This reliability gap is especially relevant for retrieval, where practitioners care about ranking metrics rather than perplexity-style objectives. The retrieval community has recently begun to establish systematic scaling behavior for retrieval components. Prior work shows consistent gains from scaling retrieval-augmented datastores~, and analyzes how sparse and dense retrieval mechanisms emerge and scale in decoder-only models, with scaling behavior depending on training objectives and compute budgets~. Complementary work studies generative retrieval and introduces objectives that enable continuous scaling measurement, revealing log-linear and power-law trends across training and inference compute~. Collectively, these results indicate that retrieval quality can exhibit predictable behavior along axes of model capacity, data, and compute. However, these studies primarily focus on retrieval as over a corpus, rather than over a retriever-induced candidate set. Rerankers typically evaluate a small set of retrieved candidates using richer interaction models , and are trained using diverse learning-to-rank objectives. Classical learning-to-rank formulations distinguish , , and paradigms~, with representative approaches such as RankNet~, RankSVM~, and ListNet~. Neural reranking has since evolved substantially with pretrained language models and cross-encoder architectures~, and many works study efficiency techniques such as distillation and architectural approximations to reduce inference cost~. Despite these advances, we lack a principled understanding of whether reranker scales predictably with model size and training exposure, and whether scaling behavior depends on the underlying objective . Our paper targets this gap: we study scaling laws , where the evaluation target is a downstream top-$k$ ranking metric and the decision set is conditioned on a first-stage retriever. We ask whether we can forecast large-model reranking performance from smaller-scale training runs, along model, data-exposure, and joint scaling axes, and whether the fitted trends differ across learning-to-rank paradigms. Because discontinuous ranking metrics can yield noisier curves, we also analyze contrastive entropy as a secondary continuous diagnostic , while keeping NDCG@10 as the primary forecasting target. Unlike dense retrieval, where random negatives probe separability in a global corpus space, reranking operates on a candidate set induced by a first-stage retriever. We use BM25 as our first-stage retriever, and hence compute CE over the BM25 top-100 candidates , reflecting the reranker’s actual decision set. To reduce sensitivity to score calibration across training paradigms, we normalize scores. We report both CE and NDCG: CE provides a smoother signal for scaling analysis, while NDCG measures downstream ranking quality. While NDCG@10 is our primary downstream metric, it is a discontinuous top-k measure and can introduce noise in curve fitting. We therefore also report contrastive entropy as a continuous diagnostic signal, analogous to perplexity-style proxies used in other scaling-law settings. Importantly, our goal is not to replace downstream evaluation: we fit and forecast scaling laws directly on NDCG@10. We also fit scaling laws over CE to help interpret training dynamics when it is stable. To measure how successful a fit is, previous approaches have focused on reporting the goodness of fit using metrics like the coefficient of determination, which don't directly shed light on performance at unknown compute. Instead, we measure the error at a held-out compute by not using the data used for fitting the curve. This helps directly gauge the downstream effectiveness of a scaling fit. We study reranker scaling along three axes—model size, training data exposure, and their joint effect—by fitting parametric curves to observed NDCG@10 and evaluating predictive accuracy via held-out checkpoints. To characterize how ranking quality improves with capacity, we fit each IR metric $$ using a saturating power law: } \;=\; a \;-\; b\,M^{-c}, where $a$ represents the asymptotic performance ceiling, $b$ controls the scale of gains, and $c$ is the model-scaling exponent governing diminishing returns as $M$ increases. We use the Ettin cross-encoder series of models at six different sizes for our experiments -- 17M, 32M, 68M, 150M, 400M and 1B. We finetune each one of them on 100k queries from the MS MARCO passage ranking dataset. For the pointwise paradigm, we use a batch size of 128, a learning rate of 2e-5, and fine-tune for 1 epoch. For listwise and pairwise paradigms, each batch consists of 16 queries, 1 positive document, and 10 anchor negatives, making the effective batch size to be 160. For all our expriments, we employ the same learning rates and finetune our models for 1 epoch. While we see clear scaling trends for MRR on MSMARCO DEV set as well as the TREC DL datasets, with the exception of TREC DL '19 We present the first systematic framework for analyzing scaling laws in reranking. Through comprehensive experiments across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise reranking, we demonstrate that reranker performance follows predictable power laws with model and data scale with traditional metrics like NDCG. Such scaling behaviour enables accurate forecasting of downstream ranking quality in the large-model regime using only smaller-scale training runs, providing a practical approach for planning expensive reranker training. We also find that scaling behavior differs across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise objectives, suggesting that a single universal fit is unlikely to capture all paradigms equally well. Similarly, while metrics like MAP simialrly show reliable scaling behavior, MRR does not show predictable scaling on all evaluation datasets. Finally, while contrastive entropy is a useful continuous proxy in dense-retrieval scaling analyses, it is less reliable for reranker forecasting in our setting due to sensitivity to score calibration and normalization: ranking improvements can occur even when score margins fluctuate. Overall, our results establish actionable scaling principles for reranking: practitioners can use small-scale sweeps to predict large-scale NDCG outcomes, allocate compute more efficiently, and choose objectives with awareness of their differing scaling behavior. Our work entails interesting future studies including extending these analyses to other candidate generation methods beyond BM25, broader out-of-domain benchmarks, alternative reranker architectures , and studying how candidate set size and retrieval quality interact with reranker scaling.
