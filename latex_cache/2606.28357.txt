However, existing multimodal recommendation systems typically rely on a singular, black-box forward inference, collapsing all these intricate decisions into an opaque scoring mechanism~. This implicit approach severely limits transparency, interpretability, and robustness, especially in challenging scenarios like cold-start and cross-domain tasks~. Most contemporary Vision-Language Models ~ in recommendation embed multimodal signals implicitly within their transformer architectures. While effective at feature fusion, these models fail to explicitly articulate their reasoning processes~, resulting in recommendations that are hard to interpret and audit. Subsequent innovations extend this paradigm through diverse representation learning strategies: UniSRec constructs item embeddings from descriptive texts rather than static IDs, while TransRec integrates multimodal user feedback through BERT and ResNet encoders for content-based personalization. Architectural unification efforts like M6-Rec convert behavioral patterns into text sequences for Transformer processing, enabling task-adaptive fine-tuning through customized loss functions. P5 and OpenP5 achieve cross-task generalization by implementing instruction-tuned LLMs that represent user-item relationships through natural language interfaces, later extended by P5-ID through novel item indexing schemes combining sequential, collaborative, and semantic signals. Multimodal generation techniques have simultaneously evolved across three directions: auxiliary feature integration, explainable recommendation, and semantic structure discovery. Early approaches like VBPR and PiNet enhance collaborative filtering through visual feature extraction and heterogeneous preference modeling, respectively, while JRL pioneers joint multimodal representation learning. Domain-specific generators have emerged for fashion , travel , and culinary recommendations , producing visually-grounded explanations. The evolution of Large Language Models has catalyzed next-generation vision-language architectures, transcending traditional visual-language systems through LLM-powered linguistic reasoning. Pioneered by architectures such as LLaVA-series~, BLIP-family~, and MiniGPT-4~, these models demonstrate exceptional visual dialog capabilities via LLM-based language encoders. However, their computational footprint, typically requiring 7B-65B parameters, creates deployment bottlenecks for edge/mobile platforms demanding real-time responsiveness. While proprietary models like Gemini~ address this via scaled variants , their closed-source nature limits adaptability. Open-source initiatives like MobileVLM~ develop compact architectures to bridge this gap.
