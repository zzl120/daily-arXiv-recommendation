While query decomposition is widely adopted as an intuitive remedy, its effectiveness across different retrieval pipeline stages remains underexplored. In this paper, we conduct a stage-aware empirical study and uncover a stark, stage-dependent effect: decomposition during initial retrieval frequently harms retrieval performance due to semantic dilution, yet substantially improves reranking by enabling more fine-grained constraint verification. Motivated by these insights, we propose a principled framework that retains the monolithic query during initial retrieval to preserve global semantic context, while employing sub-queries exclusively during reranking for fine-grained constraint matching. Extensive evaluations on the MultiConIR and SSRB benchmarks demonstrate that our framework consistently improves ranking performance for compositional queries across multiple retrieval and reranking models. We release our code at . Information Retrieval underpins modern information access by enabling systems to locate relevant documents based on user-provided context. Early systems supported multi-condition retrieval through keyword matching approaches such as BM25~ and faceted filtering mechanisms~, but these methods relied primarily on lexical overlap and struggled with semantic generalization. Over time, IR evolved toward dense neural paradigms that encode queries and documents as holistic semantic embeddings, including DPR~ and ColBERT~. More recently, large language models have enabled more precise semantic matching through reranking and query rewriting methods such as RankGPT~ and HyDE~. Despite these advances, modern retrieval systems still struggle with queries that are compositional rather than atomic, containing multiple constraints that must be jointly satisfied. Consider a query such as “” which specifies multiple independent constraints. Addressing such queries requires retrieval systems to move beyond coarse semantic matching and perform implicit logical verification, ensuring that all conditions are satisfied simultaneously. This setting is commonly referred to as multi-condition retrieval. Despite their prevalence, most modern retrievers are ill-equipped for these complex intents. Recent benchmarks like MultiConIR~ and SSRB~ have exposed a critical vulnerability: dense retrievers struggle to distinguish positive documents from "hard negatives" that share the same broad theme but violate specific constraints. While the community has explored query decomposition—breaking a complex query into simpler sub-queries—as a potential remedy, its application in retrieval systems has evolved significantly. DecompRC~ pioneered decompose‑then‑rescore for complex QA, followed by retrieval‑augmented frameworks that combine decomposition with reranking~. Agentic systems such as Self‑Ask~, Search‑R1~, and ParallelSearch~ further iterate decomposition and search to compose final responses. However, its actual utility in multi-condition scenarios remains poorly understood and inconsistently reported in the literature. This discrepancy leads to a critical but underexplored research question: In this work, we conduct a systematic empirical study of query decomposition and uncover a stark, stage-dependent effect: decomposition frequently impairs initial retrieval, but improves reranking on the same queries. We trace this asymmetry to a phenomenon we term . A complex query expresses a conjunction of conditions that jointly pinpoint a small set of target documents; decomposition strips away this conjunctive structure, leaving each sub-query with only a fragment of the original intent. Each sub-query now matches a much larger candidate pool—documents that satisfy one fragment but fail the remaining conditions. With only partial information, the retriever promotes these distractors over the true multi-condition target, weakening the discriminative signal of the original query. Rerankers, in contrast, operate over a small pre-filtered pool and attend to query–document interactions at the token level, verifying whether each sub-query is individually satisfied by a candidate document. Motivated by these insights, we propose a principled framework that strategically aligns query formats with pipeline components. Instead of employing decomposition for initial retrieval where global context is paramount, we retain the monolithic query for candidate generation to safeguard global recall. Query decomposition is applied exclusively during the subsequent reranking phase, where advanced rerankers can model fine-grained interactions between candidate documents and isolated sub-queries. This design enables explicit, constraint-level verification and effectively filters out ``almost relevant'' distractors. To summarize, our primary contributions are as follows: : We systematically evaluate query decomposition across different stages, identifying as the root causes of performance degradation during initial retrieval. : We introduce a framework that strategically aligns query formats with pipeline stages, preserving the monolithic query for retrieval while exploiting fine-grained sub-queries for reranking. : Through comprehensive evaluations on the MultiConIR and SSRB benchmarks across various embedding and reranking models, we demonstrate that our approach substantially and consistently enhances ranking precision for complex compositional queries. Traditional retrieval benchmarks such as MS MARCO~ and BEIR~ have driven the development of modern retrieval models, but they mainly evaluate retrieval for individual natural language queries based on lexical or semantic similarity. Such settings often fail to capture user intent involving multiple independent constraints. Recent benchmarks like BRIGHT~ introduce reasoning-intensive queries beyond surface matching. Nevertheless, retrieval is still framed as satisfying a single unified query objective. More recent work explicitly studies multi-condition retrieval. BIRCO~ targets retrieval tasks with multi-faceted objectives, MultiConIR~ evaluates retrieval robustness as query conditions increase, MCMR~ evaluates multi-condition multimodal retrieval, focusing on whether retrieval models can satisfy multiple fine-grained semantic constraints across modalities, and SSRB~ constructs a large-scale benchmark for natural language queries that combine exact matching constraints and fuzzy semantic requirements across multiple fields. These studies highlight the increasing difficulty of satisfying multiple constraints simultaneously, indicating that existing retrieval models still struggle with complex query requirements~. This gap motivates further research on robust multi-condition retrieval. In particular, it calls for more effective ways to handle complex queries with multiple constraints, which we discuss in the following subsection. Query decomposition has been widely studied as a strategy for handling complex information needs. Prior work on query reformulation and rewriting has been shown to improve retrieval effectiveness, particularly in conversational search and information retrieval settings~. Building on this idea, query decomposition splits a complex query into simpler sub-queries, and has been applied in multi-hop reasoning and retrieval-augmented generation , where decomposed queries can improve evidence retrieval and answer accuracy~. More recent work further optimizes decomposition to better align with downstream retrievers or dynamically select or prioritize sub-queries during retrieval~. Decomposition has also been explored in structured retrieval settings, where sub-queries are used to capture fine-grained attributes or sub-intents~. Despite these advances, existing approaches often treat decomposed sub-queries independently and focus on improving recall. This limits their ability to model global consistency across multiple constraints, particularly in multi-condition retrieval. In contrast, we study query decomposition from a stage-aware perspective and show that its effectiveness critically depends on how it is integrated into retrieval and reranking pipelines. We study information retrieval under multi-condition queries, where a query specifies multiple constraints that must be jointly satisfied. Formally, we represent a query as a set of atomic conditions $Q = \{c_1, c_2, , c_k\}$. Given a document $d$, we say $d$ is to $Q$ if it satisfies all $k$ conditions simultaneously; otherwise it is partially relevant or irrelevant. The retrieval objective is to rank fully relevant documents above all others. To model compositional queries, we consider query decomposition, which transforms a query $Q$ into a set of sub-queries via a decomposition function $$: = \{q_1, q_2, , q_m\}. Each sub-query $q_i$ is associated with a set of conditions $C \{c_1, , c_j\}$. Collectively, these sub-queries provide partial views of the original query, each capturing a subset of the constraints. We consider a standard two-stage retrieval pipeline. In the first stage, a retriever produces a candidate set $_K$ of size $K$ from the corpus. In the second stage, a reranker assigns fine-grained relevance scores to each candidate in $_K$ and returns a reordered list. At each stage, the input query can be either the original query $Q$ or a set of decomposed sub-queries $ $. Our central question is whether the effectiveness of query decomposition depends on the stage at which it is applied. Intuitively, query decomposition offers a natural way to improve multi-condition retrieval: decomposing a long, information-dense query into shorter sub-queries allows the model to be more precised about each condition. By aggregating the top candidates retrieved for each sub-query through a fusion mechanism, the final result better reflects documents that simultaneously satisfy all conditions. The decomposition function $_{}$ is formulated as: \[ _{} = \{q_i\}_{i=1}^{n} |C | = 1, \, i, \] where $n$ is the total number of conditions in $Q$, and $C $ denotes the set of semantic conditions contained within sub-query $q_i$. In contrast to the naive approach, which restricts each sub-query to a single condition, this strategy partitions a complex query $Q$ into sub-queries that each contain between $k$ and $j$ conditions, where $1 k j n$ and $n$ denotes the total number of conditions in $Q$. Unlike the previous rule-based strategies, this approach leverages an LLM to dynamically determine the optimal granularity of decomposition. Ultimately, this approach offers a principled and highly effective pipeline for modern retrieval systems to handle complex, compositional user intents. *{Limitations} First, while our study explores three representative query decomposition strategies and two merging techniques across distinct multi-condition retrieval benchmarks, the combinatorial design space of multi-stage pipelines is vast. Future work could investigate alternative decomposition granularities along with various fusion methods to further explore the performance boundaries and trade-offs of stage-aware retrieval. Second, to establish a highly controlled and rigorous experimental baseline, our evaluation primarily focuses on the Qwen3 model family across both pipeline stages. While these consistent model configurations provide clean, unconfounded insights into stage-dependent behavior, exploring the cross-architecture generalizability of our findings to other emerging dense retrievers and rerankers represents a promising direction for broader validation. Third, this work is conceptually dedicated to isolating and diagnosing the intrinsic mechanics of query decomposition across pipeline stages, rather than focusing on engineering-driven performance competition with miscellaneous retrieval-augmentation frameworks. Extending this stage-aware principle to integrate with other advanced indexing or prompting paradigms remains an open avenue for future exploration. While listwise reranking has shown strong performance in many ranking benchmarks, applying it to large candidate pools is computationally expensive due to the long-context interaction among multiple documents. In addition, long-context listwise reranking is more susceptible to positional biases such as the ``lost in the middle'' effect~, where documents located in the middle of the context receive less effective attention. In our setting, query decomposition introduces multiple sub-queries for each original query. Combining listwise reranking with decomposition would substantially increase context length and computational overhead, since all candidate documents and multiple sub-queries would need to be jointly processed. To avoid these issues, we adopt a pointwise reranking strategy, where each candidate document is independently scored against each decomposed sub-query. In our preliminary experiments, we explored a zero-shot approach using Qwen3-0.6B. In this setup, the model was prompted to directly generate a discrete numerical relevance score for each query-document pair. However, we found that this generative scoring strategy was unstable and consistently underperformed the original retrieval baseline. We attribute this degradation to two main factors: autoregressive generation introduces decoding variance and formatting inconsistency for numerical outputs. discrete generated scores are not well calibrated across different query-document pairs, making score aggregation less reliable. To address these limitations, we transitioned to a formulation using the Qwen3-Reranker series . Specifically, we formulate reranking as a binary classification task. Instead of relying on generated text outputs, we directly extract the logits corresponding to the ``Yes'' and ``No'' tokens from the final prediction layer.
