Clinicians and researchers face two interrelated challenges: ~keyword-only retrieval misses semantically related papers when medical terminology varies across studies, and ~top-ranked results frequently contain near-duplicate findings, reducing the diversity of information presented. This paper describes a hybrid retrieval system built on the TREC-COVID benchmark~ that addresses both challenges. We implement and compare six retrieval configurations: --- Sparse-only retrieval using SPLADE~. --- Dense-only retrieval using BGE embeddings from the FlagEmbedding project~. --- Two-pass Reciprocal Rank Fusion ~ combining B1 and B2. --- RRF + Maximal Marginal Relevance reranking~. --- Projection-based vector fusion using Achlioptas sparse random projection~. --- B5 with MMR reranking. We formulate three research questions: Does hybrid fusion outperform single-method approaches? Does projection-based fusion offer competitive performance compared to RRF? How effective is MMR for reducing result redundancy? Traditional sparse retrieval relies on lexical matching through models such as BM25~. SPLADE~ advances this paradigm by using a BERT-based masked language model to produce learned sparse representations over the full vocabulary, enabling implicit query expansion. Dense retrieval~ encodes queries and documents into fixed-dimensional vectors and retrieves by nearest-neighbour search. BGE , released through the FlagEmbedding/C-Pack project~, is a recent contrastive-trained encoder producing 768-dimensional L2-normalised vectors. Luan et al.~ demonstrate that combining sparse and dense representations improves retrieval effectiveness. Reciprocal Rank Fusion ~ provides a simple, effective method for merging ranked lists without requiring score normalisation, using the formula $ = _{r R} {k + _r }$ where $k=60$. The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma~ guarantees that random linear projections approximately preserve pairwise distances. Achlioptas~ showed that the projection matrix can be sparse , reducing computational cost while maintaining this guarantee for the raw projection step. We apply such a projection to map SPLADE's 30,522-dimensional sparse vectors into BGE's 768-dimensional space before the subsequent normalisation and fusion stages, whose effectiveness we evaluate empirically. Maximal Marginal Relevance ~ iteratively selects documents that are both relevant to the query and dissimilar to already-selected results, controlled by a parameter $ $. Clarke et al.~ established diversity as a formal evaluation dimension in information retrieval. Figure~ illustrates the RRF retrieval pipeline, one of the two deployed retrieval paths. Taken together, these results answer RQ2 more cautiously than RQ1. B5 is not the top-scoring system in absolute nDCG@10 on any of the reported query sets, but it does offer a distinct operating point that trades some effectiveness for lower latency and substantially higher diversity.
