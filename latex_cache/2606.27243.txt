Across this trajectory, a clear pattern emerges: as simple feature-engineering improvements become harder to obtain, further gains in recommendation quality and business impact increasingly depend on more expressive backbones and richer interaction mechanisms. This makes architecture evolution a natural target for automation in industrial recommender development. Despite its critical importance, architectural evolution in production still faces numerous challenges, ranging from combinatorial parameter tuning under capacity budgets to research-paper reproduction. Each demands expert intuition to navigate vast or even unbounded search spaces, careful assessment of structural compatibility, and validation through extensive offline-to-online pipelines. The process remains difficult to scale due to its operational complexity in industrial production environments and heavy reliance on expert judgment. Existing AutoML and generic coding-agent approaches only automate fragments of this workflow. AutoML methods~ mainly optimize local variables such as learning rate, hidden size, embedding dimension, or layer depth. However, effective model improvements often require cross-module topology changes. For example, a model update may upgrade target attention into joint Seq-Token and Non-Seq-Token modeling, redesign the logit-fusion path, or replace a standard residual connection with an AttentionRes module~. Such changes must further preserve input-output shape compatibility, dtype consistency, feature dependencies, training stability, and online inference compatibility across the production pipeline. LLMs have greatly improved generic coding agents, enabling them to generate and modify code according to user intent. Their success, however, is usually measured by software-level signals such as compilation success and unit-test passing~. In industrial recommender systems, an implementation may run without errors while still violating architecture semantics, for example by incorrectly removing sequence masking, degenerating self-attention into a simple MLP, or altering the logit-fusion path. Such runnable-but-negative candidates can degrade offline and online recommendation performance, including AUC, calibration, and business metrics; we refer to them as . Recent self-evolving recommender systems~ have shown that LLM agents can participate in production model optimization by proposing model changes and validating them through offline and online feedback. NOVA builds on this direction, but focuses on auditable architecture-level modifications rather than general model-change proposals. To make these modifications effective under production constraints, NOVA introduces two key mechanisms. First, it organizes architecture evolution as a feedback-driven search: previous modifications, metric changes, verification diagnostics, and trajectory memory are aggregated into an that guides the next modification direction. Second, it addresses by checking architecture semantics before expensive training and feeding diagnostics back as reusable forbidden directions. As a result, verification not only rejects the current invalid candidate, but also shapes later search by discouraging similar failure patterns. A candidate can be executable while still violating recommender-specific architecture semantics. For example, the training framework can execute a mask, branch connection, or feature fusion path as long as tensor shapes are compatible, but it cannot determine whether the masking direction, feature routing, or logit-fusion logic matches the intended architecture. Thus, compilation and local tests show that a graph can run, not that the architecture modification is semantically valid. The semantic gate checks these constraints according to skill specifications, rather than relying only on executability. Verification diagnostics are fed back into the architecture gradient defined in Section~. When a candidate is rejected, its failure pattern is stored in trajectory memory $H$ and used as a forbidden direction in later rounds. This feedback denoises the search by blocking false-positive directions before training and reducing repeated semantic failures. The semantic gate is driven by skill specifications rather than standalone lint rules. These specifications combine historical seed rules, accumulated forbidden patterns, and LLM-assisted checks against the intended design and model context. For uncovered cases, the gate avoids overconfident rejection and leaves the candidate to offline and online evaluation. The mechanism remains fixed across tasks, while improvements to skill specifications in one task can be reused by later tasks with similar architectures or failure patterns. The semantic gate does not judge whether a semantically valid modification is effective for the business objective; such performance-related failures are handled by offline and online evaluation.
