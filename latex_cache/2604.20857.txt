Despite these advances, end-to-end paper generation still remains noticeably incomplete in one of the most visible places: the teaser figure, which is often a method overview diagram on the first page. Unlike standard plots, which follow a routine generation pipeline from data, scientific diagrams require cross-modal abstraction and strategic layout planning~. Previous work also shows that authors often identify a ``central figure'' that serves as a de-facto graphical abstract~. To illustrate, a teaser figure should accomplish three goals: convey the paper's core contribution at a glance, pique the reader's curiosity, and faithfully represent its key findings without overstatement. In contemporary data mining and machine learning venues, the teaser figure is often the most efficient carrier of a paper's core workflow, highlighting the objects, modules, and information flow that a reader needs to build a mental model of the method. When automated pipelines omit this figure or replace it with inferior alternatives, the resulting ``paper'' cannot attract readers or persuade peers, let alone become a well-circulated scientific artifact~. Overall, a good scientific diagram compresses a long-form technical description into a structured visual program: selecting salient entities and operations, assigning them to spatial regions, routing relations without clutter, and rendering legible labels and legends. This structure-rich, symbolic nature distinguishes diagrams from natural images and is a key reason that diagram understanding has been studied as a separate problem from standard vision tasks~. Unfortunately, standard text-to-image models fail to generate scientific schematic diagrams that are dense compositions of many semantically linked objects, consistent arrow topology, and readable text~. Recent research has begun to address these issues via explicit planning or intermediate representations ~. However, even with better planners, models still need to be exposed to high-quality design exemplars to learn the visual conventions of scientific communication . This motivates a complementary direction: building a curated exemplar bank that can be retrieved and used during authoring. There is a large body of work on mining and understanding scientific figures, but existing datasets are not designed for diagram design and teaser authoring. Beyond figure mining and classification, recent work has also studied in scientific papers, aiming to select a representative figure given the paper content. For example, propose a self-supervised approach that leverages figure references in the body text to train a model for ranking figures as visual summaries, highlighting both the demand for such representative figures and the challenge of obtaining domain-specific labels at scale. Figure-caption corpora such as SciCap~ support caption generation and figure understanding at scale, yet they are dominated by heterogeneous figure types and are primarily optimized for language supervision rather than exemplar-driven visual authoring. On the classification side, DocFigure~ provides labeled scientific figures to enable figure-type recognition, but it treat diagrams as one of many categories and do not preserve the paper context that explains why the figure is structured in a particular way. ACL-Fig~ provides scientific figures and tables from ACL Anthology, but missed out on other venues like ICLR, ICML, NeurIPS, and TMLR. Meanwhile, diagram-centric datasets such as AI2D~ provide dense, element-level annotations for educational diagrams, but their domain and distribution differ substantially from the schematic conventions of modern AI/ML papers. Relatedly, recent work has begun to frame as a joint task of generating a textual summary while selecting a representative image for each paper~. Finally, retrieval-augmented generation has become a standard paradigm for grounding model outputs with external evidence~, but applying RAG to diagrams requires a high-quality, license-aware corpus, and rich textual metadata so that retrieval can be conditioned at different granularities . To our knowledge, there is no public, large-scale dataset that simultaneously offers high-quality schematic diagrams, paper metadata for domain-aware retrieval, and figure-local context spans that connect a diagram to the surrounding scientific narrative. We present DiagramBank, a large-scale dataset of 89,422 schematic diagrams mined from top-tier AI/ML venues with accompanying paper metadata, built explicitly to support multimodal retrieval and exemplar-driven teaser-style figure generation. Each dataset instance is a self-contained record that couples a diagram image with multiple layers of text context: paper title and abstract , figure caption , and figure-reference spans extracted from the body . This design enables retrieval under different query granularities, which is critical in practice: users may start from a paper-level description and progressively refine toward a figure-level target . DiagramBank is intended as an infrastructure for both the data mining community and the emerging line of scientific authoring systems. Each diagram is linked to paper-level and figure-level signals, enabling hierarchical retrieval that reduces domain drift and improves specificity. To process the visual content, we employ PDFFigures 2.0~ to parse the downloaded PDFs, extracting all figures and their corresponding captions. While PDFFigures 2.0 identifies both tables and figures, we discard table artifacts to focus strictly on visual illustrations. Beyond the image and caption, capturing the semantic context in which a figure appears is crucial for understanding its scientific intent. We utilize PyMuPDF~ to parse the text blocks of every PDF, extracting all paragraphs that explicitly cite a specific figure number. This text forms the attribute, linking the visual representation to the author's argumentative flow. A core challenge in constructing DiagramBank is distinguishing explanatory diagrams from data visualizations and natural images. We address this by implementing a filtering pipeline using Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training ~. Specifically, we utilize the ViT-B-32 model from OpenCLIP~ to classify extracted figures into discrete categories: , , , or . Only figures classified as diagrams with a confidence score exceeding $0.85$ are retained. These validated entries are stored in a relation, which contains the figure path, caption, extracted context, classification type, and confidence score. We manage the relational data using DuckDB~. To construct the primary dataset, we first execute an inner join between the and relations on the unique . The resulting schema is deliberately denormalized, repeating paper-level metadata for every figure record. This redundancy ensures that every retrieved exemplar in DiagramBank is a self-contained unit, providing all necessary semantic and structural context for retrieval-augmented generation without requiring complex runtime joins. Following aggregation, we apply a strict two-stage filtering pipeline to ensure high data quality. First, we filter based on peer-review outcomes, retaining only figures belonging to papers with positive decisions . Second, to isolate explanatory diagrams from statistical plots or natural images, we utilize a zero-shot CLIP-based classification pipeline. We define the classification as the softmax probability derived from the cosine similarity between the image embedding and the text embeddings of our four target classes . Through manual inspection of the classification results, we established a confidence threshold of $0.85$. We retain only figures classified as ``diagram'' that exceed this threshold, ensuring the dataset contains high-fidelity schematic representations while minimizing false positives. Because it is derived from real publications and can be used in generative pipelines, we summarize key ethical, privacy, fairness, and misuse considerations. DiagramBank is mined from programmatically accessible OpenReview PDFs for four AI/ML venues from 2017--2025, and we preserve source identifiers/URLs for attribution and traceability. Figures originate from third-party papers and may be subject to copyright or license terms; the dataset is intended for research and educational use, and users should cite and comply with the original works' terms. We will honor reasonable takedown requests from rights holders. We do not intentionally collect sensitive personal data. Metadata is publicly available and included only for bibliographic attribution and retrieval context. Since some figures may contain identifying elements, we provide figure-level metadata for auditing and recommend filtering/removing suspicious items; we discourage privacy-invasive use. DiagramBank reflects the AI/ML publication ecosystem and may exhibit domain, temporal, and availability biases . If an accepted-paper subset is used, it may further amplify venue-specific norms; we therefore encourage reporting results on both all-paper and accepted-only subsets when relevant. We do not claim demographic representativeness. The dataset can enable generation of paper-style diagrams, which may be misused to fabricate or mislead. We recommend clear disclosure for generated figures where appropriate, provenance tracking to retrieved exemplars, and human verification prior to publication. Recent discussions in scientific publishing also caution that AI-generated illustrations can appear convincing while containing subtle inaccuracies, underscoring the need for transparency and careful human review~.
