At a scale exceeding 10M items, NEO consistently outperforms strong production baselines for recommendation and text-based retrieval, while remaining language-steerable and supporting mixed ID–text outputs for explanations and user understanding. Beyond empirical gains, our design analysis highlights two key principles for grounding LLMs in large discrete domains: the importance of high quality semantically structured identifiers over atomic IDs, and the role of a staged alignment recipe in balancing item grounding with retention of general language competence, key for tasks involving mixed text-SIDs in output. Our results show that naive alternatives—such as random tokenization, collapsing alignment stages, or continuous pre-training—either degrade performance or compromise language capabilities, especially for tasks that require instruction following and mixed text–item generation. More broadly, we view NEO as an instance of a general approach to building fully self-contained, domain-adapted LLMs that reason jointly over entities, user behavior, and language without relying on external tools or task-specific pipelines. We believe the framework and empirical insights presented here provide practical guidance for extending LLMs to other large-scale discovery settings involving heterogeneous entities and steerable generative behavior. As user interfaces become more conversational, discovery interactions increasingly interleave explicit language-based queries with implicit behavioral feedback. Despite this convergence at the interaction level, recommendation and search are still typically implemented as separate system stacks~, leading to duplicated modeling, fragmented objectives, and substantial operational overhead. While recent work explores unifying these capabilities through generative modeling~, achieving practical consolidation at scale, especially over heterogeneous catalogs with multiple entity types, remains challenging. Large Language Models offer a natural abstraction for unifying discovery, providing a single sequence-modeling paradigm and a natural-language interface~ However, deploying a single LLM for large-scale discovery is difficult when model outputs must unambiguously reference real catalog items. Purely textual item mentions~ are ambiguous and unstable, embedding-based item sequences~ require architectural changes and struggle with long histories, and tool-augmented pipelines~ introduce orchestration complexity that limits end-to-end optimization and latency that adds practical challenges ~. In contrast, traditional generative recommenders~ have been demonstrated to scale on large datasets~, but they operate on sequences of atomic IDs and cannot interleave language with explicit item references or generate item-grounded explanations, particularly in heterogeneous catalogs. Motivated by these limitations, we focus on a constrained but practically relevant setting: a massive, heterogeneous catalog in which a single deployed model must simultaneously support recommendation, text-based retrieval, and item-grounded text generation, while remaining , and operating end-to-end and tool-free at inference time. We view this setting as an instance of a broader research problem: how to adapt LLMs to reason jointly over domain entities, user behavior, and language in a fully self-contained manner. We introduce { NEO}, a framework that adapts an open-weight decoder-only LLM into a single end-to-end, catalog-grounded generator. NEO represents items using semantic identifiers ~ and trains a single model to consume and produce sequences that interleave natural language with typed item identifiers . Natural-language prompts control the task, target entity type, and output format , while constrained decoding guarantees that generated identifiers correspond to valid catalog items without restricting free-form text. Inspired by multimodal alignment, we treat SIDs as a distinct modality and integrate them via a staged training recipe comprising semantic foundation, domain grounding, and capability induction, while preserving the standard next-token prediction objective. Within this framework, we examine a set of research questions relevant to general design choices in domain-adapted LLMs. We study the role of semantically structured identifiers versus atomic IDs, the impact of different alignment strategies on generalization and language retention, and the ability of a single model to support multiple discovery tasks under explicit language control. To ground these questions in practice, we evaluate NEO at scale on a catalog exceeding 10M items from a major streaming platform, spanning multiple item types and tasks including recommendation, search, and user understanding. Across offline experiments, NEO consistently outperforms strong task-specific baselines, exhibits positive cross-task transfer, and demonstrates a practical path toward consolidating large-scale discovery into a single language-steerable and catalog-grounded generative model. {figures/stages2.pdf} Figure~ presents a general staged pipeline for training a domain-adapted, fully self-contained LLM, of which NEO instantiates the first three stages. Stage~1 learns SIDs as discrete token sequences representing catalog entities and their semantic structure. Stage~2 grounds these identifiers by aligning SID-text pairs to learn a shared space where text and SIDs are interchangeable, supporting mixed text–SID sequences and sequential domain signals. Stage~3 then induces discovery capabilities via multi-task instruction-following, prioritizing breadth over single-task specialization, whereas an optional Stage~4 captures subsequent task-specific objective optimization using larger datasets and specialized objectives . We compare with alternative training strategies, including continuous pre-training -based approaches, in Section~. NEO adapts a pre-trained open-weight decoder-only LLM to operate jointly over natural language and discrete item identifiers, while preserving the standard next-token prediction objective and requiring no architectural modifications . Text inputs are tokenized using the native tokenizer of the backbone LLM, while catalog items are represented as short sequences of SID tokens obtained by discretizing item embeddings. Both text tokens and SID tokens are embedded into a single shared sequence and processed by the same decoder stack, enabling the model to reason uniformly over language and items. To reliably integrate this extended vocabulary and align item identifiers with linguistic representations, NEO is trained using a three-stage recipe, as shown in . First, item content is converted into SIDs through embedding discretization . Second, the newly introduced SID tokens are aligned with the LLM's embedding space . Third, the model is instruction-tuned on a mixture of discovery tasks, including recommendation, retrieval, recommendation explanations, and user interest analysis . In Stage 1, we focus on constructing discrete item representations that can be used directly by an LLM. We represent each catalog item $e$ with $ $: a short sequence of $M$ discrete tokens, each with $K$ possible values, that compactly encodes the semantic content of the item. Intuitively, SIDs provide a discrete token-level abstraction of items that preserves semantic neighborhood structure while remaining compatible with autoregressive generation. See Appendix for formal details. We construct SIDs by discretizing content-based item embeddings in a hierarchical fashion using residual quantizers , inducing a coarse-to-fine decomposition: earlier codes capture broad semantic regions, and later codes refine within-region distinctions. This property aligns naturally with autoregressive generation, where tokens are produced sequentially. We design the discretization to ensure semantic coherence, stability under distribution shift , and compatibility with large heterogeneous catalogs. For heterogeneous entities, embeddings of different item types may lie in different latent spaces, thus requiring multiple quantizers. To integrate SIDs and natural language, we define a unified vocabulary $=_{} _{}$. We extend the LLM's base vocabulary by $M K$ SID tokens, which are randomly initialized, and add the corresponding parameters to the LLM head when their weights are untied. SIDs are interleaved with text in both inputs and outputs. Following delimiter-based conventions commonly used in Multimodal LLMs item references are emitted only within explicit item spans: $ \ \ c_1\ \ c_M \ \ $. This design allows the model to freely interleave natural language and item references while enabling constrained decoding within SID spans. Stage~2 aligns SIDs with the LLM's linguistic embedding space. This stage is inspired by MLLMs, where non-textual modalities are represented either as discrete tokens~ or as continuous features interfaced through encoders and projection layers~. MLLMs are typically trained using staged recipes that introduce a new modality, align it with language, and then apply instruction tuning. Following this paradigm, we treat SIDs as a discrete ``foreign-language'' modality and learn a bidirectional mapping between SID sequences and natural language. This enables the model to both and mixed text--SID sequences, while preserving the standard next-token prediction objective. Let $s_e= $ denote the SID sequence for item $e$, let $t_e$ denote a short textual descriptor of $e$ derived from available metadata, and let $c_e$ denote the item type of $e$. We train the model using three complementary objectives: Given one or more SID spans , the model predicts a natural-language descriptor: $ t_e $ Given a natural-language query $q$, the model predicts the corresponding item SID span: $ \ s_e \ $. To reduce ambiguity and noise in the supervision, queries are constructed from a subset of item fields . We hypothesize that the more specific the prompts are, the better the alignment quality is, due to reduced semantic ambiguity. Given one or more SID spans , the model predicts the item type: $ c_e $. Together, these objectives encourage the model to align SIDs with language at the levels of semantics, retrieval, and type structure. To prevent catastrophic forgetting during the alignment phase, we freeze the pre-trained backbone parameters and optimize only: the input embeddings corresponding to newly introduced SID tokens, and the output weights used to predict SID tokens . This design preserves the linguistic competence of the backbone while enabling effective alignment between SIDs and natural language. Stage~3 trains the domain-grounded model to perform personalization tasks and discovery under explicit control via natural-language instructions, while producing SIDs, text, or mixed SIDs--text outputs. We unfreeze and fine-tune all model parameters. Alternatively, Stage~3 can be implemented using parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods when full fine-tuning is impractical. In this paper, we focus on simply introducing a large variety of types of tasks to the model including text-based retrieval, recommendation, and text generation across heterogeneous items. We save for future work demonstrating the ability to generalize in this stage to new, zero-shot capabilities. We train NEO using supervised instruction tuning over the unified vocabulary $$, where each training example is a prefix–completion pair $ $. The prefix $x$ is the input context and includes the system prompt and user instruction, optionally augmented with lightweight user features and/or item references expressed as SID spans. The completion $y$ is the model output and may contain natural language, SID spans, or both. During training, we apply the loss only to tokens in $y$, masking all tokens in~$x$. We restrict $x$ to single-turn interactions to simplify evaluation; the same formulation extends directly to multi-turn dialogues by concatenating the full conversation history $ pairs) and masking all user messages. All tasks are described through prompts that follow a consistent schema that explicitly specifies the task , the target item type, and the desired output format . This explicit conditioning enables fine-grained control over model behavior using natural-language instructions. The tasks are formulated as next-token prediction over mixed text--SID sequences that may involve items from multiple types: Given an instruction, lightweight user or context features, and a short interaction history represented as SIDs, the model predicts the next consumed item of the specified type as a SID. Given a natural-language query , the model generates the SID of the most relevant item . The model generates a recommended item SID together with a natural-language justification grounded in the user's history and the recommended item . Given a user's interaction history expressed as SIDs, the model generates a short natural-language description of the user's interests. In settings where natural-language data is unavailable , we prompt a stronger LLM to distill synthetic supervision for NEO’s training. Because multi-token SIDs induce a large combinatorial output space , we constrain decoding so that generated SIDs always map to existing catalog items. We precompute all valid SID tuples and store them in a prefix trie. During autoregressive decoding, we apply trie-based masking only within ... spans, restricting each next token to trie-consistent continuations. Outside SID spans, we restrict generation to text tokens , which prevents accidental SID token emission in free-form text while preserving the ability to introduce item references when needed. The mapping from items to SID tuples is not necessarily one-to-one, as multiple items may share the same SID. We resolve such collisions by mapping each SID tuple to a single canonical item, selected based on multiple possible heuristics . For retrieval tasks, we generate multiple candidate items by applying beam search over SID tokens. We evaluate NEO at scale across recommendation, retrieval, and user understanding tasks, focusing on effectiveness, steerability, and robustness under realistic production constraints. We evaluate NEO using a sample of interaction logs from a large-scale media streaming platform, spanning a catalog of over 10M items and approximately 15M users. The catalog includes multiple item types, including episodes, shows, audiobooks, and artists. We use around 5M examples in the domain grounding stage, while in the capability induction stage we train on a total of 10M training examples and test on around 100K examples. We compare the NEO-trained LLM against strong and mature production systems optimized over multiple years using large-scale data and extensive feature engineering. For recommendation tasks , the baseline combines Graph Neural Networks with a two-tower architecture~ and models cross-item-type relationships through item co-consumption patterns, inspired by~. It incorporates weak signals to better handle cold-start users, and uses categorical features alongside LLM-encoded item metadata. For text-based retrieval, the baseline is a dense retrieval system similar to~, trained on historical search logs. Training data includes query-entity pairs, multi-step reformulation sessions , manually curated semantic queries, and synthetic queries generated as done in~. The query encoder processes a textual concatenation of the user’s geographic location and the search query, while the entity encoder represents item metadata using concatenated metadata such as name and description. We evaluate recommendation and text-based retrieval performance using standard ranking metrics: Hit Rate@K and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain@K , with $K \{10, 30\}$ . There are no baselines for grounded text-SID mixed generation. These tasks are completely novel in our setting. Each item is represented using its available content and metadata. For artists, we encode tracks from track spectrograms into audio embeddings and average them by artist to obtain artist-level vectors. For shows, episodes, and audiobooks, we embed title and description text using the Qwen3 embeddings pre-trained text model~. Metadata quality can vary, particularly for podcast episodes, where descriptions may be missing or largely identical across episodes of the same show. To mitigate this, we generate richer episode summaries using a pre-trained LLM applied to multiple available metadata fields, and embed these summaries using the same text encoder. We construct SIDs using Residual $K$-means~ with $M{=}4$ codebooks. We use $K{=}256$ centroids per codebook for audiobooks, episodes and shows, and $K{=}1024$ for artists to better model the higher complexity of audio embeddings. We construct separate SID vocabularies per item type, as early experiments showed that embedding spaces differ substantially across types. All SID tokens, along with span delimiters and are added to the LLM vocabulary, extending the embedding layer and LLM head by $7168 + 2$. To mitigate the curse of dimensionality of $K$-means, we reduce embedding dimensionality to $1024$ dimensions using the Matryoshka property~ of Qwen3 embeddings. We solve collisions with a popularity-based selection for items colliding in the same SID bucket. challenges this solution without seeing significant differences from random selection. In our heterogeneous setting, using a single quantizer for all item embedding spaces would severely compromise SIDs quality. We construct paired alignment examples linking each item SID $s_e$ with a short textual descriptor $t_e$ derived from metadata. For audiobooks, episodes, and shows, descriptors include titles, descriptions, summaries, topics, and categories. For artists, descriptors include names, genres, and Wikipedia snippets when available. Text-to-SID queries $q$ are constructed from a subset of these fields to reduce ambiguity. We review related work through the lens of the broader problem introduced in the previous section: adapting LLMs to operate over domain entities and user behavior in a fully self-contained manner, while remaining language-steerable and scalable to large, heterogeneous catalogs. Prior work addresses aspects of this challenge across representation learning, generative recommendation, and LLM-based personalization, but typically resolves key requirements--such as item grounding, task control, language, or scalability--by externalizing complexity to specialized architectures, auxiliary tools, or task-specific pipelines. Many approaches rely on atomic item IDs, which limits generalization in cold-start and long-tail regimes~. These models are typically designed for single-task next-item prediction and operate purely over ID sequences, making it difficult to incorporate natural language and support multiple discovery tasks within a single model. To address limitations of atomic IDs, recent work has explored both continuous item embeddings~ and SIDs~. Although continuous embeddings are effective in many multimodal settings , injecting long sequences of dense vectors into an LLM can be bandwidth- and memory-intensive for users with long interaction histories, and often requires architectural modifications or additional training objectives beyond standard next-token prediction. In contrast, SIDs can be treated as ordinary tokens by both traditional and generative sequence models, while preserving the semantic neighborhood structure that is richer than atomic item IDs~. One influential line of work that operationalizes SIDs for recommendation begins with TIGER~. TIGER introduces a pipeline that learns item embeddings, discretizes them into SIDs, and applies autoregressive modeling for recommendation. Subsequent work improves identifier quality using retrieval-oriented or contrastive objectives~, as well as by incorporating collaborative signals during code learning~. However, most SID-based recommenders are not language-steerable and do not support mixed text-ID inputs or outputs, limiting their applicability to tasks such as search and natural-language explanation. In addition, their interfaces offer limited flexibility for rich textual inputs and do not naturally support operating over multiple item types within a single model. From the perspective of domain-adapted LLMs, these approaches provide strong item-level modeling but lack a unified interface for language steerability, multi-task discovery, and heterogeneous entity types. Recent work applies LLMs to personalization by introducing natural-language interfaces for recommendation and discovery, framing user intent and context directly in text~. These approaches rely on text-only representations~, textual and rule-based IDs~, or atomic IDs~, and some explore unifying search and recommendation within a single model~. However, such methods are often evaluated on relatively small single-typed datasets, struggle with cold-start items when SIDs are not used, or face scalability limitations when applied to large catalogs, particularly when relying on free-form textual references to items. To address item ambiguity and expand capability, several systems augment LLMs with external tools or retrievers for personalized recommendation~. For example, TalkPlay-tools~ orchestrates multiple tools via planning to answer user queries, while RecMind~ relies on external knowledge and tool use for zero-shot personalization. While effective in certain settings, these approaches are not trained end-to-end, operate primarily in the text modality, leading to ambiguity when referencing catalog items, and introduce additional orchestration complexity and latency at inference time. We summarize these trade-offs in . SIDs offer a discrete representation of entities that preserves semantic structure while remaining compatible with autoregressive generation~. TalkPlay~ reformulates music recommendation as token generation by expanding the LLM vocabulary with feature-engineered multimodal music tokens, enabling single turn interactions that interleave text and SIDs in both input and output. However, this approach focuses on a single item type and task, relies on an external retriever operating over SIDs, is not evaluated at large scale, and does not incorporate user history for personalization. Concurrent work such as PLUM~ explores a related direction based on continual pre-training and supervised fine-tuning with SIDs, and shows partial language-steerability through qualitative in-context learning examples. However, PLUM currently does not support mixed text–ID outputs in downstream tasks, focuses exclusively on recommendations, and is limited to a single item type. Prior SID-based systems primarily target single-task recommendation or retrieval, and do not study how discrete entity representations can be integrated into a language-steerable, multi-task LLM that supports mixed text+item input { and} output while maintaining general language competence. In contrast to prior work that applies SIDs within LLMs primarily for recommendation, we introduce a typed mixed-sequence catalog interface and a staged alignment recipe. This enables an open-weight decoder-only LLM to interleave natural language with valid identifiers from a heterogeneous catalog and support recommendation, text retrieval, and item-grounded text generation and user understanding in a single, tool-free model. Thus, each SID is an ordered tuple of discrete codes drawn from $M$ codebooks. NEO is agnostic to a specific quantizer method used to construct SIDs. However, in this work we focus on residual quantizers, which often induces a coarse-to-fine decomposition: earlier codes capture broad semantic regions, and later codes refine within-region distinctions. This property aligns naturally with autoregressive generation, where tokens are produced sequentially. Specifically, we adopt Residual $K$-means~ for its flexibility and empirical stability, particularly compared to alternatives such as RQ-VAE, which often require multiple auxiliary losses to avoid poor local minima~. Residual $k$-means represents each embedding as a sequence of codebook indices by iteratively quantizing the residual error. Concretely, we maintain $M$ codebooks $\{C_1, ,C_M\}$, each containing $K$ centroids $\{ _{m,1}, , _{m,K}\}$. Starting from the initial residual $r_0 = x_e$, we select the closest centroid at each stage c_m &= _{j \{1, ,K\}} \|r_{m-1} - _{m,j}\|_2^2, and update the residual as: r_m &= r_{m-1} - _{m,c_m}, m=1, ,M. The SID length and codebook size $K$ trade off addressability, semantic coherence, and sequence length. Longer SIDs increase addressability but inflate prompt length, while shorter SIDs risk semantic collisions. We guide this choice using two criteria: collision rate, the fraction of SID tuples assigned to multiple items; and prefix coherence, the average cosine similarity between items sharing the same SID prefix. We select $M$ and $K$ to minimize collisions while maintaining high semantic coherence for short prefixes, enabling reliable generation without excessive sequence overhead. The quality of SIDs depends critically on the underlying item embedding $x_e$. Such embeddings may be derived from collaborative signals~, content metadata~, or hybrid representations~, provided they induce a meaningful semantic geometry over items. In practice, item representations must remain stable under distribution shift. In domains with strong seasonality and rapidly evolving trends , embeddings derived from collaborative signals can drift quickly, causing an item to be reassigned different identifiers over short time spans. This drift may require frequent retraining of both LLM and the quantizer, or the introduction of additional stabilization mechanisms. Moreover, collaborative signals are often sparse or unavailable for cold-start items. For these reasons, NEO constructs SIDs using content-based item representations, and defers delegates the incorporation of collaborative signals to later instruction-tuning stages, where the LLM can learn to leverage user interaction history directly. We have analyzed the stability of our CF-based embeddings by sampling 1{,}000{,}000 random artists, computed each artist's top-50 nearest neighbors under the CF embedding space, and measured neighborhood overlap across time using Jaccard similarity. Over a 3-week horizon, the median overlap is 0.59 , corresponding to roughly 37 out of 50 neighbors retained; the least stable decile has overlap 0.37, corresponding to roughly 27 out of 50 neighbors retained. CF-based SIDs may exhibit temporal instability due to evolving user behavior. To connect NEO-trained generative models with embedding-based retrieval, we explored whether the learned SID token embeddings can be used to construct an item embedding space for approximate $k$-NN. After training Qwen3-0.6B with NEO, we represent each item by the concatenation of the $M$ learned input embeddings corresponding to its SID tokens. We then build a simple episode-retrieval recommender where each user is represented by the average embedding of episodes streamed in the previous 30 days, and recommendations are obtained via approximate $k$-NN in the resulting embedding space. In our experiments, this approach significantly underperforms the production retrieval baseline and the generative NEO model. A likely explanation is that concatenating token embeddings yields very high-dimensional item vectors , which can make nearest-neighbor retrieval less reliable without additional metric learning or dimensionality reduction. We also evaluated alternative text-based embedding models for item representations, but did not observe improvements.
