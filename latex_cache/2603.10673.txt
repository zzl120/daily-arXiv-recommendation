Nunc pulvinar massa et mattis lacinia. {figs/1.pdf} Large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of domains , fundamentally reshaping how intelligent decision-making systems reason and interact with users . In recommender systems, these advances have stimulated growing interest in LLM-based agent paradigms, where agents model user behaviors through language-driven interaction and reasoning , enabling more expressive and interpretable preference representations compared to traditional collaborative filtering approaches . However, despite these promising developments, most existing agent-based recommender methods largely mirror human-centered decision-making patterns and adopt a predominantly user-centric modeling paradigm . They primarily optimize user utility while treating items as passive functional entities , thereby failing to adequately account for the interests of other critical stakeholders, such as content providers and platform operators . Such a user-centric decision paradigm is inherently unsustainable in multi-sided marketplaces. In particular, the pervasive Matthew effect concentrates exposure on already popular items , leaving long-tail creators with limited visibility . This imbalance weakens creator incentives, leading to creator churn and a subsequent decline in high-quality content production . Moreover, excessive optimization for short-term user engagement exacerbates a winner-take-all dynamic, gradually homogenizing the content pool. Ultimately, this erosion of supply-side diversity undermines user experience and harms the platform's long-term ecosystem health. Although some recent methods introduce item agents or content agents , these agents typically function only as information carriers or attribute descriptors. Their role remains largely auxiliary, serving to better satisfy user preferences rather than actively pursuing reasonable exposure or long-term benefits on behalf of the items themselves. To address this limitation at its root, we propose a }-party LLM-agent }ommendation framework that explicitly aligns the interests of users, items, and the platform, as illustrated in Figure~. The framework adopts a disentangled two-stage architecture that preserves user experience while empowering items with agency to actively compete for exposure through personalized self-promotion. At the platform level, global ranking is further regulated by incorporating system-level constraints, ensuring long-term fairness and ecosystem stability. Stage 1: Generative item self-promotion. We empower item agents with the capability to actively compete for visibility. Items are no longer passive candidates; instead, they become active participants by generating personalized promotion tailored to the target user's interest preference. For example, the same CD player might emphasize ``high audio fidelity'' to musicians, ``popular tracks'' to students, and ``easy audio playback'' to seniors. This mechanism not only improves matching quality but also provides long-tail items with opportunities to gain exposure. Stage 2: Platform-led multi-objective re-ranking. After Stage 1 generates a high-quality candidate list, the platform agent performs sequential re-ranking to balance multiple objectives. Specifically, for each candidate item, the agent considers immediate user relevance, platform-level fairness and expected item utility. The platform agent makes ranking decisions one position at a time, this sequential strategy ensures that ranking choices consider both current item exposure and long-term fairness. Notably, incorporating item self-promotion led to an increase in both the average exposure and click-through probability of items, while simultaneously improving platform-level fairness. At the same time, the introduction of personalized persuasive content enhanced recommendation accuracy on the user side. These results demonstrate that our framework can simultaneously support the objectives of users, items, and the platform. The remarkable reasoning capabilities of LLMs have fundamentally reshaped intelligent decision-making, stimulating a surge of interest in LLM-based agent paradigms within recommender systems . Unlike traditional filtering, these agents model complex behaviors through language-driven reasoning, enabling more expressive preference representations . Within this research frontier, a prominent direction utilizes agents as high-fidelity surrogates to simulate human behavior . Frameworks like Agent4Rec and DyTA4Rec incorporate sociological traits and dynamic profile updates to capture user interest evolution. To ensure long-term sustainability, the PUMA framework enables efficient migration of personalized prompt assets across evolving model architectures. Beyond simulation, agents function as intelligent interfaces to improve decision quality through deliberate reasoning . Methods such as STARec and ReasonRec incorporate reasoning mechanisms to mitigate hallucinations and enhance robustness, while LatentR3 further optimizes efficiency by utilizing reinforced latent tokens instead of explicit text generation. Meanwhile, decentralized multi-agent architectures decompose complex tasks into specialized roles or model bidirectional dynamics . Frameworks like AgentCF and Rec4Agentverse introduce item agents to interact autonomously with user agents for preference refinement. To address the long-term impact of these interactions, the bi-learning planner coordinates macro-level guidance for sustained engagement. However, existing multi-agent paradigms primarily focus on maximizing one-sided user utility while neglecting the interests of the items. In this work, we propose a novel framework that explicitly models item agents and equips them with the ability to advocate for their own visibility, while maintaining alignment with user preferences. User-centric recommendation often causes most exposure to go to a few popular items, leaving many creators and their content invisible. This lack of visibility hinder the creator experience, ultimately leading to a decline in content production and platform engagement . Early methodologies primarily utilized co-learning frameworks to synchronize user and provider representations , or developed specific mechanisms like PRINCE to provide counterfactual interpretability for the provider side. Recently, researchers have explored "mirroring" user-centric techniques, with models such as DualRec treating items as active queries to identify suitable users and tackle the user availability challenge in dual-target optimization. Concurrently, generative approaches such as HLLM-Creator have begun leveraging LLMs to produce personalized creative content, thereby enhancing item appeal through automated content augmentation. FlyThinker further advances this by enabling reasoning and generation to proceed concurrently, allowing for the dynamic guidance of long-form personalized responses. However, these frameworks remain viewing creators as passive targets for algorithmic optimization or static subjects of content generation and lack the explicit modeling of autonomous agency. Different from these passive approaches, our work empowers creators with agentic capabilities, enabling tailored promotion of their content to users. The pervasive Matthew effect in recommendation leads to exposure concentration, leaving vast long-tail products invisible and causing cold-start problems . Consequently, platforms must implement systematic interventions to reconcile the trade-off between accuracy and multi-stakeholder fairness . Classical research addresses this by formulating exposure allocation as a constrained optimization problem or by learning debiased representations to neutralize provider-side biases. Beyond static debiasing, SPRec introduces self-play mechanisms to iteratively mitigate filter bubbles and improve fairness without additional data. Another dominant paradigm utilizes post-hoc re-ranking to achieve equitable resource distribution via social welfare criteria. To account for temporal dynamics, models like LTP-MMF introduce mechanisms to mitigate the deleterious feedback loop effect. Similarly, the bi-learning planner utilizes LLMs to manage macro-level guidance for optimizing long-term user engagement. Recently, multi-agent social choice frameworks, such as SCRUF-D , introduce agents to arbitrate conflicting fairness criteria. While these methods move toward decentralized decision-making, they struggle to perceive fine-grained item-level signals. In this work, we formulate platform regulation as an agentic re-ranking problem that enables adaptive coordination of relevance, fairness, and item survival objectives. In this work, we proposed TriRec, a tri-party LLM-agent recommendation framework that explicitly coordinates users, items, and the platform, addressing the limitations of user-centric agentic recommendation paradigms. TriRec adopts a two-stage design: item agents generate personalized self-promotion content to enhance semantic matching, while a platform agent performs sequential multi-objective re-ranking to jointly optimize relevance, item utility, and exposure fairness. Extensive experiments on four public benchmarks demonstrate that TriRec consistently improves ranking accuracy, exposure fairness, and item-side utility. Notably, item self-promotion simultaneously benefits both item exposure and user-side effectiveness, challenging the conventional trade-off between relevance and fairness. Our results highlight the importance of explicitly modeling multi-stakeholder objectives for sustainable recommender systems and show that platform-aware agent coordination provides a practical path beyond user-centric optimization. Future work will explore richer platform objectives, such as creator lifecycle modeling. In this section, we formally define the recommendation problem addressed by TriRec from a multi-stakeholder and agentic perspective. Different from conventional single-objective recommendation settings that mainly optimize user relevance, our framework explicitly considers the potentially conflicting objectives of users, items, and the platform. We first introduce stakeholder-specific utility functions to characterize their respective optimization goals. Based on these formulations, we then describe how the overall recommendation process is decomposed into a two-stage agentic pipeline, where preference-oriented interaction modeling and platform-level exposure regulation are handled in a decoupled yet coordinated manner. Based on the above utility definitions, the overall recommendation process in TriRec is realized through a two-stage agentic pipeline. Specifically, Stage~1 focuses on constructing relevance-oriented candidate rankings by modeling fine-grained user--item semantic alignment via agent-based interaction and personalized item self-promotion. This stage produces a high quality preference backbone without introducing exposure regulation. Stage~2 then operates on top of the Stage~1 outputs by treating recommendation as a sequential control problem. The platform agent observes the system state, including historical exposure and relevance rankings, and performs state-aware re-ranking actions to jointly optimize user utility, item exposure utility, and platform-level fairness over time. This decoupled design enables TriRec to separate semantic preference modeling from long-term exposure regulation, while maintaining a closed-loop decision process that dynamically adapts to evolving system states.
