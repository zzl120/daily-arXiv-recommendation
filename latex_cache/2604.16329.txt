 Current scientific paper recommendation systems produce a single similarity score that conflates multiple dimensions of relatedness, preventing users from specifying why papers should be similar. We present SciFACE , a reranking framework that decomposes paper similarity into two independent dimensions: Background and Method . SciFACE trains two separate cross-encoder models on 5,891 real paper pairs labeled by GPT-4o-mini with facet-specific annotations validated against human judgment. SciFACE makes deliberate design choices that frame its advantages and scope of use: Cross-encoders require $O $ pairwise inference, making them suited for reranking small candidate pools rather than large-scale retrieval. This is an intentional design decision: by operating as a precision amplifier at the final reranking stage, SciFACE avoids the approximation costs inherent to bi-encoder architectures. The computational constraint does not limit applicability, as modern academic search APIs already perform first-stage retrieval efficiently. SciFACE's pipeline consists of two steps---GPT-4o-mini annotation and SciBERT fine-tuning---with no multi-stage preprocessing, specialized segmentation models, or citation-pretrained base required. This simplicity lowers the barrier to reproduction and adaptation to new scientific domains. As shown in our error analysis , the model struggles when methodologically similar papers use entirely different terminology or when high-level algorithmic terms mask implementation differences . This is a known limitation of abstract-level encoding. We model Background and Method---the primary axes of controllable literature exploration. Other facets such as ``Results'' or ``Data'' are left for future work. Knowledge distillation from SciFACE's high-precision Cross-Encoder into a Bi-Encoder or Multi-Vector architecture would enable efficient large-scale retrieval while preserving facet-aware ranking quality. Incorporating explicit method taxonomies or LLM-extracted canonical method descriptors could bridge the cross-domain terminology barrier. A hierarchical encoding scheme that represents both algorithmic families and task-specific instantiations separately could improve recall for granularity mismatch cases. Extending the framework to include a Result facet would allow users to find papers with similar empirical findings, completing the Background--Method--Result triad established by CSFCube.
