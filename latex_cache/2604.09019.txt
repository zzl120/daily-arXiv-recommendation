We have shown that two-hop QA retrieval divides into Q-dominant and B-dominant regimes, determined by whether the hop-2 target entity is named in the question. Three theorems formalize this structure: AUC calibrates to cosine separation margin , P1 is decisive for routing and P2 qualifies the B-dominant case—both predicates derivable from surface text, holding across encoders and datasets , and bridge advantage requires the relation-bearing sentence specifically, not entity occurrence alone . operationalizes this theory as a five-feature binary router with a single frozen deployment rule ($ =0.25$ across all three datasets; $ =0.5$ is reported separately as an in-domain ablation upper bound). The result is a cross-dataset no-regret system: $+5.6$ pp in-domain on 2Wiki, $+5.3$ pp zero-shot on MuSiQue , and $+1.1$ pp positive non-significant transfer on HotpotQA . Training uses 881 embedding scores for regime labels and 100 human-annotated sentences for the selector; no LLM is invoked at query time. The principal finding with practical consequence is that the routing bottleneck is not feature engineering but routing accuracy . Improving the learned routing policy—perhaps via calibrated confidence or domain-adaptive features—is the primary path to closing the gap to oracle performance. *{Limitations} The theorems are formalized for two-hop compositional benchmarks where questions are constructed by composing single-hop questions, making P1 and P2 binary and well-defined. The regime theory may not directly extend to: questions with implicit rather than explicit compositional structure; multi-hop chains beyond two hops; encoders trained with bridge conditioning. The router is trained on 2WikiMultiHopQA and zero-shot applied to MuSiQue and HotpotQA—further evaluation on additional domains is needed.
