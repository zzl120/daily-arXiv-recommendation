Standard long-context approaches address this through attention window extension or memory-augmented retrieval, but both share a critical blind spot. These entity states and causal progressions are encoded not in token co-occurrence patterns but in the between entities across a document. Current approaches to integrating structural knowledge with retrieval face three critical limitations that make them unsuitable for this setting. First, most methods rely on static, pre-constructed knowledge graphs like ConceptNet or Freebase that cannot represent domain-specific or session-specific entities---the XYZ-2000 printer model, its specific error codes, or the sequence of actions taken during this particular support session~. Second, existing graph-neural retrieval approaches operate over these fixed general-purpose graphs, providing no mechanism to extract and reason over relationships that only emerge from the content at hand~. Third, integrating on-the-fly knowledge graph construction with neural retrieval for long-context inference remains largely unexplored~. We present , a framework that directly addresses these failures by constructing during inference and integrating graph-structural embeddings with textual semantics for improved long-context modeling. The key insight is that the same input text used for language modeling contains rich structural information---entities, their types, and the relationships between them---that can be extracted in real-time to build a domain-adaptive knowledge graph. Unlike static-graph approaches~, KGERMAR captures relationships specific to each document collection as it processes them. Unlike purely semantic systems including ERMAR~ and MemLong~, KGERMAR maintains three specialized memory components---contextual, semantic, and structural---enabling retrieval that distinguishes between passages mentioning related terms coincidentally versus passages describing explicit relationships between the same entities. This distinction is critical precisely in long-context settings, where the relevant passage may appear thousands of tokens earlier and share only structural, not lexical, similarity with the query. As illustrated in Figures~ and~, KGERMAR processes input text through an integrated pipeline: transformer-based NER and relation extraction identify entities and relationships, constructing a contextual knowledge graph; Relational Graph Convolutional Networks ~ encode graph structure into entity embeddings capturing both individual properties and structural roles; cross-modal attention fuses graph-structural embeddings with textual entity embeddings from the base language model; three memory banks store contextual key-value pairs , semantic text embeddings, and structural entity embeddings, with retrieval signals combined via learned fusion weights. Crucially, the structural memory bank enables the model to retrieve the passage describing the in response to a query about a ---not because the two passages share vocabulary, but because both involve the same device entity in a causally connected state progression. We position KGERMAR within three research areas: memory-augmented retrieval for long contexts, knowledge graph methods, and knowledge-enhanced language models. Dense retrieval methods~ learn semantic similarity in continuous embedding spaces. RAG~ pioneered integrating dense retrieval with generative models, enabling external memory access beyond model parameters. For long-context modeling, RETRO~ demonstrated retrieval from large corpora, MemLong~ introduced chunk-level memory with side networks, and ERMAR~ advanced this with ranking mechanisms over intermediate LM key-value pairs. Agentic systems like Mem0~ and A-Mem~ extend RAG for interactive agents but focus on cross-session persistence rather than within-document structural reasoning. Extended-attention models~ and long-context LLMs~ make more tokens accessible but do not improve retrieval ---they cannot prioritize structurally relevant passages over semantically coincident ones, nor do they offer the memory efficiency gains that retrieval-based architectures provide on commodity hardware. Critically, all memory-augmented systems rely exclusively on semantic similarity without modeling entity relationships~, limiting precision in knowledge-intensive scenarios where entity states and causal relationships determine relevance. Knowledge graph construction has evolved from rule-based systems like NELL~ and OpenIE~ to neural approaches~, while graph representation methods including TransE~, RotatE~, and R-GCN~ encode pre-constructed graphs into dense embeddings. Knowledge-enhanced language models---ERNIE~, KnowBERT~, K-BERT~, GreaseLM~, and GraphFormers~---integrate structured knowledge during pre-training. Graph-enhanced retrieval systems QA-GNN~ and DRAGON~ apply GNNs over ConceptNet for question answering, while earlier work used static knowledge bases for query expansion~. All these approaches share a critical constraint: they rely on static, general-purpose knowledge graphs with fixed vocabularies, making them ineffective when relevant entities and relationships must be derived from specialized content itself~. KGERMAR occupies a distinct position at the intersection of memory-augmented retrieval and dynamic knowledge graph construction. Unlike static graph approaches~, it constructs domain-specific knowledge graphs from input text during inference. Unlike memory-augmented systems~, it captures explicit entity relationships alongside semantic similarity. Unlike knowledge-enhanced models~, its structural knowledge adapts to each document at inference time. Building on ERMAR~, KGERMAR augments contextual memory with semantic memory and structural memory , producing a retrieval signal space strictly richer than any prior approach---applicable across technical support, scientific literature, and legal document analysis~. We fine-tuned KGERMAR on ~, a high-quality deduplicated corpus spanning web, book, and code domains, designed for long-context training. It contains 84.7K training examples, preprocessed with a sliding window of 512-token strides to ensure coverage of long sequences. Evaluation was conducted on three benchmark datasets representing distinct long-context domains: ~ , a structured encyclopedia corpus that benefits from entity-relationship modeling; ~ , a book-length narrative corpus requiring long-range dependency tracking; and ~ , a mathematical and scientific text corpus testing performance on formal, knowledge-dense content. These three datasets were selected to evaluate KGERMAR across structurally diverse long-context settings, following the evaluation protocol of MemLong~ and ERMAR~. Perplexity is computed on the final 2048 tokens of sequences ranging from 1024 to 32768 tokens~. We fine-tuned OpenLLaMA-3B~ using LoRA~ for parameter-efficient training. The model comprises $L=26$ transformer layers and $H=32$ attention heads with rotational position encoding~. Layer 13 serves as the memory layer storing historical key-value pairs, while layers $\{14, 18, 22, 26\}$ are augmented with retrieval causal attention. KGERMAR employs a memory capacity of 32,768 key-value pairs with BGE-M3 embeddings~ for semantic similarity computation. For knowledge graph construction, named entity recognition uses BERT-large fine-tuned on CoNLL-2003, and relation extraction uses BERT-base fine-tuned on TACRED~, consistent with the model specification in Section~. KGERMAR was evaluated against state-of-the-art models at two parameter scales. At 7B parameters: LLaMA-2-7B~ as a standard transformer baseline; LongLoRA-7B-32k~ using sparse attention for 32K-token contexts; and YARN-128k-7B~ with dynamic position embeddings supporting up to 128K tokens. At 3B parameters: OpenLLaMA-3B~ as the base architecture; LongLLaMA-3B~ in two retrieval configurations ; MemLong-3B~ as our primary memory-augmented baseline; ERMAR~ as our direct predecessor; and Phi3-128k~, a strong non-memory-augmented long-context model included to contextualize the tradeoffs between memory-augmented retrieval and extended-attention approaches.
